## Unit 3: Types of Reactions

### Introduction to Organic Reaction Mechanisms

Organic reaction mechanisms describe the step-by-step sequence of bond-breaking and bond-forming events that occur during a chemical transformation. Understanding mechanisms is essential for predicting reaction outcomes, designing synthetic routes for drug molecules, and understanding drug metabolism and degradation pathways.

**Key Concepts in Reaction Mechanisms**:

| Concept | Definition | Pharmaceutical Relevance |
|---------|------------|--------------------------|
| Electrophile | Electron-deficient species that accepts electron pairs | Many drugs act as electrophiles in alkylating DNA (anticancer agents) |
| Nucleophile | Electron-rich species that donates electron pairs | Functional groups in drugs (amines, OH) act as nucleophiles in metabolism |
| Leaving group | Atom or group that departs with bonding electrons | Important in prodrug activation and drug degradation |
| Carbocation | Positively charged carbon intermediate | Involved in many drug synthesis steps and metabolic rearrangements |
| Carbanion | Negatively charged carbon intermediate | Involved in carbon-carbon bond formation in drug synthesis |
| Free radical | Species with unpaired electron | Involved in lipid peroxidation, drug-induced oxidative stress |

### 1. Oxidation Reactions

#### Definition

Oxidation in organic chemistry involves:
- Increase in oxygen or decrease in hydrogen
- Loss of electrons
- Increase in oxidation state of carbon

#### Common Oxidation Reactions in Pharmaceuticals

**1. Oxidation of Alcohols**

| Alcohol Type | Oxidizing Agent | Product | Pharmaceutical Application |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|---------------------------|
| Primary alcohol | PCC (pyridinium chlorochromate) | Aldehyde | Synthesis of aldehyde intermediates |
| Primary alcohol | KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇/H⁺ | Carboxylic acid | Synthesis of carboxylic acid drugs |
| Secondary alcohol | Any oxidant (PCC, CrO₃, KMnO₄) | Ketone | Synthesis of ketone-containing drugs |
| Tertiary alcohol | Resists oxidation | No reaction | Used as protecting groups |

**Mechanism of Chromium-Based Oxidation**:
- Formation of chromate ester
- Elimination via E2-like mechanism
- Carbonyl product formed

**Pharmaceutical Example**: Oxidation of secondary alcohols in steroid synthesis to produce ketone-containing hormones.

**2. Oxidation of Aldehydes to Carboxylic Acids**

Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids by:
- Tollens' reagent [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ (silver mirror test)
- Fehling's solution (Cu²⁺ in tartrate)
- KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇
- Air oxidation (autoxidation)

**Mechanism**: Nucleophilic addition of water to aldehyde gives gem-diol, which is oxidized.

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**: Aldehyde-containing drugs may oxidize during storage, affecting stability. The body oxidizes aldehydes to carboxylic acids in metabolism (e.g., alcohol metabolism).

**3. Oxidation of Alkenes**

| Reaction | Reagent | Product | Pharmaceutical Application |
|----------|---------|---------|---------------------------|
| Epoxidation | Peracids (m-CPBA) | Epoxide | Synthesis of epoxide intermediates; epoxide drugs |
| Dihydroxylation | Cold dilute KMnO₄ or OsO₄ | 1,2-diol (syn) | Synthesis of diol-containing drugs |
| Cleavage | Hot KMnO₄ or O₃ | Carbonyl compounds | Structure determination, synthesis |

**Mechanism of Epoxidation**: Electrophilic addition of oxygen to double bond via "butterfly" mechanism.

**Pharmaceutical Example**: Epoxidation of alkenes in drug metabolism (CYP450 enzymes) can lead to reactive intermediates.

**4. Oxidation of Aromatic Compounds**

| Reaction | Reagent | Product | Application |
|----------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Side-chain oxidation | Hot KMnO₄ | Benzoic acid | Synthesis of aromatic acids |
| Phenol oxidation | Various | Quinones | Vitamin K, coenzyme Q |

**Pharmaceutical Example**: Oxidation of toluene side chain in synthesis of benzoic acid preservatives.

**5. Baeyer-Villiger Oxidation**

**Definition**: Oxidation of ketones to esters or lactones using peracids or peroxides.

**Mechanism**: Nucleophilic addition of peracid to carbonyl, followed by migration of one group to oxygen (migratory aptitude: tertiary alkyl > secondary > benzyl > primary > methyl).

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of lactone-containing drugs (e.g., statins, macrolide antibiotics). Also involved in drug metabolism (CYP450-catalyzed oxidations).

**6. Oxidation in Drug Metabolism**

The body's cytochrome P450 enzymes catalyze various oxidations:
- Hydroxylation of aliphatic and aromatic carbons
- Epoxidation of alkenes
- N-, O-, and S-dealkylation
- Oxidation of alcohols and aldehydes
- Oxidative deamination

**Pharmaceutical Significance**:
- Converts lipophilic drugs to more water-soluble metabolites for excretion
- Can produce active metabolites (prodrug activation)
- May generate reactive intermediates causing toxicity

### 2. Reduction Reactions

#### Definition

Reduction in organic chemistry involves:
- Decrease in oxygen or increase in hydrogen
- Gain of electrons
- Decrease in oxidation state of carbon

#### Common Reducing Agents

| Reducing Agent | Strength | Selectivity | Pharmaceutical Application |
|----------------|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| NaBH₄ (sodium borohydride) | Mild | Reduces aldehydes, ketones, acid chlorides; not acids, esters | Selective reduction in drug synthesis |
| LiAlH₄ (lithium aluminum hydride) | Strong | Reduces almost all carbonyls (acids, esters, amides, nitriles) | Multi-step drug synthesis |
| H₂ with catalyst (Pd, Pt, Ni) | Variable | Hydrogenation of alkenes, alkynes, nitro, etc. | Large-scale industrial synthesis |
| DIBAL-H | Selective | Aldehydes from esters, nitriles | Controlled reductions |
| Na/NH₃ (liquid) | Specific | Reduces alkynes to trans-alkenes; Birch reduction of aromatics | Specialized reductions |

#### Common Reduction Reactions

**1. Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds**

| Substrate | Reagent | Product | Pharmaceutical Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|------------------------|
| Aldehyde | NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄ | Primary alcohol | Synthesis of alcohol intermediates |
| Ketone | NaBH₄ or LiAlH₄ | Secondary alcohol | Synthesis of alcohol drugs (e.g., statins) |
| Carboxylic acid | LiAlH₄ only | Primary alcohol | Synthesis of alcohols from natural acids |
| Ester | LiAlH₄ | Two alcohols | Synthesis of alcohols |
| Amide | LiAlH₄ | Amine | Synthesis of amine drugs |

**Mechanism of Hydride Reduction**:
- Nucleophilic attack by H⁻ on electrophilic carbonyl carbon
- Formation of alkoxide intermediate
- Protonation gives alcohol

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of chiral alcohols for drugs like atorvastatin (Lipitor).

**2. Hydrogenation (Reduction of Multiple Bonds)**

| Substrate | Catalyst | Product | Notes |
|-----------|----------|---------|-------|
| Alkene | Pd/C, PtO₂, Raney Ni | Alkane | Syn addition of H₂ |
| Alkyne | Lindlar's catalyst (Pd/CaCO₃/quinoline) | cis-Alkene | Syn addition, stops at alkene |
| Alkyne | Na/NH₃ (liquid) | trans-Alkene | Dissolving metal reduction |
| Nitroarene | H₂/Pd or Fe/HCl | Arylamine | Synthesis of aniline drugs |
| Nitrile | H₂/Ni or LiAlH₄ | Primary amine | Synthesis of amine drugs |
| Imine | NaBH₃CN or H₂/catalyst | Amine | Reductive amination |

**Mechanism of Catalytic Hydrogenation**:
- Adsorption of H₂ on metal surface
- Adsorption of substrate
- Stepwise transfer of H atoms
- Desorption of product

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Large-scale hydrogenation in drug manufacturing (e.g., reduction of nitro groups in antibiotic synthesis).

**3. Reductive Amination**

**Definition**: One-pot conversion of aldehydes or ketones to amines using ammonia or amines and a reducing agent.

**Mechanism**:
1. Carbonyl + amine → imine (or iminium ion)
2. Imine reduced to amine by NaBH₃CN, NaBH₄, or H₂/catalyst

**Pharmaceutical Significance**:
- Most common method for synthesizing amine drugs
- Used in synthesis of antidepressants, antihistamines, etc.
- Mimics biological transamination reactions

**4. Clemmensen Reduction**

**Definition**: Reduction of aldehydes or ketones to methylene (CH₂) using zinc amalgam and HCl.

**Mechanism**: Involves carbenoid intermediates on metal surface.

**Application**: Conversion of ketones to alkyl chains in steroid synthesis.

**5. Wolff-Kishner Reduction**

**Definition**: Reduction of aldehydes or ketones to methylene using hydrazine and strong base (KOH) at high temperature.

**Mechanism**: Formation of hydrazone, then deprotonation, loss of N₂, and protonation.

**Application**: Reduction of ketones to alkanes where acidic conditions (Clemmensen) would damage sensitive groups.

**6. Birch Reduction**

**Definition**: Reduction of aromatic rings to 1,4-cyclohexadienes using Na or Li in liquid NH₃ with an alcohol.

**Mechanism**: Dissolving metal reduction via radical anion intermediates.

**Application**: Synthesis of partially reduced aromatic compounds for drug intermediates.

**7. Reduction in Drug Metabolism**

- **Azo reduction**: By gut microflora, activates certain prodrugs
- **Nitro reduction**: To amines (can be toxic or therapeutic)
- **Carbonyl reduction**: To alcohols (phase I metabolism)
- **Disulfide reduction**: Cleaves disulfide bonds in proteins

### 3. Acylation Reactions

#### Definition

Acylation is the introduction of an acyl group (R–CO–) into a molecule. It is a fundamental reaction in pharmaceutical synthesis for introducing amide, ester, and ketone functionalities.

#### Common Acylating Agents

| Acylating Agent | Reactivity | Byproduct | Pharmaceutical Application |
|-----------------|------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Acid chlorides (RCOCl) | Very high | HCl | Most reactive, widely used |
| Acid anhydrides [(RCO)₂O] | High | RCOOH | Acetylation (aspirin synthesis) |
| Esters (RCOOR') | Moderate | R'OH | Transesterification, aminolysis |
| Carboxylic acids (RCOOH) | Low (needs activation) | H₂O | Fischer esterification |
| Amides (RCONH₂) | Very low | NH₃ | Rarely used directly |

#### Types of Acylation Reactions

**1. Acylation of Alcohols (Esterification)**

R–OH + R'–CO–X → R'–COO–R + HX

**Mechanism (with acid chloride)** :
- Nucleophilic attack of alcohol on carbonyl
- Tetrahedral intermediate
- Elimination of leaving group

**Pharmaceutical Examples**:
- Aspirin synthesis: Acetylation of salicylic acid with acetic anhydride
- Prodrug formation: Esterification of carboxylic acid drugs to improve bioavailability

**2. Acylation of Amines (Amide Formation)**

R–NH₂ + R'–CO–X → R'–CONH–R + HX

**Mechanism**: Similar to esterification; amines are more nucleophilic than alcohols.

**Pharmaceutical Examples**:
- Paracetamol synthesis: Acetylation of p-aminophenol
- Peptide bond formation in protein synthesis
- Penicillin structure contains amide bonds

**3. Friedel-Crafts Acylation**

**Definition**: Introduction of acyl group onto aromatic rings using acid chloride and Lewis acid catalyst (AlCl₃).

Ar–H + R–COCl + AlCl₃ → Ar–CO–R + HCl + AlCl₃

**Mechanism**:
1. Formation of acylium ion (R–C⁺=O)
2. Electrophilic attack on aromatic ring
3. Loss of proton to restore aromaticity

**Pharmaceutical Examples**:
- Synthesis of aromatic ketones (e.g., propiophenone derivatives)
- Intermediate for many drug syntheses

**4. Acylation of Carbon Nucleophiles**

Enolates and other carbanions undergo acylation to give β-diketones, β-keto esters, etc. (Claisen condensation).

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of β-diketone antibiotics (tetracyclines).

#### Pharmaceutical Significance of Acylation

| Application | Purpose | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| Prodrug design | Mask polar groups to improve absorption | Ester prodrugs of ampicillin (pivampicillin) |
| Drug derivatization | Improve stability or modify properties | Acetylation of morphine to heroin (more lipophilic) |
| Protection of functional groups | Prevent unwanted reactions during synthesis | Acylation of amines during multi-step synthesis |
| Metabolism | Acetylation is a phase II metabolic reaction | Isoniazid acetylation in tuberculosis treatment |
| Drug synthesis | Form amide/ester bonds in APIs | Most peptide drugs, β-lactam antibiotics |

### 4. Esterification

#### Definition

Esterification is the formation of esters from carboxylic acids and alcohols. It is a specific type of acylation.

#### Fischer Esterification

**Reaction**: R–COOH + R'–OH ⇌ R–COO–R' + H₂O

**Conditions**: Acid catalyst (conc. H₂SO₄, HCl, or p-toluenesulfonic acid), heat, often with removal of water to drive equilibrium.

**Mechanism**:

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Protonation of carbonyl oxygen (activates carbonyl) |
| 2 | Nucleophilic attack by alcohol |
| 3 | Proton transfer |
| 4 | Loss of water (dehydration) |
| 5 | Deprotonation to give ester |

**Factors Affecting Fischer Esterification**:
- **Alcohol structure**: Reactivity: 1° > 2° > 3° (steric hindrance)
- **Acid structure**: Less hindered acids react faster
- **Equilibrium**: Can be shifted by using excess reagent or removing water

#### Other Esterification Methods

| Method | Reagents | Advantages |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Acid chloride + alcohol | RCOCl + R'OH + base | Fast, irreversible, high yield |
| Acid anhydride + alcohol | (RCO)₂O + R'OH | Good for acetic esters |
| DCC coupling | RCOOH + R'OH + DCC (dicyclohexylcarbodiimide) | Mild conditions, for sensitive compounds |
| Transesterification | RCOOR'' + R'OH (catalyzed) | Exchange of alkoxy groups |

#### Pharmaceutical Applications of Esterification

**1. Prodrug Design**

Esterification of carboxylic acid or alcohol drugs to improve:
- Oral absorption (increased lipophilicity)
- Masking of unpleasant taste
- Prolonged duration of action
- Site-specific delivery

**Examples**:
- **Enalapril**: Ethyl ester prodrug of enalaprilat (ACE inhibitor)
- **Bambuterol**: Prodrug of terbutaline (bronchodilator)
- **Oseltamivir (Tamiflu)**: Ethyl ester prodrug
- **Aspirin**: Acetyl ester of salicylic acid (improves GI tolerance)

**2. Drug Stability**

Esterification can protect labile groups from oxidation or other degradation.

**3. Formulation**

Esters are used as:
- Solvents (ethyl acetate, isopropyl myristate)
- Plasticizers in coatings
- Penetration enhancers in topical formulations

**4. Metabolism**

Ester hydrolysis (by esterases) is a common metabolic pathway, often activating prodrugs or inactivating drugs.

### 5. Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

Nucleophilic substitution reactions involve replacement of a leaving group (L) by a nucleophile (Nu). They are among the most important reactions in organic synthesis and drug metabolism.

#### Types: SN1 and SN2

| Feature | SN1 (Substitution Nucleophilic Unimolecular) | SN2 (Substitution Nucleophilic Bimolecular) |
|---------|----------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Kinetics | Rate = k [substrate] | Rate = k [substrate][Nu] |
| Molecularity | Unimolecular | Bimolecular |
| Steps | Two steps (slow then fast) | One step (concerted) |
| Intermediate | Carbocation | Transition state (no intermediate) |
| Stereochemistry | Racemization (if chiral) | Inversion (Walden inversion) |
| Substrate reactivity | 3° > 2° > 1° (carbocation stability) | 1° > 2° > 3° (steric hindrance) |
| Nucleophile strength | Less important | Important |
| Leaving group | Important | Important |
| Solvent | Polar protic favors | Polar aprotic favors |
| Rearrangement | Possible (carbocation rearranges) | Not possible |

#### SN1 Mechanism

**Steps**:
1. **Slow step (rate-determining)** : Leaving group departs, forming carbocation.
2. **Fast step**: Nucleophile attacks carbocation.
3. **Deprotonation** (if nucleophile was neutral).

**Characteristics**:
- Carbocation stability determines rate: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl
- Racemization at chiral centers (attack from both sides)
- Rearrangements possible via hydride or alkyl shifts
- Favored by polar protic solvents (stabilize carbocation and leaving group)

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Some drug degradation pathways (e.g., hydrolysis of tertiary alkyl halides)
- Synthesis of certain drug intermediates

#### SN2 Mechanism

**Characteristics**:
- Concerted: bond formation and bond breaking occur simultaneously
- Backside attack by nucleophile
- Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
- Steric hindrance slows reaction: CH₃ > 1° > 2° >> 3°
- Favored by polar aprotic solvents (DMF, DMSO, acetone)
- Strong nucleophiles favored

**Transition State**: Pentacoordinate carbon with partial bonds to nucleophile and leaving group.

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Alkylation reactions in drug synthesis
- Methylation of drugs in metabolism
- Synthesis of quaternary ammonium compounds

#### Nucleophiles and Leaving Groups

**Common Nucleophiles (in order of strength)** :

| Nucleophile | Type | Pharmaceutical Relevance |
|-------------|------|--------------------------|
| RS⁻, R⁻ | Very strong | Rare in physiological conditions |
| I⁻, CN⁻, HS⁻ | Strong | CN⁻ used in synthesis |
| NH₃, RNH₂ | Moderate | Amino groups in drugs |
| RO⁻, OH⁻ | Moderate | Alkoxide formation |
| H₂O, ROH | Weak | Hydrolysis reactions |
| F⁻, NO₃⁻, ClO₄⁻ | Very weak | Poor nucleophiles |

**Leaving Group Ability**:
Good leaving groups are weak bases (stable anions): 
I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ >> F⁻
Also good: tosylate (OTs), mesylate (OMs), triflate (OTf)

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**: In alkylating agents (anticancer drugs), chloride is the leaving group that allows DNA alkylation.

#### Factors Affecting Nucleophilic Substitution

| Factor | Effect on SN1 | Effect on SN2 |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Substrate structure | More substituted faster | Less substituted faster |
| Nucleophile strength | Minor effect | Major effect |
| Leaving group ability | Important | Important |
| Solvent | Polar protic favors | Polar aprotic favors |
| Concentration | Only substrate in rate law | Both substrate and Nu in rate law |

#### Pharmaceutical Examples

**1. Synthesis of Ethers (Williamson Synthesis)** :
SN2 reaction of alkoxide with primary alkyl halide.

**2. Alkylation of Amines** :
SN2 reaction of amine with alkyl halide (but overalkylation is a problem).

**3. Anticancer Alkylating Agents** :
Nitrogen mustards (e.g., cyclophosphamide) undergo SN2 reactions with DNA bases, crosslinking DNA.

**4. Prodrug Activation** :
Some prodrugs are activated by SN2 reactions (e.g., with glutathione).

**5. Drug Degradation** :
Hydrolysis of alkyl halides and esters can occur via SN1 or SN2 depending on structure.

### 6. Elimination Reactions

Elimination reactions involve loss of two atoms or groups from a molecule to form a multiple bond (usually an alkene).

#### Types: E1 and E2

| Feature | E1 (Elimination Unimolecular) | E2 (Elimination Bimolecular) |
|---------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Kinetics | Rate = k [substrate] | Rate = k [substrate][base] |
| Molecularity | Unimolecular | Bimolecular |
| Steps | Two steps (slow then fast) | One step (concerted) |
| Intermediate | Carbocation | Transition state (no intermediate) |
| Substrate reactivity | 3° > 2° > 1° | 3° > 2° > 1° (for base strength) |
| Base strength | Less important | Important |
| Leaving group | Important | Important |
| Stereochemistry | Non-stereospecific | Anti-periplanar required |
| Rearrangement | Possible | Not possible |
| Competition with substitution | SN1 competes | SN2 competes |

#### E1 Mechanism

**Steps**:
1. **Slow step**: Leaving group departs, forming carbocation.
2. **Fast step**: Base abstracts a β-hydrogen, forming alkene.

**Characteristics**:
- Same carbocation intermediate as SN1
- Follows Zaitsev's rule (more substituted alkene is major product)
- Rearrangements possible
- Favored by polar protic solvents
- Weak bases favor E1 over E2

#### E2 Mechanism

**Characteristics**:
- Concerted: Base abstracts β-H while leaving group departs
- Anti-periplanar geometry required (H and leaving group must be 180° apart)
- Strong base favors E2
- Stereospecific: gives specific alkene stereochemistry
- Follows Zaitsev's rule with small bases; with bulky bases (e.g., tert-butoxide), Hofmann product (less substituted alkene) may dominate

**Transition State**: Partial bonds to H (being removed) and leaving group, double bond forming.

#### Zaitsev's Rule vs. Hofmann Rule

| Rule | Description | Conditions |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Zaitsev's rule | More substituted alkene is major product | Small bases, E1, E2 with small bases |
| Hofmann rule | Less substituted alkene is major product | Bulky bases (steric hindrance), E2 |

#### Competition: Substitution vs. Elimination

| Conditions | Favored Reaction |
|------------|------------------|
| Strong nucleophile, 1° substrate | SN2 |
| Strong base, 3° substrate | E2 |
| Weak nucleophile/base, 3° substrate | SN1/E1 (mixture) |
| High temperature | Elimination favored |
| Bulky base | Elimination favored (Hofmann product) |

#### Pharmaceutical Relevance

**1. Drug Synthesis**:
Elimination reactions are used to introduce double bonds into drug molecules (e.g., steroid synthesis).

**2. Drug Degradation**:
Some drugs undergo elimination under basic conditions or at high temperatures, leading to loss of activity.

**3. Metabolism**:
Dehydrogenation (oxidative elimination) is a metabolic pathway catalyzed by enzymes.

**4. Prodrug Design**:
Some prodrugs are designed to undergo elimination to release the active drug (e.g., β-elimination of phosphate prodrugs).

### 7. Addition Reactions

Addition reactions occur when two molecules combine to form a single product, typically across a multiple bond (C=C, C≡C, C=O). They are the reverse of elimination reactions.

#### Types of Addition Reactions

**1. Electrophilic Addition to Alkenes**

This is the most common addition reaction, where an electrophile adds to the π bond, forming a carbocation that is then attacked by a nucleophile.

**General Mechanism**:
1. **Slow step**: Electrophile adds to π bond, forming carbocation.
2. **Fast step**: Nucleophile attacks carbocation.

**Examples**:

| Reaction | Reagent | Product | Regiochemistry | Stereochemistry |
|----------|---------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Hydrogenation | H₂/catalyst | Alkane | - | Syn addition |
| Halogenation | X₂ (Br₂, Cl₂) | Vicinal dihalide | Anti addition via halonium ion | Anti |
| Hydrohalogenation | HX | Alkyl halide | Markovnikov | Variable |
| Hydration | H₂O/H⁺ | Alcohol | Markovnikov | Variable |
| Oxymercuration | Hg(OAc)₂, then NaBH₄ | Alcohol | Markovnikov | No rearrangement |
| Hydroboration | BH₃, then H₂O₂/OH⁻ | Alcohol | Anti-Markovnikov | Syn addition |
| Epoxidation | Peracid | Epoxide | - | Stereospecific |

**Markovnikov's Rule**: In addition of HX to unsymmetrical alkene, H adds to the carbon with more H atoms (less substituted), and X adds to more substituted carbon. Reason: formation of more stable carbocation.

**Anti-Markovnikov addition**: Occurs with HBr in presence of peroxides (free radical mechanism).

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Synthesis of alcohol, halide, and epoxide intermediates
- Epoxidation in drug metabolism (can form reactive intermediates)
- Hydrogenation used in large-scale drug manufacturing

**2. Electrophilic Addition to Alkynes**

Similar to alkenes but can add one or two equivalents.
- With one equivalent HX → vinyl halide (Markovnikov)
- With two equivalents HX → geminal dihalide
- With one equivalent X₂ → dihaloalkene (trans usually)
- With two equivalents X₂ → tetrahaloalkane

**3. Nucleophilic Addition to Carbonyl Compounds**

Aldehydes and ketones undergo nucleophilic addition due to electrophilic carbonyl carbon.

**General Mechanism**:
1. Nucleophile attacks carbonyl carbon.
2. Tetrahedral intermediate forms.
3. Protonation gives alcohol (or elimination gives derivative).

**Examples**:

| Nucleophile | Product | Pharmaceutical Application |
|-------------|---------|---------------------------|
| H₂O | Gem-diol (hydrate) | Equilibrium in solutions |
| ROH | Hemiacetal, then acetal | Protecting groups, carbohydrate chemistry |
| HCN | Cyanohydrin | Synthesis of amino acids, hydroxy acids |
| Grignard reagent | Alcohol | Carbon-carbon bond formation in drug synthesis |
| NaHSO₃ | Bisulfite addition product | Purification of aldehydes/ketones |
| NH₃, RNH₂ | Imine (Schiff base) | Drug-receptor interactions, synthesis |
| NH₂OH | Oxime | Characterization, synthesis |
| Hydrazine | Hydrazone | Synthesis, drug intermediates |
| Wittig reagent | Alkene | Synthesis of alkenes from carbonyls |

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Imine formation is involved in many enzyme mechanisms (e.g., pyridoxal phosphate)
- Schiff bases are intermediates in drug synthesis
- Grignard reactions are fundamental in building complex drug molecules

**4. Michael Addition (Conjugate Addition)**

**Definition**: Nucleophilic addition to α,β-unsaturated carbonyl compounds (enones), where addition occurs at the β-carbon.

**Mechanism**: Nucleophile attacks β-carbon, forming enolate, which is then protonated.

**Nucleophiles**: Enolates (giving 1,5-dicarbonyls), amines, thiols, etc.

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Key reaction in steroid synthesis
- Involved in biosynthesis of natural products
- Some drugs act as Michael acceptors (e.g., certain anticancer agents alkylate proteins via Michael addition)

**5. Diels-Alder Reaction**

**Definition**: [4+2] cycloaddition between a conjugated diene and a dienophile (alkene or alkyne) to form a six-membered ring.

**Mechanism**: Concerted, pericyclic; stereospecific (cis principle retained).

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**:
- Synthesis of complex natural products (steroids, alkaloids)
- Synthesis of aromatic drug intermediates
- Used in combinatorial chemistry for drug discovery

### 8. Rearrangement Reactions

Rearrangement reactions involve migration of an atom or group from one atom to another within the same molecule, resulting in a structural isomer.

#### Common Rearrangements in Pharmaceutical Chemistry

**1. Carbocation Rearrangements**

In SN1 and E1 reactions, carbocations may rearrange via hydride shift or alkyl shift to form more stable carbocations.

**Example**: 3,3-dimethyl-1-butene with HCl gives rearranged product due to hydride shift.

**Pharmaceutical Relevance**: Explains unexpected products in drug synthesis and metabolism.

**2. Pinacol Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Acid-catalyzed rearrangement of vicinal diols (1,2-diols) to carbonyl compounds.

**Mechanism**: Protonation, loss of water, carbocation formation, migration of alkyl group, deprotonation.

**Pharmaceutical Example**: Synthesis of steroids and terpenes.

**3. Beckmann Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Acid-catalyzed rearrangement of ketoximes to N-substituted amides.

**Mechanism**: Protonation of oxime OH, migration of anti group to nitrogen, loss of water, formation of nitrilium ion, hydration to amide.

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of caprolactam (for nylon) and amide drugs. Also involved in drug metabolism.

**4. Hofmann Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Reaction of primary amides with Br₂/NaOH to give primary amines with one less carbon.

**Mechanism**: N-bromination, deprotonation, migration of R group to nitrogen (with loss of CO₂), protonation.

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of amine drugs from carboxylic acids.

**5. Curtius Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Thermal decomposition of acyl azides to give isocyanates, which can be trapped with nucleophiles to yield amines, urethanes, or ureas.

**Mechanism**: Loss of N₂, migration of R group to nitrogen, formation of isocyanate.

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of amines and derivatives.

**6. Claisen Rearrangement**

**Definition**: [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement of allyl vinyl ethers to γ,δ-unsaturated carbonyls.

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of natural products and complex molecules.

**7. Cope Rearrangement**

**Definition**: [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement of 1,5-dienes.

**8. Fries Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Rearrangement of phenolic esters to ortho- or para-hydroxy ketones under Lewis acid catalysis.

**Pharmaceutical Application**: Synthesis of hydroxy ketone intermediates.

**9. Benzilic Acid Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Base-catalyzed rearrangement of 1,2-diketones to α-hydroxy acids.

**10. Wagner-Meerwein Rearrangement**

**Definition**: Carbocation rearrangement involving migration of alkyl groups in terpene and steroid chemistry.

#### Pharmaceutical Significance of Rearrangements

| Rearrangement | Application in Pharmacy |
|---------------|------------------------|
| Beckmann | Synthesis of amide drugs, caprolactam |
| Hofmann | Synthesis of amines from acids |
| Curtius | Synthesis of amines, urethanes (prodrugs) |

### 9. Polymerization Reactions

Polymerization is the process of combining many small molecules (monomers) to form large chain-like molecules (polymers). In pharmacy, polymers are essential for drug delivery systems, excipients, and biomedical applications.

#### Types of Polymerization

**1. Addition Polymerization (Chain Growth)**

Monomers with double bonds (alkenes) polymerize via free radical, cationic, or anionic mechanisms.

**Mechanism**:
- **Initiation**: Formation of active center (radical, cation, anion)
- **Propagation**: Sequential addition of monomers
- **Termination**: Combination, disproportionation, or chain transfer

**Examples**:
| Polymer | Monomer | Pharmaceutical Application |
|---------|---------|---------------------------|
| Polyethylene | Ethylene | Packaging, containers |
| Polypropylene | Propylene | Packaging, medical devices |
| Polystyrene | Styrene | Tissue culture plates, labware |
| Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) | Methyl methacrylate | Bone cement, contact lenses |
| Poly(vinyl chloride) (PVC) | Vinyl chloride | IV bags, tubing |
| Poly(vinyl acetate) | Vinyl acetate | Adhesives, coatings |
| Polyacrylamide | Acrylamide | Gel electrophoresis, drug delivery |

**2. Condensation Polymerization (Step Growth)**

Monomers with two functional groups react with loss of small molecule (H₂O, HCl, etc.).

**Examples**:

| Polymer | Monomers | Linkage | Pharmaceutical Application |
|---------|----------|---------|---------------------------|
| Polyesters | Diol + diacid | Ester | Biodegradable sutures (PLA, PGA) |
| Polyamides | Diamine + diacid | Amide | Nylon, peptide polymers |
| Polyurethanes | Diol + diisocyanate | Urethane | Catheters, implants |
| Polycarbonates | Bisphenol A + phosgene | Carbonate | Medical devices |
| Polysaccharides | Monosaccharides | Glycosidic | Natural polymers (starch, cellulose) |

**3. Ring-Opening Polymerization**

Cyclic monomers (lactones, lactams, epoxides) polymerize by ring opening.

**Examples**:
- Polylactic acid (PLA) from lactide
- Polycaprolactone (PCL) from ε-caprolactone
- Poly(ethylene oxide) from ethylene oxide
- Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) from lactide and glycolide

#### Pharmaceutical Applications of Polymers

| Application | Polymer Examples | Role |
|-------------|------------------|------|
| Tablet binders | PVP, HPMC, starch | Hold tablet ingredients together |
| Tablet coatings | HPMC, ethyl cellulose, Eudragit | Taste masking, controlled release |
| Sustained release matrices | HPMC, PLGA, ethyl cellulose | Control drug release rate |
| Transdermal patches | Polyisobutylene, silicones | Adhesive matrix |
| Hydrogels | PVA, PEO, crosslinked polymers | Wound dressings, contact lenses |
| Biodegradable implants | PLGA, PLA, PCL | Long-acting injectables |
| Nanoparticles | PLGA, PLA, chitosan | Targeted drug delivery |
| Micelles | Block copolymers (PEG-PLA) | Solubilization of poorly soluble drugs |
| Viscosity enhancers | Carbomers, cellulose derivatives | Ophthalmic solutions, gels |
| Packaging | Polyethylene, polypropylene | Blister packs, bottles |

#### Biodegradable Polymers in Drug Delivery

| Polymer | Degradation Time | Drug Delivery Application |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| PLGA | Weeks to months (tunable) | Microparticles, nanoparticles, implants |
| PLA | Months to years | Sutures, implants |
| PCL | >1 year | Long-term implants |
| Chitosan | Enzymatic degradation | Mucoadhesive delivery |
| Gelatin | Days to weeks | Hydrogels, nanoparticles |
| Alginate | Ion exchange | Beads, hydrogels |

#### Polymerization in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

**1. Synthesis of Polymeric Excipients**:
Controlled polymerization to achieve specific molecular weights and properties.

**2. Cross-linking**:
To form hydrogels and insoluble matrices for controlled release.

**3. Copolymerization**:
To tune properties (e.g., PLGA combines fast-degrading PGA and slow-degrading PLA).

**4. Graft Polymerization**:
To modify surfaces or create functional polymers.
