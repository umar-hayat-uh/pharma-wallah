# Chapter 1: Allergens and Allergenic Preparations 

#### **1. Introduction to Allergy and Atopy**

*   **Definition of Allergy**: An allergy is a harmful immune response to a substance (an allergen) that is harmless to most individuals. The term was first defined in 1906 by von Pirquet as a "changed or altered reaction in the body".
*   **Historical Context**: Symptoms of allergic diseases like asthma have been recognized for centuries, with mentions in ancient texts such as the Papyrus Ebers.
*   **Atopy**: This term describes a hereditary predisposition to develop classic allergic diseases like asthma, hay fever, and atopic dermatitis. Individuals with a family history of atopy are genetically predisposed to produce immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies against common environmental allergens.
*   **Epidemiology**: Approximately 10% to 30% of the global population is affected by allergic diseases, and the prevalence has been increasing.

#### **2. Types of Hypersensitivity**

Hypersensitivity reactions are classified into four types (I-IV) based on the underlying immune mechanism and time course.

*   **Type I (Immediate, IgE-Mediated)**: This is the "true" allergy. Upon first exposure to an allergen, susceptible individuals produce allergen-specific IgE antibodies, which bind to mast cells. Subsequent exposures trigger mast cells to release inflammatory mediators like histamine within minutes. This causes conditions such as allergic rhinitis (hay fever), allergic asthma, food and drug allergies, and anaphylaxis.
*   **Type II (Cytotoxic)**: This reaction involves IgG or IgM antibodies binding to antigens on the surface of the body's own cells, marking them for destruction. Examples include hemolytic transfusion reactions and some autoimmune diseases like Goodpasture syndrome.
*   **Type III (Immune Complex-Mediated)**: This occurs when antigen-antibody complexes (immune complexes) form and deposit in tissues, causing inflammation. Conditions like lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, and serum sickness are examples.
*   **Type IV (Delayed, Cell-Mediated)**: This reaction is not antibody-driven but is mediated by T cells. It typically develops 48-72 hours after exposure. Examples include contact dermatitis (e.g., poison ivy, nickel allergy), certain drug reactions, and the tuberculin skin test.

#### **3. Classification of Allergens**

Allergens are classified based on their route of entry into the body. The table below provides a systematic overview:

| Type of Allergen | Definition & Key Characteristics | Common Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Inhalant (Respiratory)** | Airborne particles that contact nasal/bronchial mucosa. Major cause of seasonal (pollinosis) and perennial rhinitis. | Pollens (trees, grasses, ragweed), mold spores, house dust mites, animal dander, volatile oils. |
| **Ingestant (Food)** | Allergens present in foodstuffs that are swallowed. | Cow's milk, eggs, soy, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat. |
| **Injectant** | Substances introduced parenterally. | Insect venom (bee stings), therapeutic drugs (e.g., penicillin), vaccines, antivenoms. |
| **Contactant** | Substances that cause a reaction upon direct contact with the epithelium, often leading to Type IV hypersensitivity. | Poison ivy/oak, metals (nickel), fragrances, cosmetics, latex, preservatives. |
| **Infectant** | Allergens derived from metabolic wastes or products of pathogenic microorganisms. | Proteins from bacteria, viruses, or fungi. |
| **Infestant** | Allergens from parasitic microorganisms in or on the body. | Allergens from mites or other parasites. |

**Note on Modern Allergens**: Contemporary environmental changes have introduced new sources, such as air pollution, industrial chemicals, food additives, and processed foods, which are linked to the rising prevalence and severity of allergic diseases.

#### **4. Allergy Tests and Diagnosis**

Testing is indicated when avoidance measures and medical management are insufficient, or when a definitive diagnosis is needed for management. It should not be used for screening asymptomatic individuals.

| Test Type | Principle & Purpose | Evaluates Reaction Type | Key Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Skin Prick Test (SPT)** | A drop of allergenic extract is pricked into the skin's surface. A wheal-and-flare reaction within 15-20 minutes indicates sensitization. | **Type I (Immediate)** | **Gold standard** for inhalant and many food allergies. Requires stopping antihistamines before testing. |
| **Intradermal Test** | A small amount of allergen is injected into the dermis. More sensitive than SPT. | **Type I (Immediate)** | Used for insect venom and penicillin allergy when SPT is negative. Carries a higher risk of systemic reaction. |
| **Serum Specific IgE Test** | Measures levels of allergen-specific IgE antibodies in blood (e.g., via ImmunoCAP). | **Type I (Immediate)** | Useful when SPT is not feasible (e.g., severe skin conditions, patient on antihistamines, risk of anaphylaxis). |
| **Patch Test** | Allergens are applied to patches placed on the skin for 48 hours; reactions are read at 48-96 hours. | **Type IV (Delayed)** | **Gold standard** for diagnosing allergic contact dermatitis (e.g., to metals, fragrances). |

#### **5. Treatment of Allergy**

*   **Avoidance**: The primary and most effective strategy is identifying and avoiding the causative allergen.
*   **Pharmacotherapy**: Symptomatic relief is achieved using antihistamines (oral, nasal, ocular), corticosteroids (nasal sprays, inhalers, topical), and bronchodilators.
*   **Emergency Treatment**: Anaphylaxis requires immediate administration of **epinephrine (adrenaline)** via auto-injector.
*   **Allergen Immunotherapy (AIT)**: Also known as "allergy vaccine" therapy, this is the only treatment that modifies the underlying immune disease. It involves administering gradually increasing doses of standardized allergenic extracts to induce clinical and immunological tolerance.

#### **6. Preparation of Allergy Vaccines (Allergen Immunotherapy Extracts)**

Allergen vaccines are pharmaceutical preparations derived from natural sources that contain the allergenic components responsible for sensitization. The manufacturing and standardization process is critical for their safety and efficacy.

*   **Source Material Selection**: The process begins with the selection of appropriate allergenic source materials (e.g., specific pollens, mite cultures). The material must contain all relevant allergens in sufficient amounts.
*   **Extraction**: The active allergenic components, which are typically water-soluble proteins, are extracted from the source material using aqueous solutions.
*   **Standardization**: This is the most crucial step to ensure **batch-to-batch consistency**. Standardization involves:
    1.  **Securing Optimal Complexity**: Ensuring the final extract contains the full spectrum of relevant allergens from the source.
    2.  **Controlling Major Allergens**: Quantifying key allergenic proteins (e.g., *Der p 1* in dust mites).
    3.  **Reproducing Allergenic Potency**: Using biological or immunological methods to ensure consistent strength.
*   **Formulation**: The standardized extract is formulated for clinical use, often by adsorption to a carrier like **aluminum hydroxide (alum)** to create a depot effect and enhance the immune response.
*   **Regulatory Oversight**: Specific guidelines from regulatory agencies (like the FDA and EMA) govern the entire manufacturing process to guarantee the quality, safety, and efficacy of the final allergenic product.
