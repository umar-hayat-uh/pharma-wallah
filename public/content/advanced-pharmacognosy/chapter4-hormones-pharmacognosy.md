# Chapter 4: Hormones in Pharmacognosy 

#### **1. Introduction and Chemical Classification**
*   **Definition**: Hormones are specific chemical messengers secreted by ductless endocrine glands or specialized cells. They are transported via the bloodstream to exert regulatory effects on the activity of specific target cells or organs located elsewhere in the body.
*   **Scope in Pharmacognosy**: The discipline focuses on hormones derived from natural sources (animal tissues, plants, microbes) and their use as crude extracts, purified substances, or as lead compounds for semi-synthesis and drug design.
*   **Chemical Classification**: Hormones are classified based on their chemical structure, which determines their solubility, mechanism of action, and methods of extraction:
    1.  **Peptide/Protein Hormones**: Chains of amino acids (e.g., insulin, growth hormone, oxytocin). Water-soluble, acting via membrane receptors.
    2.  **Amino Acid Derivatives**: Derived from tyrosine or tryptophan (e.g., thyroid hormones T3/T4, epinephrine, melatonin). Mostly water-soluble.
    3.  **Steroid Hormones**: Derived from cholesterol (e.g., estrogens, testosterone, cortisol, aldosterone). Lipid-soluble, acting via intracellular receptors.
    4.  **Eicosanoids**: Derived from arachidonic acid (e.g., prostaglandins, leukotrienes). Act as local hormones.

#### **2. Commercial Production, Extraction, and Isolation**
The production method depends on the hormone's chemical nature, complexity, and required quantity.

*   **Direct Extraction from Animal Tissues**: The traditional method for many peptide and steroid hormones.
    *   **Source**: Endocrine glands from slaughterhouse animals (e.g., pancreata for insulin, adrenal glands for epinephrine, ovaries for estrogens).
    *   **Process**: Involves careful collection, immediate freezing or preservation, homogenization, and solvent extraction (often using aqueous or alcohol-based solvents). This is followed by a series of purification steps such as salt precipitation, chromatography, and crystallization.
*   **Semi-synthesis**: A hybrid approach where a natural precursor, often extracted from plants or animals, is chemically converted into the desired hormone.
    *   **Example**: Diosgenin, a steroidal sapogenin extracted from certain *Dioscorea* (yam) species, is used as a starting material for the commercial semi-synthesis of progesterone, cortisol, and other steroid hormones. This method revolutionized steroid therapy in the mid-20th century.
*   **Recombinant DNA Technology (rDNA)**: The modern, predominant method for producing complex peptide and protein hormones.
    *   **Process**: The gene encoding the human hormone (e.g., insulin, growth hormone) is inserted into a host organism (e.g., *E. coli* bacteria or yeast).
    *   **Advantages**: Provides a pure, abundant, and non-allergenic product identical to human hormone, eliminating ethical concerns and supply issues associated with animal sources.

#### **3. Animal Hormones: Pharmacognostical Study**
Animal-derived hormones have been cornerstone therapeutics. Their study involves understanding source, isolation, and therapeutic use.

| Chemical Class | Key Examples | Natural Source (Traditional) | Major Therapeutic/Physiological Role | Pharmacognostical Note |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Peptide Hormones** | **Insulin** | Pancreas of pigs (porcine) or cows (bovine). | Regulation of blood glucose; treatment of diabetes mellitus. | Modern therapy uses human insulin produced by **recombinant technology**. |
| | **Oxytocin** | Posterior pituitary gland. | Stimulates uterine contractions during labor and milk ejection. | Now synthesized chemically (nonapeptide). |
| | **Growth Hormone** | Anterior pituitary gland. | Stimulates growth and cell reproduction. | Previously sourced from cadavers; now exclusively **recombinant human GH**. |
| **Amino Acid Derivatives** | **Thyroid Hormones (T4, T3)** | Thyroid gland (desiccated thyroid). | Regulation of metabolism. | Now synthetically produced (levothyroxine sodium). |
| | **Epinephrine (Adrenaline)** | Adrenal medulla or synthetic. | Vasoconstriction, bronchodilation; treatment of anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest. | Can be extracted but is commonly synthesized. |
| **Steroid Hormones** | **Cortisol (Hydrocortisone)** | Adrenal cortex. | Anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive; treatment of Addison's disease. | Originally extracted; now produced semi-synthetically from plant sterols. |
| | **Sex Steroids (Estradiol, Testosterone, Progesterone)** | Ovaries, testes, corpus luteum; also plant precursors. | Hormone replacement therapy, contraception. | **Diosgenin** from yams is a key precursor for semi-synthesis. |

#### **4. Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)**
These are endogenous signaling molecules that regulate plant growth, development, and responses to stimuli. While not used as direct human therapeutics, their study is crucial in agriculture and for understanding plant-derived medicinal compounds.

*   **Auxins (e.g., Indole-3-acetic acid - IAA)**:
    *   **Function**: Promote cell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation, and fruit development.
    *   **Commercial Source/Use**: Synthetic auxins (e.g., 2,4-D) are widely used as herbicides and rooting agents.
*   **Gibberellins (e.g., GA₃ - Gibberellic Acid)**:
    *   **Function**: Stimulate stem elongation, seed germination, and fruit setting.
    *   **Commercial Production**: Fermented by the fungus *Fusarium fujikuroi*. Used to increase fruit size (e.g., seedless grapes) and in malting barley.
*   **Cytokinins (e.g., Zeatin)**:
    *   **Function**: Promote cell division, delay senescence, and stimulate bud growth.
    *   **Source**: Found in developing fruits and seeds. Synthetic versions are used in tissue culture.
*   **Abscisic Acid (ABA)**:
    *   **Function**: Induces stomatal closure during water stress, promotes seed dormancy, and inhibits growth.
    *   **Role**: Critical for plant stress response; studied for its potential analogs.
