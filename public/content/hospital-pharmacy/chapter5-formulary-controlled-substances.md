# Chapter 5 : The Formulary System & Controlled Substances 

These two chapters are intrinsically linked. The **Hospital Formulary System** is the overarching governance framework for *all* medications, while **Controlled Substances** represent the highest-risk category within that framework, requiring an additional layer of stringent regulatory control. Together, they form the bedrock of safe, effective, and legally compliant drug management in a hospital.

#### **1. The Hospital Formulary System: Foundation of Drug Policy**
The formulary is a dynamic, continually updated list of medications approved for use within an institution. It is not merely a list but a **system of policies and procedures** governing drug evaluation, selection, procurement, and use.

*   **Purpose & Objectives**:
    *   **Ensure Therapeutic Efficacy & Safety**: Promote the use of the most effective and safest drugs available.
    *   **Contain Costs**: Leverage standardization and selective contracting to manage pharmaceutical expenses.
    *   **Facilitate Education**: Provide a focused set of drugs for staff to become proficient in using.
    *   **Support Quality Assurance**: Create standards against which drug use can be evaluated and improved.
*   **Governance: The Pharmacy & Therapeutics (P&T) Committee**: This is the multi-disciplinary body (including physicians, pharmacists, nurses, administrators) responsible for formulary management. Its key tasks are:
    1.  **Drug Evaluation & Selection**: Reviewing clinical evidence (efficacy, safety, pharmacoeconomic data) to make evidence-based formulary decisions.
    2.  **Formulary Maintenance**: Adding new agents, deleting outdated ones, and implementing therapeutic interchanges (e.g., substituting one proton pump inhibitor for another based on contract).
    3.  **Policy Development**: Creating guidelines for the use of high-risk, high-cost, or non-formulary drugs.

#### **2. Controlled Substances: Stringent Management Within the Formulary**
Controlled substances (CS) are drugs with abuse potential, regulated by the **Controlled Substances Act (CSA)** and enforced by the **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**. They are integrated into but managed far more strictly than the general formulary.

*   **Key Regulatory Tenets**:
    *   **Secure Storage**: Must be stored in a **double-locked, immovable cabinet** (e.g., within a locked safe or room). Automated Dispensing Cabinets (ADCs) used for CS must meet robust security standards.
    *   **Documentation & Accountability**: Every milligram must be accounted for from receipt to administration or waste, using specific **DEA order forms (Form 222)** for Schedule II purchases and meticulous perpetual inventory logs.
    *   **Loss Reporting**: Any discrepancy or diversion must be investigated and reported to the DEA if significant.

#### **3. Integrated Management: From Procurement to Waste**
The management of both formulary and controlled drugs follows a similar lifecycle, but with critical differences in scrutiny and documentation.

| Management Phase | Formulary Medications (General) | Controlled Substances (Additional/Stricter Requirements) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Procurement & Selection** | Based on P&T Committee review of clinical need, cost, and safety. Purchased via competitive bidding or group contracts. | Must be **medically necessary** and justified within the formulary. Requires a **DEA registration** for the hospital (separate from practitioner licenses). Schedule II drugs require a **DEA Form 222** or electronic equivalent for ordering. |
| **2. Distribution & Storage** | Central pharmacy storage; distributed via unit dose, ADC, or floor stock models. | **Secure, dual-locked storage** is mandatory at all points (central pharmacy, satellites, nursing units). Access is strictly limited. |
| **3. Prescribing & Ordering** | Must be by an authorized prescriber. Non-formulary requests may require prior authorization. | Prescribers must have a **valid DEA license**. Institutional protocols often mandate **verbal orders be confirmed in writing within 24 hours**, especially for Schedule II drugs. |
| **4. Dispensing & Documentation** | Dispensed against a valid patient order. Documentation supports billing and clinical care. | Requires **complete and accurate perpetual inventory**. For each dose: **patient name, date, drug, amount, dispenser signature, and administering nurse signature**. **"See-Thru"** signature logs are often used. |
| **5. Administration** | Administered by licensed nurse per order. | Requires **independent double-check by two nurses** for high-risk CS (e.g., IV opioids). The waste of any **partial dose must be witnessed and co-signed** immediately. |
| **6. Inventory Management** | Regular physical counts for financial and reordering purposes. | **Mandatory scheduled counts**: **Schedule II** drugs are counted continuously (perpetual inventory) with **rigorous end-of-shift counts** in patient care areas. **Schedules III-V** have regular scheduled counts (e.g., weekly). All counts must be **verified by two licensed personnel** (e.g., nurse & nurse, or nurse & pharmacist). |
| **7. Disposal & Waste** | Follows environmental and safety guidelines. | **Witnessed destruction** is required for expired, contaminated, or wasted doses. Documentation must include drug, quantity, reason, and signatures of two witnesses. Some institutions use **commercial reverse-distributors** for official disposal. |

#### **4. The Role of Technology and Quality Assurance**
*   **Technology**: A robust **Pharmacy Information System** integrated with **Automated Dispensing Cabinets (ADCs)** and the **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** is essential. It enforces formulary restrictions, flags drug interactions, manages controlled substance perpetual inventories electronically, and creates an immutable audit trail.
*   **Quality Assurance**: Both systems require ongoing monitoring.
    *   **Formulary System**: Audited via **drug utilization reviews (DUR)** to ensure compliance with use guidelines and identify outliers.
    *   **Controlled Substances**: Audited via **regular reconciliation** of dispensing records, inventory counts, and patient administration records to detect discrepancies that may indicate diversion or error.

### **Key References from Recommended Books & Sources**
1.  **American Society of Health-System Pharmacists (ASHP). (2020). ASHP Guidelines on the Pharmacy and Therapeutics Committee and the Formulary System.** The definitive professional standard for establishing and maintaining a formulary system.
2.  **United States Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA). *Practitioner's Manual.* & *Title 21, Code of Federal Regulations (CFR), Part 1300 et seq.*** The primary legal source for all requirements regarding ordering, storing, dispensing, recordkeeping, and disposal of controlled substances.
3.  **American Hospital Formulary Service (AHFS). *AHFS Drug Information*.** While primarily a drug reference, its introductory sections provide the philosophical and practical guidelines for a rational, evidence-based formulary system.
4.  **Brown, T. R. (Ed.). (2006). *Handbook of Institutional Pharmacy Practice*. ASHP.** Contains essential chapters on formulary management, the P&T Committee, and the specific operational procedures for controlled substance security and inventory control.
5.  **Thompson, J. E. (2009). *A Practical Guide to Contemporary Pharmacy Practice* (3rd ed.). Lippincott Williams & Wilkins.** Offers practical checklists and sample forms for formulary management and controlled substance logistics.
