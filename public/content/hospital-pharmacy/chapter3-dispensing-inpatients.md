# Chapter 3: Dispensing to Inpatients – Detailed Notes

Dispensing medications to hospitalized patients is a core, complex function of a hospital pharmacy. The chosen system must balance **safety, efficiency, cost, and clinical involvement**. This chapter covers the traditional and modern models that define this critical process.

#### **1. Methods of Dispensing and Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)**
There are four primary systems for distributing drugs to inpatients, each with distinct workflows and control levels[reference:0]. Written SOPs are essential for each to ensure consistency, safety, and accountability.

| System | Description | Key Advantages | Key Disadvantages / Considerations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Individual Drug Order (Prescription) System** | Physician writes a prescription for each patient; medication is dispensed per order from the pharmacy, often for a 24-hour or short course supply. | • Direct pharmacist review of every order.[reference:1]<br>• Clear inventory control and patient charging.[reference:2]<br>• Facilitates pharmacist-doctor-nurse interaction. | • Labor-intensive and costly.[reference:3]<br>• Potential for delays in medication administration. |
| **Complete Floor Stock System** | A limited selection of drugs, approved by the Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee (PTC), is stored on the nursing unit for nurse administration.[reference:4] | • Immediate availability of medications.<br>• Useful for after-hours supply.[reference:5] | • Less pharmacist control; higher risk of misuse or error.<br>• Requires careful periodic review to avoid overstocking.[reference:6] |
| **Combined Individual & Floor Stock System** | The individual order system is used as the primary method, supplemented by a limited floor stock for emergency or common drugs.[reference:7] | • Balances control with accessibility.<br>• Most commonly used system in many hospitals.[reference:8] | • Requires clear policies defining which drugs are floor stock. |
| **Unit Dose Distribution System** | Medications are provided in single, ready-to-administer packages, dispensed typically for a 24-hour period. This is the modern standard for safety. | • **Greatly reduces medication errors.**[reference:9]<br>• Minimizes drug waste.<br>• Eliminates nursing station storage needs.[reference:10] | • Requires significant initial investment in packaging and logistics.[reference:11] |

**Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs):** For any system, SOPs must detail every step: order receipt & verification, labeling, preparation, final check, delivery, and documentation. They also must cover error reporting, controlled substance handling, and after-hours procedures.

#### **2. Unit Dose Dispensing**
This is not just a packaging change but a comprehensive **patient-specific distribution system** where each dose is packaged, labeled, and checked individually.

*   **Core Principle**: "Those medications which are ordered, packed, handled, administered and charged in multiples of single dose units"[reference:12].
*   **Key Models**:
    *   **Centralized Unit Dose Dispensing (CUDD)**: All inpatient drugs are stored, prepared, and dispensed in unit doses from the main pharmacy at the time the dose is due[reference:13].
    *   **Decentralized Unit Dose Dispensing (DUDD)**: Operates through small **satellite pharmacies** located on patient care floors[reference:14]. Prescriptions are sent directly to the satellite, entered into a patient profile, filled by technicians, and checked by a pharmacist before administration[reference:15].
    *   **Combined CUDD-DUDD**: Some hospitals operate both central and satellite pharmacies simultaneously for flexibility[reference:16].
*   **Automation & Evidence**: Automated Unit Dose Dispensing Systems (UDDS) using robots have shown significant benefits. A systematic review concluded they **significantly reduce medication administration errors (MAEs)** compared to traditional ward stock systems[reference:17]. While patient-specific dispensing is highly accurate, the automation is a costly and complex investment[reference:18].

#### **3. Satellite Pharmacy Concepts**
A satellite pharmacy is a **remote but integrated unit** of the main pharmacy, located within a specific patient care area (e.g., ICU, oncology ward) to provide dedicated services[reference:19].

*   **Purpose & Benefits**:
    *   **Proximity to Care Teams**: Brings the pharmacist physically closer to physicians and nurses, facilitating real-time consultation and clinical pharmacy services[reference:20].
    *   **Improved Turnaround Time**: Enables faster dispensing and delivery of medications, especially for time-sensitive drugs.
    *   **Specialized Focus**: Can be tailored to the needs of a specific department (e.g., a dedicated cytotoxic unit in a cancer ward)[reference:21].
*   **Operational Requirements**: As per guidelines, a satellite pharmacy requires[reference:22]:
    *   **Secure, controlled access** to prevent unauthorized entry.
    *   **Dedicated workspace** with dispensing benches, computer workstations, and counseling areas.
    *   **Appropriate environmental controls** (temperature, humidity, lighting) for drug storage and preparation.
    *   **A direct logistical and managerial link** to the central pharmacy.

#### **4. Emerging Concepts & Technology**
*   **Automated Dispensing Cabinets (ADCs)**: Secure storage cabinets located on nursing units, allowing nurses to retrieve patient-specific medications using electronic controls. They remain under the pharmacy's authority and integrate with the hospital's information system[reference:23].
*   **Integrated Computerized Systems**: The "patient server" concept and computerized physician order entry (CPOE) systems aim to streamline the entire process from prescribing to administration, reducing transcription errors and delays[reference:24].
