
# Chapter 6: Dispensing During Off-Hours & Off-Site Pharmacies 

The primary pharmacy cannot operate 24 hours a day in most hospitals. This chapter details the systems and procedures that bridge the gap when the main pharmacy is closed and the methods for managing medication services at remote locations.

#### **1. Core Principles and Importance**
The goal is to balance **accessibility** for urgent patient needs with **safety, control, and accountability**. Ineffective off-hours systems are a known high-risk area for medication errors and drug diversion. Key principles include:
*   **Minimization**: The need for after-hours access should be minimized through robust daytime services (e.g., proper discharge planning, adequate unit stock).
*   **Control & Security**: Systems must be designed to prevent unauthorized access and ensure all medication removals are documented and reviewed.
*   **Pharmacy Oversight**: Ultimate responsibility rests with the pharmacy department, which must establish, monitor, and audit all after-hours procedures.

#### **2. Methods for Off-Hours Dispensing**
Hospitals typically use a layered approach, combining several methods to cover different scenarios.

| Method | Description | Typical Use Case | Key Safety/Control Mechanisms |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **After-Hours Medication Cabinets** | A locked cabinet or closet on the nursing unit, stocked with a limited, pre-approved formulary of urgent-need medications. | Standard method for most non-critical, urgent needs when pharmacy is closed (e.g., antibiotics for a new infection, antiemetics). | **Limited, P&T-approved formulary**. **Two-nurse witness** for removal (one removes, one documents). **Sealed, numbered kits** or **night cupboard books** for tracking. Mandatory **pharmacy review and reconciliation** the next business day. |
| **Automated Dispensing Cabinets (ADCs) - After-Hours Mode** | Using the ADC's override or "after-hours" function, which allows access to a broader range of medications than normally available for a specific patient. | **True emergencies** where the needed drug is not in the standard after-hours cabinet (e.g., a specific anticonvulsant, life-saving antidote). | **Strictly for emergencies only.** Requires **override reason documentation** in the system (e.g., "STAT for code blue"). Generates an **immediate electronic alert** to pharmacy/administration. **Mandatory, rigorous pharmacy follow-up** to verify clinical justification and complete proper profiling/billing. |
| **On-Call Pharmacist** | A pharmacist is available by pager or phone to return to the hospital or provide remote verification and guidance. | Complex situations, preparation of high-risk medications (e.g., chemotherapy), or when nursing staff is uncertain. Required in many standards for certain high-risk medication accesses. | Provides **professional judgment and verification**. May involve **remote order verification** via a secure system or **physical return** to the pharmacy. |
| **Standard Unit Stock/Floor Stock** | A small inventory of frequently used, non-urgent routine medications (e.g., stool softeners, topical creams) kept on the unit. | Reduces trivial after-hours calls but is **not** for new/urgent orders. | Part of the regular inventory, replenished by pharmacy. Not a substitute for proper after-hours procedures for new medication orders. |

#### **3. Managing Off-Site or Satellite Pharmacies**
These are permanent, smaller pharmacy units located in remote clinics, outpatient cancer centers, or affiliated community health centers. They operate under the license of the main hospital but face unique challenges.

*   **Operational Models**:
    *   **Fully Staffed Satellites**: Have a pharmacist and technician(s) on-site during defined hours. They operate semi-independently but are linked to the main hospital's formulary, IT systems, and purchasing contracts.
    *   **Telepharmacy-Enabled Satellites**: A technician is on-site to handle dispensing logistics, while a pharmacist at a central location performs all clinical checks and verifications via a secure audio-video link and remote access to the system. This model is crucial for extending services to rural or low-volume locations.
*   **Key Management Requirements**:
    *   **Policy & Procedure Alignment**: All SOPs must align with the main pharmacy's policies but be adapted to the satellite's specific scope of services.
    *   **Integrated Technology**: Must have real-time access to the **hospital's Pharmacy Information System (PIS)** and **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** to view patient profiles, drug histories, and enter orders.
    *   **Unified Formulary**: Must adhere to the main hospital's formulary decisions. Non-formulary requests must follow the same central P&T Committee process.
    *   **Supply Chain Linkage**: Inventory is typically managed as a sub-inventory of the central pharmacy, with routine deliveries. Must have contingency plans for after-hours needs at the satellite site itself.
    *   **Quality Assurance**: Regular audits of dispensing accuracy, documentation, and compliance must be conducted by the central pharmacy leadership.

#### **4. Technology and Integration**
Technology is the linchpin that makes modern off-hours and off-site systems safe and auditable.
*   **Pharmacy Information System (PIS)**: The backbone. Allows for remote order entry and verification by an on-call pharmacist.
*   **Automated Dispensing Cabinets (ADCs)**: Provide detailed, patient-specific audit trails for every medication removal, even during overrides.
*   **Electronic Health Record (EHR) Integration**: Ensures the medication list is updated in real-time, providing a single source of truth for all caregivers.
*   **Barcode Medication Administration (BCMA)**: Works in concert with ADCs; nurses scan the patient and the drug from the ADC drawer, providing a final check even for after-hours medications.

### **Key References from Recommended Books & Sources**
*   **Brown, T. R. (Ed.). (2006). *Handbook of Institutional Pharmacy Practice*. ASHP.** Contains essential chapters on the design and management of satellite pharmacy services and the policies governing after-hours medication distribution.
*   **American Society of Health-System Pharmacists (ASHP). (2018). *ASHP Guidelines on the Safe Use of Automated Dispensing Cabinets.*** Provides the definitive safety standards for the configuration, use, and override protocols of ADCs, which are central to off-hours dispensing.
*   **Thompson, J. E. (2009). *A Practical Guide to Contemporary Pharmacy Practice* (3rd ed.). Lippincott Williams & Wilkins.** Offers practical protocols and checklists for setting up after-hours medication cabinets and managing night/weekend pharmacy operations.
*   **Holdford, D. A., & Brown, T. R. (2010). *Introduction to Hospital and Health-System Pharmacy Practice*. ASHP.** Discusses the organizational models for integrated health systems, including the management of off-site ambulatory and specialty pharmacy locations.
