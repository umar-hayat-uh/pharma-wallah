## Unit 4: The Heart

### Introduction to Cardiac Physiology

The heart is a muscular organ that functions as a dual pump, propelling blood through the pulmonary and systemic circulations. Its rhythmic contractions are generated spontaneously by specialized cardiac muscle cells and are modulated by neural and hormonal inputs. Understanding the anatomy and physiology of the heart is essential for comprehending cardiovascular function and the basis of many clinical conditions.

**Functions of the Heart**:
- **Generating blood pressure**: Contractions create pressure that propels blood through vessels.
- **Routing blood**: Separates pulmonary and systemic circulations.
- **Ensuring one-way flow**: Valves prevent backflow.
- **Regulating blood supply**: Adjusts heart rate and stroke volume to meet metabolic demands.

### 1. Physiological Anatomy of Heart Muscle

#### 1.1 Types of Cardiac Muscle

Cardiac muscle (myocardium) has characteristics intermediate between skeletal and smooth muscle. It is striated like skeletal muscle but functions as a functional syncytium.

**Comparison of Muscle Types**:

| Feature | Cardiac Muscle | Skeletal Muscle | Smooth Muscle |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Striations** | Yes | Yes | No |
| **Nuclei** | Single, central | Multiple, peripheral | Single, central |
| **Cell shape** | Branched, cylindrical | Long, cylindrical | Spindle-shaped |
| **Intercalated discs** | Yes | No | No |
| **Gap junctions** | Yes (at intercalated discs) | No | Yes (in some) |
| **Automaticity** | Yes (some cells) | No | Yes (in some) |
| **Action potential duration** | Long (200-400 ms) | Short (2-5 ms) | Variable |
| **Refractory period** | Long (prevents tetanus) | Short | Variable |
| **Tetanus** | Cannot occur | Can occur | Can occur |
| **Control** | Involuntary, intrinsic | Voluntary | Involuntary |

#### 1.2 Microscopic Structure of Cardiac Muscle

**Cardiomyocytes**:
- Length: 50-100 μm
- Diameter: 10-25 μm
- Branched, interconnected
- Contain myofibrils with sarcomeres (similar to skeletal muscle)
- Abundant mitochondria (~30-40% of cell volume) – high oxidative metabolism
- Glycogen stores

**Intercalated Discs**:
Specialized cell-cell junctions that connect cardiac muscle cells both mechanically and electrically.

| Component | Structure | Function |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Desmosomes (macula adherens)** | Protein plaques with cadherins; intermediate filaments (desmin) anchor | Mechanical adhesion; prevent cell separation during contraction |
| **Fascia adherens** | Actin filaments anchor via cadherins | Transmit contractile force between cells |
| **Gap junctions (connexons)** | Clusters of connexin proteins forming channels | Allow ion flow (electrical coupling); enable action potential propagation between cells |

**Functional Syncytium**:
- Atria form one functional unit (atrial syncytium)
- Ventricles form another (ventricular syncytium)
- Atria and ventricles are electrically separated by fibrous skeleton (except at AV node)

#### 1.3 Cardiac Muscle Action Potential

Cardiac action potentials differ significantly from those in skeletal muscle and neurons. They have a prolonged plateau phase (Phase 2) due to slow calcium channels, which prevents tetanus and allows time for calcium-induced calcium release.

**Phases of Ventricular Action Potential**:

| Phase | Name | Ion Movement | Mechanism |
|-------|------|--------------|-----------|
| **Phase 4** | Resting membrane potential | Stable at -90 mV | K⁺ channels open (inward rectifier K⁺ channels, I<sub>K1</sub>) |
| **Phase 0** | Rapid depolarization | Fast Na⁺ channels open (I<sub>Na</sub>) | Na⁺ influx → rapid upstroke to +20 mV |
| **Phase 1** | Early rapid repolarization | Transient K⁺ channels open (I<sub>to</sub>) | K⁺ efflux causes slight repolarization ("notch") |
| **Phase 2** | Plateau | L-type Ca²⁺ channels open (I<sub>Ca,L</sub>); slow K⁺ channels delayed | Ca²⁺ influx balances K⁺ efflux → prolonged plateau (200-400 ms) |
| **Phase 3** | Rapid repolarization | Ca²⁺ channels inactivate; delayed rectifier K⁺ channels (I<sub>Kr</sub>, I<sub>Ks</sub>) open | K⁺ efflux dominates → repolarization to -90 mV |

**Refractory Periods**:
- **Absolute refractory period (ARP)** : From Phase 0 to mid-Phase 3; no new action potential can be initiated
- **Effective refractory period (ERP)** : Slightly longer than ARP; includes time when a weak stimulus might produce a local response but not propagated action potential
- **Relative refractory period (RRP)** : Late Phase 3; a stronger-than-normal stimulus can initiate an action potential

**Significance**: Long refractory period prevents tetanus (essential for pump function – allows filling between contractions).

**Comparison of Cardiac Action Potentials**:

| Cell Type | Resting Potential | Upstroke | Plateau | Duration | Rate |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|---------|----------|------|
| **SA node** | -60 mV (unstable) | Slow (Ca²⁺ channels) | No | ~150 ms | Fastest (60-100/min) |
| **Atrial muscle** | -80 mV | Fast (Na⁺ channels) | Short | ~150 ms | Follows SA node |
| **AV node** | -60 mV (unstable) | Slow (Ca²⁺ channels) | No | ~150 ms | Slower conduction (delay) |
| **Purkinje fibers** | -90 mV | Fast (Na⁺ channels) | Yes | ~300 ms | Fast conduction |
| **Ventricular muscle** | -90 mV | Fast (Na⁺ channels) | Yes | ~300 ms | Follows Purkinje |

#### 1.4 Excitation-Contraction Coupling in Cardiac Muscle

Similar to skeletal muscle but with important differences: Ca²⁺ influx triggers Ca²⁺ release from SR (calcium-induced calcium release, CICR).

**Steps**:

| Step | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1 | Action potential propagates along sarcolemma and into T-tubules |
| 2 | L-type Ca²⁺ channels (dihydropyridine receptors) on T-tubule open |
| 3 | Small Ca²⁺ influx (trigger Ca²⁺) |
| 4 | Trigger Ca²⁺ binds to ryanodine receptors (RyR2) on SR, causing them to open |
| 5 | Large Ca²⁺ release from SR (calcium spark) |
| 6 | Cytosolic [Ca²⁺] rises from ~0.1 μM to ~1-10 μM |
| 7 | Ca²⁺ binds to troponin C → tropomyosin moves → cross-bridge formation → contraction |
| 8 | Relaxation: Ca²⁺ pumped back into SR (SERCA2a) and out of cell (NCX, PMCA) |

**Key Differences from Skeletal Muscle**:

| Feature | Skeletal Muscle | Cardiac Muscle |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Source of Ca²⁺** | SR only | SR and extracellular (trigger) |
| **RyR isoform** | RyR1 | RyR2 |
| **Coupling** | Mechanical (DHPR directly coupled to RyR) | Chemical (CICR) |
| **Extracellular Ca²⁺ dependence** | Low (can contract without external Ca²⁺ briefly) | High (contractility depends on external Ca²⁺) |
| **SR Ca²⁺ load** | High | Moderate |
| **SERCA regulation** | Little | Regulated by phospholamban (inhibits; phosphorylation relieves inhibition) |

**Force-Frequency Relationship**:
- Increased heart rate → increased Ca²⁺ influx per unit time → increased SR Ca²⁺ load → increased contractility (Bowditch effect, treppe)

### 2. Structure of the Heart

#### 2.1 Gross Anatomy

**Location and Size**:
- Located in mediastinum, between lungs
- Apex: points left, anterior, inferior (5th intercostal space, midclavicular line)
- Base: posterior, superior (great vessels attach)
- Size: approximately 12 cm long, 8-9 cm wide, weighs ~250-350 g (adult)

**Pericardium**:

| Layer | Description | Function |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Fibrous pericardium** | Tough, inelastic connective tissue | Anchors heart; prevents overdistension |
| **Serous pericardium** | Thin, double membrane | |
| - Parietal layer | Lines fibrous pericardium | Secretes pericardial fluid |
| - Visceral layer (epicardium) | Adherent to heart surface | |
| **Pericardial cavity** | Space between parietal and visceral layers | Contains 15-50 mL pericardial fluid (lubricates, reduces friction) |

**Heart Wall Layers**:

| Layer | Composition | Function |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Epicardium** (visceral pericardium) | Mesothelium + connective tissue | Protective outer layer; contains coronary vessels, nerves, fat |
| **Myocardium** | Cardiac muscle (spiral bundles) | Contractile layer; thickest in left ventricle |
| **Endocardium** | Endothelium + connective tissue | Lines chambers and valves; continuous with blood vessel endothelium |

#### 2.2 Chambers of the Heart

The heart has four chambers: two atria (receiving chambers) and two ventricles (pumping chambers).

| Chamber | Wall Thickness | Function | Special Features |
|---------|---------------|----------|------------------|
| **Right atrium** | Thin (2-3 mm) | Receives deoxygenated blood from SVC, IVC, coronary sinus | Pectinate muscles; crista terminalis; fossa ovalis |
| **Right ventricle** | Moderate (3-5 mm) | Pumps blood to pulmonary circulation (low pressure) | Trabeculae carneae; papillary muscles; moderator band (septomarginal trabecula) |
| **Left atrium** | Thin (2-3 mm) | Receives oxygenated blood from pulmonary veins | Smooth walls; pectinate muscles in auricle |
| **Left ventricle** | Thick (8-15 mm) | Pumps blood to systemic circulation (high pressure) | Trabeculae carneae; papillary muscles; no moderator band |

**Interatrial Septum**:
- Separates left and right atria
- Foramen ovale (fetal) closes after birth → fossa ovalis

**Interventricular Septum**:
- Separates left and right ventricles
- Mostly muscular, but membranous portion superiorly (near atrioventricular valves)

#### 2.3 Valves of the Heart

Valves ensure one-way blood flow. They open and close passively in response to pressure gradients.

**Atrioventricular (AV) Valves**:

| Valve | Location | Cusps | Chordae Tendineae | Papillary Muscles |
|-------|----------|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Tricuspid valve** | Between right atrium and ventricle | 3 cusps (anterior, posterior, septal) | Attach to cusps | Attach to chordae; contract during systole to prevent prolapse |
| **Mitral valve (bicuspid)** | Between left atrium and ventricle | 2 cusps (anterior, posterior) | Attach to cusps | Attach to chordae; prevent inversion during systole |

**Semilunar Valves**:

| Valve | Location | Cusps | Function |
|-------|----------|-------|----------|
| **Pulmonary valve** | Between right ventricle and pulmonary trunk | 3 semilunar cusps | Prevents backflow from pulmonary trunk to RV during diastole |
| **Aortic valve** | Between left ventricle and aorta | 3 semilunar cusps | Prevents backflow from aorta to LV during diastole |

**Valve Function**:
- **Ventricular systole**: AV valves close (prevent backflow to atria); semilunar valves open
- **Ventricular diastole**: AV valves open; semilunar valves close (prevent backflow from arteries)

**Valve Disease**:

| Condition | Definition | Causes | Murmur |
|-----------|------------|--------|--------|
| **Stenosis** | Valve orifice narrowed; impedes forward flow | Congenital, rheumatic fever, calcific degeneration | Systolic (aortic/pulmonary stenosis) or diastolic (mitral/tricuspid stenosis) |
| **Regurgitation (insufficiency)** | Valve incompetent; allows backflow | Valve damage, dilated annulus, papillary muscle dysfunction | Systolic (mitral/tricuspid regurgitation) or diastolic (aortic/pulmonary regurgitation) |

#### 2.4 Coronary Circulation

The heart receives blood via coronary arteries; venous return via coronary sinus.

**Coronary Arteries**:

| Artery | Branches | Territory Supplied |
|--------|----------|---------------------|
| **Left main coronary artery** | Left anterior descending (LAD), left circumflex (LCx) | |
| - LAD | Septal perforators, diagonal branches | Anterior interventricular septum, anterior wall of LV, part of RV |
| - LCx | Obtuse marginal branches | Lateral and posterior wall of LV |
| **Right coronary artery (RCA)** | Acute marginal, posterior descending (PDA – in 85% of people) | Right ventricle, SA node (60%), AV node (80%), inferior wall of LV |

**Coronary Veins**:
- Great cardiac vein, middle cardiac vein, small cardiac vein drain into coronary sinus → right atrium
- Anterior cardiac veins drain directly into right atrium
- Thebesian veins (venae cordis minimae) drain directly into chambers (especially right side)

**Coronary Blood Flow**:
- Resting flow: ~250 mL/min (5% of cardiac output)
- Increases 4-5× during exercise
- Flow occurs mainly during diastole (ventricular contraction compresses intramyocardial vessels)

**Regulation**:
- Primarily metabolic (adenosine, NO, CO₂, O₂, K⁺, H⁺)
- Autoregulation (constant flow over pressure range 60-180 mmHg)
- Neural (sympathetic: α constriction, β₂ dilation; parasympathetic minimal)

### 3. Course of Blood Flow Through the Heart

The heart pumps blood through two circuits in series: pulmonary and systemic.

**Pathway of Blood Flow**:

```
Systemic veins (SVC, IVC, coronary sinus)
             ↓
        RIGHT ATRIUM
             ↓ (through tricuspid valve)
        RIGHT VENTRICLE
             ↓ (through pulmonary valve)
        PULMONARY TRUNK
             ↓ (left and right pulmonary arteries)
        LUNGS (gas exchange: O₂ in, CO₂ out)
             ↓ (pulmonary veins)
        LEFT ATRIUM
             ↓ (through mitral valve)
        LEFT VENTRICLE
             ↓ (through aortic valve)
        AORTA
             ↓ (systemic arteries)
        SYSTEMIC CAPILLARIES (gas exchange: O₂ out, CO₂ in)
             ↓ (systemic veins)
        (Return to right atrium)
```

**Flow Rates**:
- Cardiac output = 5 L/min (both ventricles pump equal volumes over time)
- Pulmonary circulation receives same flow as systemic (but at lower pressure)

**Fetal Circulation** (special features):
- Foramen ovale (bypasses lungs): shunts blood from right atrium to left atrium
- Ductus arteriosus: connects pulmonary trunk to aorta (bypasses lungs)
- Ductus venosus: bypasses liver
- Umbilical vein and arteries

### 4. The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle refers to the sequence of mechanical and electrical events during one complete heartbeat. It includes periods of contraction (systole) and relaxation (diastole).

#### 4.1 Phases of the Cardiac Cycle

For a heart rate of 75 bpm, cycle length = 0.8 seconds (systole ~0.3 s, diastole ~0.5 s).

**Ventricular Systole** (0.3 s):

| Phase | Description | AV Valves | Semilunar Valves | Ventricular Pressure | Ventricular Volume | ECG |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|-----|
| **1. Isovolumetric contraction** | Ventricles contract with all valves closed; pressure rises rapidly but volume constant | Closed | Closed | Rising rapidly (LV: 0 → 80 mmHg; RV: 0 → 8 mmHg) | Constant (end-diastolic volume) | After R wave (QRS) |
| **2. Rapid ejection** | Ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure; semilunar valves open; blood ejected rapidly | Closed | Open | Peaks (LV ~120 mmHg; RV ~25 mmHg) | Decreases rapidly | ST segment |
| **3. Reduced ejection** | Ventricular pressure falls slightly below arterial pressure but momentum keeps blood flowing; ejection slows | Closed | Open | Declining slightly | Decreases slowly | End of T wave |

**Ventricular Diastole** (0.5 s):

| Phase | Description | AV Valves | Semilunar Valves | Ventricular Pressure | Ventricular Volume | ECG |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|-----|
| **4. Isovolumetric relaxation** | Ventricles relax with all valves closed; pressure drops rapidly | Closed | Closed | Falling rapidly (LV: 80 → 0 mmHg; RV: 8 → 0 mmHg) | Constant (end-systolic volume) | End of T wave |
| **5. Rapid filling** | AV valves open when ventricular pressure falls below atrial pressure; blood rushes from atria into ventricles | Open | Closed | Low (continues falling) | Increases rapidly | After T wave |
| **6. Reduced filling (diastasis)** | Atrial and ventricular pressures nearly equal; filling slows | Open | Closed | Low (slowly rising) | Increases slowly | Before P wave |
| **7. Atrial systole** | Atria contract, ejecting additional blood into ventricles (~20-30% of ventricular filling) | Open | Closed | Slight rise | Increases to end-diastolic volume | After P wave |

**Atrial Pressure Waves**:
- **a wave**: Atrial systole
- **c wave**: Bulging of AV valves into atria during isovolumetric contraction
- **v wave**: Atrial filling during ventricular systole (venous return)

#### 4.2 Pressure-Volume Loop

The pressure-volume loop graphically represents the cardiac cycle, relating left ventricular pressure to volume.

**Key Points on Loop**:

| Point | Event | Volume | Pressure |
|-------|-------|--------|----------|
| **A** | End-diastole (mitral valve closes) | End-diastolic volume (EDV) ~120 mL | ~5-10 mmHg |
| **B** | Aortic valve opens | EDV | Diastolic pressure (~80 mmHg) |
| **C** | End-systole (aortic valve closes) | End-systolic volume (ESV) ~50 mL | Systolic pressure (~120 mmHg) |
| **D** | Mitral valve opens | ESV | ~5-10 mmHg |

**Loop Segments**:
- **A → B**: Isovolumetric contraction
- **B → C**: Ejection
- **C → D**: Isovolumetric relaxation
- **D → A**: Filling

**Clinical Relevance**:
- **Stroke volume** (SV) = EDV - ESV (~70 mL)
- **Ejection fraction** (EF) = SV / EDV (normal ~55-70%)
- Loop shifts with changes in preload, afterload, contractility

### 5. Conduction System of the Heart

The heart has specialized cells that generate and conduct electrical impulses, ensuring coordinated contraction.

#### 5.1 Components of the Conduction System

| Component | Location | Function | Intrinsic Rate |
|-----------|----------|----------|----------------|
| **Sinoatrial (SA) node** | Right atrium near superior vena cava | Primary pacemaker; initiates impulse | 60-100/min |
| **Internodal pathways** | Atrial myocardium (anterior, middle, posterior) | Conduct impulse from SA node to AV node | - |
| **Atrioventricular (AV) node** | Lower interatrial septum, near coronary sinus | Delays impulse (allows atrial contraction to complete before ventricular activation) | 40-60/min |
| **Bundle of His** | Interventricular septum (membranous) | Conducts impulse to ventricles | - |
| **Right bundle branch** | Right side of septum | Conducts to right ventricle | - |
| **Left bundle branch** | Left side of septum | Conducts to left ventricle (divides into anterior and posterior fascicles) | - |
| **Purkinje fibers** | Subendocardial network in both ventricles | Rapidly distribute impulse to ventricular muscle | 20-40/min |

**Pacemaker Hierarchy**:
- SA node (dominant, fastest rate) → normally suppresses lower pacemakers (overdrive suppression)
- AV junction (latent pacemaker) → if SA node fails
- Purkinje fibers (ventricular escape rhythm) → if SA and AV nodes fail

#### 5.2 Action Potential of Pacemaker Cells (SA Node)

SA node cells do not have a stable resting potential; they exhibit spontaneous diastolic depolarization (pacemaker potential).

**Phases**:

| Phase | Name | Ion Movement | Mechanism |
|-------|------|--------------|-----------|
| **Phase 4** | Spontaneous depolarization (pacemaker potential) | "Funny" current (I<sub>f</sub>) – Na⁺ influx; T-type Ca²⁺ channels open late | Gradual depolarization toward threshold |
| **Phase 0** | Upstroke | L-type Ca²⁺ channels (I<sub>Ca,L</sub>) open | Ca²⁺ influx causes depolarization |
| **Phase 3** | Repolarization | K⁺ channels open (I<sub>K</sub>); Ca²⁺ channels inactivate | K⁺ efflux repolarizes cell |

**I<sub>f</sub> (Funny Current)** :
- Mixed Na⁺/K⁺ inward current activated by hyperpolarization (at end of action potential)
- Modulated by cAMP (sympathetic ↑, parasympathetic ↓)
- Responsible for pacemaker activity

**Autonomic Regulation**:

| Autonomic Input | Effect on SA Node | Mechanism |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Sympathetic (norepinephrine)** | Increases heart rate (positive chronotropy) | β₁ receptors → ↑ cAMP → ↑ I<sub>f</sub>, ↑ I<sub>Ca,L</sub> → steeper Phase 4 slope |
| **Parasympathetic (acetylcholine)** | Decreases heart rate (negative chronotropy) | M₂ receptors → ↓ cAMP (via Gi) → ↓ I<sub>f</sub>, ↑ K⁺ conductance (I<sub>K,ACh</sub>) → hyperpolarization, slower Phase 4 |

#### 5.3 Conduction Velocities

| Tissue | Conduction Velocity (m/s) |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Atrial muscle | 0.3-0.5 |
| Internodal pathways | 1.0 |
| AV node | 0.05 (slowest – accounts for PR interval) |
| Bundle of His | 1.0-1.5 |
| Purkinje fibers | 2.0-4.0 (fastest – ensures rapid ventricular activation) |
| Ventricular muscle | 0.3-0.5 |

**AV Nodal Delay**:
- Due to slow conduction (small fibers, fewer gap junctions, depolarization via Ca²⁺ channels)
- Allows atria to complete contraction before ventricles contract
- Protects ventricles from rapid atrial rates (e.g., atrial fibrillation)

#### 5.4 Electrocardiogram (ECG) Correlation

The ECG records the electrical activity of the heart from body surface. Each wave corresponds to underlying electrical events.

| ECG Wave | Duration | Electrical Event |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| **P wave** | 0.08-0.10 s | Atrial depolarization |
| **PR interval** | 0.12-0.20 s | Atrial depolarization + AV nodal delay (time from SA node to ventricular activation) |
| **QRS complex** | <0.12 s | Ventricular depolarization |
| **ST segment** | Variable | Ventricular depolarization complete; plateau phase (Phase 2) |
| **T wave** | 0.16 s | Ventricular repolarization |
| **QT interval** | 0.35-0.45 s | Total ventricular depolarization + repolarization |

### 6. Heart Sounds

Heart sounds are produced by turbulence and vibration caused by valve closure and blood flow.

#### 6.1 Normal Heart Sounds

| Sound | Timing | Cause | Auscultation Site |
|-------|--------|-------|-------------------|
| **S₁ (first heart sound)** | Beginning of systole (after QRS) | Closure of AV valves (mitral and tricuspid) | Best at apex (mitral) and lower left sternal border (tricuspid) |
| **S₂ (second heart sound)** | End of systole (end of T wave) | Closure of semilunar valves (aortic and pulmonary) | Best at 2nd intercostal space, right (aortic) and left (pulmonary) sternal border |
| **S₃ (third heart sound)** | Early diastole (rapid filling phase) | Rapid ventricular filling; sudden deceleration of blood against ventricular wall | Normal in children, young adults; pathological in heart failure |
| **S₄ (fourth heart sound)** | Late diastole (atrial systole) | Atrial contraction against stiff ventricle (reduced compliance) | Pathological (hypertension, aortic stenosis, cardiomyopathy) |

**Splitting of S₂**:
- **Physiologic splitting**: Inspiration increases venous return to right heart → prolonged RV ejection → pulmonary valve closes later → S₂ split widens (A₂ then P₂)
- **Paradoxical splitting**: Aortic valve closure delayed (left bundle branch block, aortic stenosis) → split heard on expiration
- **Fixed splitting**: Atrial septal defect – no respiratory variation

#### 6.2 Heart Murmurs

Murmurs are prolonged sounds caused by turbulent blood flow through abnormal valves, septal defects, or increased flow.

**Classification**:

| Timing | Valve | Murmur |
|--------|-------|--------|
| **Systolic murmurs** | | |
| | Aortic stenosis | Crescendo-decrescendo (ejection murmur) |
| | Pulmonary stenosis | Ejection murmur |
| | Mitral regurgitation | Holosystolic (blowing) |
| | Tricuspid regurgitation | Holosystolic |
| | Ventricular septal defect | Holosystolic |
| **Diastolic murmurs** | | |
| | Aortic regurgitation | Early diastolic (decrescendo) |
| | Pulmonary regurgitation | Early diastolic |
| | Mitral stenosis | Mid-diastolic (rumbling) with presystolic accentuation |
| | Tricuspid stenosis | Mid-diastolic |
| **Continuous murmurs** | Patent ductus arteriosus | Machinery murmur |

**Grading of Murmurs** (1-6):
- Grade 1: Very faint, heard only with special effort
- Grade 2: Quiet but readily heard
- Grade 3: Moderately loud
- Grade 4: Loud, with palpable thrill
- Grade 5: Very loud, heard with edge of stethoscope
- Grade 6: Extremely loud, heard without stethoscope

### 7. Electrocardiogram (ECG)

#### 7.1 ECG Leads

ECG records electrical activity from different perspectives (leads).

**Standard Limb Leads (Bipolar)** :

| Lead | Positive Electrode | Negative Electrode | View |
|------|-------------------|--------------------|------|
| I | Left arm | Right arm | Lateral wall |
| II | Left leg | Right arm | Inferior wall |
| III | Left leg | Left arm | Inferior wall |

**Augmented Limb Leads (Unipolar)** :

| Lead | Positive Electrode | Reference | View |
|------|-------------------|-----------|------|
| aVR | Right arm | Left arm + left leg | Right side (cavity) |
| aVL | Left arm | Right arm + left leg | Lateral wall |
| aVF | Left leg | Right arm + left arm | Inferior wall |

**Precordial (Chest) Leads**:

| Lead | Position | View |
|------|----------|------|
| V₁ | 4th intercostal space, right sternal border | Septal |
| V₂ | 4th intercostal space, left sternal border | Septal |
| V₃ | Between V₂ and V₄ | Anterior |
| V₄ | 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line | Anterior |
| V₅ | 5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line | Lateral |
| V₆ | 5th intercostal space, midaxillary line | Lateral |

#### 7.2 Normal ECG Waveforms

**P Wave**:
- Duration: <0.12 s
- Amplitude: <0.25 mV
- Represents atrial depolarization
- Biphasic in V₁ (positive then negative)

**PR Interval**:
- Duration: 0.12-0.20 s
- From start of P wave to start of QRS
- Represents AV nodal delay and conduction through His-Purkinje system

**QRS Complex**:
- Duration: <0.12 s
- Represents ventricular depolarization
- Q wave: first negative deflection
- R wave: first positive deflection
- S wave: negative after R wave
- Septal Q waves (small) in lateral leads (I, aVL, V₅-V₆) are normal

**ST Segment**:
- Isoelectric (same level as PR segment)
- Represents early ventricular repolarization (plateau phase)
- Elevation or depression indicates pathology

**T Wave**:
- Asymmetric, upright in leads with positive QRS
- Represents ventricular repolarization
- Amplitude: <10 mm in precordial leads

**QT Interval**:
- Duration varies with heart rate (corrected QT = QTc)
- QTc = QT / √RR (Bazett formula) – normal <0.44 s
- Prolonged QT increases risk of torsade de pointes

**U Wave**:
- Small wave after T wave (sometimes seen)
- Represents Purkinje fiber repolarization (or afterdepolarizations)
- Prominent in hypokalemia

#### 7.3 Relationship of ECG to the Cardiac Cycle

| ECG Wave | Mechanical Event |
|----------|------------------|
| **P wave** | Atrial depolarization → atrial systole (follows P wave by ~0.04 s) |
| **PR segment** | AV nodal delay; atrial contraction completes |
| **QRS complex** | Ventricular depolarization → beginning of ventricular systole |
| **ST segment** | Ventricles depolarized; plateau phase; ejection occurs |
| **T wave** | Ventricular repolarization → end of systole, beginning of diastole |
| **TP segment** | Diastole (ventricular filling) |

### 8. Clinical Significance of Abnormal ECG Patterns

#### 8.1 Rhythm Abnormalities (Arrhythmias)

| Arrhythmia | ECG Features | Mechanism |
|------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Sinus tachycardia** | Normal P waves, rate >100/min | Increased sympathetic tone |
| **Sinus bradycardia** | Normal P waves, rate <60/min | Increased vagal tone; sinus node dysfunction |
| **Sinus arrhythmia** | Phasic variation with respiration | Normal (especially young) |
| **Premature atrial contractions (PAC)** | Early P wave (different morphology); normal QRS | Ectopic atrial focus |
| **Atrial fibrillation** | No P waves; irregularly irregular QRS; fibrillatory baseline | Multiple re-entrant circuits in atria |
| **Atrial flutter** | Sawtooth flutter waves (rate 250-350/min); variable AV block | Re-entry in right atrium |
| **Premature ventricular contractions (PVC)** | Wide QRS (>0.12 s), no preceding P wave; full compensatory pause | Ventricular ectopic focus |
| **Ventricular tachycardia** | Wide QRS ≥3 beats at rate >100/min; may be monomorphic or polymorphic | Re-entry or automatic focus in ventricles |
| **Ventricular fibrillation** | Chaotic, irregular waveform; no identifiable QRS | Multiple re-entrant circuits; lethal |
| **First-degree AV block** | PR interval >0.20 s | Delayed AV conduction |
| **Second-degree AV block (Mobitz I)** | Progressive PR prolongation until dropped QRS (Wenckebach) | AV nodal dysfunction |
| **Second-degree AV block (Mobitz II)** | Intermittent dropped QRS without PR prolongation | Infranodal conduction disease |
| **Third-degree AV block** | Complete AV dissociation; P waves and QRS unrelated; escape rhythm | Complete heart block |

#### 8.2 Myocardial Ischemia and Infarction

| Finding | ECG Pattern | Significance |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| **Ischemia** | ST depression, T wave inversion | Subendocardial ischemia |
| **Injury** | ST elevation (≥1 mm in limb leads, ≥2 mm in precordial leads) | Transmural injury; acute MI |
| **Infarction** | Pathologic Q waves (≥0.04 s, >25% of R wave) | Necrosis; electrically silent area |
| **Non-ST elevation MI (NSTEMI)** | ST depression, T inversion, positive troponin | Subendocardial infarction |

**Localization of MI**:

| Leads with ST Elevation | Affected Wall | Coronary Artery |
|-------------------------|---------------|------------------|
| V₁-V₄ | Anterior | LAD |
| I, aVL, V₅-V₆ | Lateral | LCx or diagonal branches |
| II, III, aVF | Inferior | RCA (80%) or LCx (20%) |
| V₁-V₂ (tall R, ST ↓) | Posterior | RCA or LCx (posterior descending) |
| V₃R, V₄R | Right ventricle | RCA |

#### 8.3 Chamber Hypertrophy and Enlargement

| Condition | ECG Criteria |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Left atrial enlargement (P mitrale)** | Wide, notched P wave in II; biphasic P in V₁ with large negative terminal component |
| **Right atrial enlargement (P pulmonale)** | Tall, peaked P wave in II, III, aVF (>2.5 mm) |
| **Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH)** | Tall R waves in V₅-V₆ + deep S in V₁-V₂; strain pattern (ST depression, T inversion in lateral leads) |
| **Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH)** | Tall R in V₁ (>7 mm) or R/S >1; right axis deviation |

#### 8.4 Electrolyte Abnormalities

| Condition | ECG Changes |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Hyperkalemia** | Tall, peaked T waves; widened QRS; loss of P wave; sine wave → arrest |
| **Hypokalemia** | Flat T waves, prominent U waves; ST depression; prolonged QT |
| **Hypercalcemia** | Short QT |
| **Hypocalcemia** | Prolonged QT (due to prolonged ST segment) |

#### 8.5 Drug Effects

| Drug | ECG Changes |
|------|-------------|
| **Digoxin** | Scooped ST depression (digitalis effect); prolonged PR; short QT; arrhythmias in toxicity |
| **Quinidine, procainamide** | Prolonged QT, widened QRS (Na⁺ channel block) |
| **Amiodarone** | Prolonged QT |
| **Tricyclic antidepressants** | Widened QRS, prolonged QT (Na⁺ channel block) |

### 9. Key Definitions

| Term | Definition | Normal Value |
|------|------------|--------------|
| **Systole** | Phase of ventricular contraction | - |
| **Diastole** | Phase of ventricular relaxation and filling | - |
| **Stroke volume (SV)** | Volume of blood ejected per beat by each ventricle | 60-100 mL/beat |
| **Cardiac output (CO)** | Volume of blood pumped per minute by each ventricle (CO = HR × SV) | 4-8 L/min |
| **Cardiac index (CI)** | Cardiac output normalized for body surface area (CI = CO / BSA) | 2.5-4.0 L/min/m² |
| **Ejection fraction (EF)** | Fraction of end-diastolic volume ejected (EF = SV / EDV) | 55-70% |
| **Preload** | Ventricular wall stress at end-diastole; determined by end-diastolic volume (EDV) | - |
| **Afterload** | Ventricular wall stress during systole; determined by arterial pressure and ventricular size | - |
| **Contractility (inotropy)** | Intrinsic strength of ventricular contraction independent of preload and afterload | - |
| **End-diastolic volume (EDV)** | Volume in ventricle at end of diastole (just before systole) | ~120 mL |
| **End-systolic volume (ESV)** | Volume remaining in ventricle after ejection | ~50 mL |

**Frank-Starling Mechanism**:
- Increased preload (EDV) → increased force of contraction → increased SV
- Intrinsic property of cardiac muscle (length-tension relationship)
- Ensures balance between right and left ventricular output
