## Unit 1: Basic Cell Functions

### Introduction to Cell Physiology

Cell physiology is the study of the functions and activities of cells, the fundamental units of life. Understanding basic cell functions is essential for comprehending how organs and systems operate in health and disease. This unit covers the structural organization of cells, the composition of body fluid compartments, mechanisms of molecular transport across cell membranes, and fundamental cellular processes including the cell cycle and cell death.

---

### 1. Physical Structure of the Cell

#### 1.1 Overview of Cellular Organization

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of the human body. Approximately 30-40 trillion cells exist in the human body, each performing specialized functions while sharing common structural features.

**Two Major Classes of Cells in the Human Body**:

| Class | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Sex cells (gametes)** | Haploid cells (23 chromosomes) | Spermatozoa, ova |
| **Somatic cells** | Diploid cells (46 chromosomes, 23 pairs) | All other body cells |

**General Structure of a Typical Cell**:

All cells contain three major components:
1. **Cell membrane (plasma membrane)** : Outer boundary separating intracellular from extracellular fluid
2. **Cytoplasm and organelles**: Intracellular structures with specific functions
3. **Nucleus**: Contains genetic material (except red blood cells which lack nuclei)

#### 1.2 The Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

The cell membrane is a thin, pliable, elastic structure approximately 7.5-10 nm thick. It serves as a selective barrier that regulates the movement of substances in and out of the cell.

**Structure: Fluid Mosaic Model (Singer and Nicolson, 1972)** :

| Component | Composition | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Lipid bilayer** | Phospholipids (amphipathic: hydrophilic heads, hydrophobic tails), cholesterol, glycolipids | Provides structural barrier; maintains fluidity |
| **Integral proteins** | Proteins embedded within the lipid bilayer | Transport (channels, carriers), receptors, enzymes |
| **Peripheral proteins** | Proteins attached to membrane surface (inner or outer) | Cell signaling, structural support, enzymes |
| **Glycocalyx** | Carbohydrate chains attached to proteins (glycoproteins) and lipids (glycolipids) | Cell recognition, adhesion, immune protection |

**Lipid Composition**:
- **Phospholipids**: Form the basic bilayer structure
  - Hydrophilic heads (phosphate-containing) face aqueous environments (extracellular and intracellular fluid)
  - Hydrophobic tails (fatty acids) face inward, away from water
- **Cholesterol**: Inserted between phospholipids; regulates membrane fluidity and stability
- **Glycolipids**: Lipids with carbohydrate chains; contribute to cell recognition

**Membrane Proteins Functions**:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Ion channels** | Pores that allow specific ions to pass through |
| **Carriers/transporters** | Bind substances and transport them across membrane |
| **Pumps** | Active transporters that move substances against concentration gradient (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase) |
| **Receptors** | Bind signaling molecules (hormones, neurotransmitters) |
| **Enzymes** | Catalyze reactions at membrane surface |
| **Structural proteins** | Attach to cytoskeleton for cell shape and stability |
| **Cell adhesion molecules** | Connect cells to each other or to extracellular matrix |

**Membrane Asymmetry**:
- Outer leaflet faces extracellular fluid
- Inner leaflet faces cytoplasm
- Asymmetric distribution of lipids and proteins reflects functional specialization

#### 1.3 Cytoplasm and Organelles

The cytoplasm is the gel-like substance filling the cell, consisting of cytosol (fluid portion) and organelles.

**Cytosol**:
- Composed of water, ions, metabolites, and proteins
- Site of many metabolic reactions (glycolysis, protein synthesis)
- Contains cytoskeleton for structural support

**Membrane-Bound Organelles**:

| Organelle | Structure | Functions |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Nucleus** | Double membrane (nuclear envelope) with pores; contains chromatin (DNA + histones) and nucleolus | Stores genetic material; controls cell activities; rRNA synthesis in nucleolus |
| **Endoplasmic reticulum (ER)** | Network of membranous tubules and sacs (cisternae) | |
| - Rough ER | Ribosomes attached | Protein synthesis (secretory and membrane proteins); protein folding |
| - Smooth ER | No ribosomes | Lipid synthesis; detoxification; calcium storage; carbohydrate metabolism |
| **Golgi apparatus** | Stack of flattened membranous sacs (cisternae) | Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins; synthesizes lysosomes; produces secretory vesicles |
| **Lysosomes** | Membrane-bound vesicles containing hydrolytic enzymes | Intracellular digestion (phagocytosis, autophagy); degrade worn-out organelles |
| **Peroxisomes** | Membrane-bound vesicles containing oxidative enzymes | Fatty acid oxidation; detoxification of hydrogen peroxide (catalase) |
| **Mitochondria** | Double membrane; inner membrane folds (cristae); own DNA | ATP production (oxidative phosphorylation); calcium regulation; apoptosis regulation |
| **Secretory vesicles** | Membrane-bound vesicles | Store and release substances (hormones, neurotransmitters, enzymes) |

**Non-Membrane-Bound Organelles**:

| Organelle | Structure | Functions |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Ribosomes** | Composed of rRNA and proteins (large and small subunits) | Protein synthesis (translation); free in cytoplasm or bound to RER |
| **Proteasomes** | Protein complexes | Degrade ubiquitin-tagged proteins |
| **Cytoskeleton** | Network of protein filaments | Cell shape, support, movement, division |
| - Microfilaments | Actin filaments | Cell movement, muscle contraction, cytokinesis |
| - Intermediate filaments | Various proteins (keratin, vimentin, lamin) | Structural support, nuclear envelope integrity |
| - Microtubules | Tubulin polymers | Cell shape, intracellular transport, mitotic spindle, cilia/flagella |
| **Centrioles** | Paired cylindrical structures (microtubule-based) | Organize mitotic spindle during cell division |

#### 1.4 Nucleus

The nucleus is the control center of the cell, containing genetic material and directing cellular activities.

| Component | Structure | Function |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Nuclear envelope** | Double membrane (inner and outer) with nuclear pores | Separates nucleus from cytoplasm; regulates transport |
| **Nuclear pores** | Large protein complexes (nuclear pore complexes) | Selective transport of molecules (RNA, proteins) |
| **Nucleolus** | Dense region without membrane | rRNA synthesis; ribosome subunit assembly |
| **Chromatin** | DNA + histone proteins (nucleosomes) | Genetic information; transcription regulation |
| - Heterochromatin | Condensed, transcriptionally inactive | Gene silencing |
| - Euchromatin | Loosely packed, transcriptionally active | Active gene expression |
| **Nuclear matrix** | Protein scaffold | Structural support |

### 2. Extracellular Fluid and Intracellular Fluid

Body fluids are distributed into two major compartments: intracellular fluid (ICF) and extracellular fluid (ECF). The composition and regulation of these compartments are critical for cellular function and homeostasis.

#### 2.1 Body Fluid Compartments

**Total Body Water (TBW)** :
- Accounts for approximately 50-60% of body weight in adults (varies with age, sex, body fat)
- Lean individuals have higher percentage; obese individuals have lower percentage
- Newborns: ~75% water; elderly: ~45-50%

**Distribution of Body Water**:

| Compartment | Percentage of TBW | Volume in 70 kg adult (L) | Location |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------------------|----------|
| **Intracellular fluid (ICF)** | 60-65% | ~25 L | Inside cells |
| **Extracellular fluid (ECF)** | 35-40% | ~15 L | Outside cells |
| - Interstitial fluid | 75% of ECF | ~11.5 L | Between cells, not in blood vessels |
| - Plasma | 25% of ECF | ~3.5 L | Fluid portion of blood |
| - Transcellular fluid | Small fraction | ~1 L | Cerebrospinal fluid, synovial fluid, pleural/pericardial fluid, digestive secretions |

#### 2.2 Composition of ICF and ECF

The two compartments have markedly different ionic compositions, maintained by selective permeability of cell membranes and active transport mechanisms.

**Ionic Composition (approximate concentrations)** :

| Substance | Extracellular Fluid (ECF) | Intracellular Fluid (ICF) |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Sodium (Na⁺)** | 135-145 mEq/L (high) | 10-14 mEq/L (low) |
| **Potassium (K⁺)** | 3.5-5.0 mEq/L (low) | 140-150 mEq/L (high) |
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | 2.1-2.6 mEq/L (ionized ~1.2) | Very low (~0.0001 mEq/L) |
| **Magnesium (Mg²⁺)** | 1.5-2.0 mEq/L | ~15-20 mEq/L |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | 100-110 mEq/L (high) | 5-15 mEq/L (low) |
| **Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)** | 24-30 mEq/L | 8-12 mEq/L |
| **Phosphate (HPO₄²⁻/H₂PO₄⁻)** | 1-2 mEq/L | 40-50 mEq/L |
| **Proteins** | Low (plasma ~16 mEq/L; interstitial lower) | High (~40 mEq/L) |
| **Glucose** | ~90 mg/dL | Variable |
| **pH** | 7.35-7.45 | ~7.2 (slightly more acidic) |

**Key Differences**:
- **ECF**: High Na⁺, high Cl⁻, high HCO₃⁻; low K⁺, low Mg²⁺, low phosphate
- **ICF**: High K⁺, high Mg²⁺, high phosphate; low Na⁺, low Cl⁻, low HCO₃⁻
- **Proteins**: Higher in ICF (anions at physiological pH) and plasma (vs. interstitial fluid)

**Maintenance of Ionic Gradients**:
- **Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (sodium-potassium pump)** : Active transport pump in cell membrane
  - Pumps 3 Na⁺ out of cell and 2 K⁺ into cell per ATP hydrolyzed
  - Maintains high intracellular K⁺ and low intracellular Na⁺
  - Essential for resting membrane potential, cell volume regulation, secondary active transport

#### 2.3 Functions of ICF and ECF

**Intracellular Fluid (ICF)** :
- Site of most metabolic reactions (glycolysis, protein synthesis, etc.)
- Contains enzymes, organelles, and structural elements
- Ionic composition optimal for intracellular enzyme function

**Extracellular Fluid (ECF) – "Internal Environment"** :
- Provides stable environment for cells (Claude Bernard: "milieu intérieur")
- **Interstitial fluid**: Bathes cells directly; supplies nutrients, removes wastes
- **Plasma**: Transports substances throughout body (nutrients, wastes, hormones, gases)
- **Transcellular fluids**: Specialized functions (lubrication, protection, ion regulation)

**Homeostatic Regulation**:
- Kidneys regulate volume and composition of ECF (and thus ICF indirectly)
- Cardiovascular system ensures adequate circulation
- Hormonal systems (ADH, aldosterone, ANP) fine-tune fluid and electrolyte balance

### 3. Movement of Molecules Across Cell Membranes

The cell membrane is selectively permeable, allowing some substances to pass while restricting others. Transport mechanisms are classified based on energy requirement and direction relative to concentration gradients.

#### 3.1 Overview of Transport Mechanisms

| Transport Type | Energy Required | Direction Relative to Gradient | Examples |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------------------------|----------|
| **Passive transport** | No | Down gradient (high → low) | Diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion |
| **Active transport** | Yes (ATP) | Against gradient (low → high) | Na⁺/K⁺ pump, Ca²⁺ pump |
| **Secondary active transport** | Yes (indirect, via ion gradient) | Against gradient (coupled to ion moving down gradient) | Co-transport, counter-transport |
| **Vesicular transport** | Yes (ATP) | Bulk transport in/out | Endocytosis, exocytosis |

#### 3.2 Diffusion

Diffusion is the net movement of molecules from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration due to random thermal motion.

**Factors Affecting Rate of Diffusion**:

| Factor | Relationship | Explanation |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Concentration gradient** | Directly proportional | Greater difference → faster diffusion |
| **Temperature** | Directly proportional | Higher temperature → faster molecular motion |
| **Molecular size** | Inversely proportional | Smaller molecules diffuse faster |
| **Membrane surface area** | Directly proportional | Larger area → more diffusion |
| **Membrane thickness** | Inversely proportional | Thicker membrane → slower diffusion |
| **Distance** | Inversely proportional | Longer distance → slower diffusion |
| **Diffusion coefficient (D)** | Directly proportional | Depends on molecule and medium |

**Fick's Law of Diffusion**:
J = -D × A × (ΔC/Δx)

Where:
- J = diffusion rate (flux)
- D = diffusion coefficient
- A = surface area
- ΔC = concentration difference
- Δx = diffusion distance

**Types of Diffusion Across Membranes**:

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Simple diffusion through lipid bilayer** | Lipid-soluble (nonpolar) molecules dissolve in lipid bilayer and diffuse through | O₂, CO₂, N₂, fatty acids, steroid hormones, ethanol |
| **Simple diffusion through protein channels** | Small, water-soluble (polar) molecules or ions pass through aqueous pores | Ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺) through ion channels |
| **Facilitated diffusion** | Carrier proteins bind substances and undergo conformational change to transport them; saturable, stereospecific | Glucose (GLUT transporters), amino acids |

**Characteristics of Facilitated Diffusion**:
- **Specificity**: Transporters bind specific molecules
- **Saturation**: Rate reaches maximum (Vmax) when all carriers occupied
- **Competition**: Similar molecules compete for same carrier
- **No energy requirement**: Movement down concentration gradient

**Ion Channels**:
- Proteins forming pores for ion passage
- **Selectivity**: Allow specific ions based on size and charge
- **Gating**: Channels open/close in response to stimuli
  - **Voltage-gated**: Respond to membrane potential (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺ channels)
  - **Ligand-gated**: Respond to binding of chemical (neurotransmitter receptors)
  - **Mechanically-gated**: Respond to stretch or pressure

#### 3.3 Osmosis

Osmosis is the net diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from an area of higher water concentration (lower solute concentration) to an area of lower water concentration (higher solute concentration).

**Osmotic Pressure**:
- The pressure required to prevent net movement of water across a semipermeable membrane
- Proportional to the concentration of osmotically active particles (osmolality)

**Osmolality and Osmolarity**:

| Term | Definition | Units |
|------|------------|-------|
| **Osmole (Osm)** | 1 mole of osmotically active particles | mol |
| **Osmolality** | Osmoles per kilogram of solvent | Osm/kg |
| **Osmolarity** | Osmoles per liter of solution | Osm/L |

**Calculating Osmolarity**:
Osmolarity = Molarity × Number of dissociable particles per molecule

| Substance | Molarity | Dissociation | Osmolarity |
|-----------|----------|--------------|------------|
| Glucose | 1 M | None (1 particle) | 1 Osm/L |
| NaCl | 1 M | 2 particles (Na⁺ + Cl⁻) | 2 Osm/L |
| CaCl₂ | 1 M | 3 particles (Ca²⁺ + 2 Cl⁻) | 3 Osm/L |

**Tonicity**:
Tonicity describes the effect of a solution on cell volume based on the concentration of non-penetrating solutes.

| Tonicity | Effect on Cell | Examples |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| **Isotonic** | No net water movement; cell volume unchanged | 0.9% NaCl, 5% dextrose (initially isotonic, but dextrose metabolized) |
| **Hypotonic** | Water enters cell; cell swells and may burst (hemolysis) | 0.45% NaCl, distilled water |
| **Hypertonic** | Water leaves cell; cell shrinks (crenation) | 3% NaCl, concentrated dextrose solutions |

**Osmotic Equilibrium**:
- Water moves rapidly across cell membranes to equalize osmotic pressure
- Cells regulate volume by adjusting intracellular solute content (via transporters)
- Kidneys regulate overall body fluid osmolality

#### 3.4 Active Transport

Active transport moves substances against their concentration or electrochemical gradient, requiring energy (ATP) and specific carrier proteins.

**Primary Active Transport**:
Direct use of ATP to transport substances.

**Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (Sodium-Potassium Pump)** :
- Location: Plasma membrane of all cells
- Stoichiometry: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP
- Functions:
  - Maintains low intracellular Na⁺, high intracellular K⁺
  - Establishes resting membrane potential
  - Drives secondary active transport
  - Regulates cell volume
  - Provides gradient for nerve and muscle excitability

**Ca²⁺ ATPase (Calcium Pump)** :
- Location: Plasma membrane (PMCA) and sarcoplasmic reticulum (SERCA)
- Function: Maintains very low intracellular Ca²⁺ (essential for signaling)

**H⁺ ATPase (Proton Pump)** :
- Location: Gastric parietal cells (H⁺/K⁺ ATPase), renal tubular cells, lysosomes
- Function: Acid secretion, pH regulation

**H⁺/K⁺ ATPase** :
- Location: Gastric parietal cells
- Function: Exchanges H⁺ for K⁺; responsible for gastric acid secretion (target of proton pump inhibitors)

#### 3.5 Secondary Active Transport (Co-transport and Counter-transport)

Secondary active transport uses the energy stored in an ion gradient (usually Na⁺ gradient established by Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase) to transport another substance against its gradient.

**Co-transport (Symport)** :
- Both substances move in the same direction across membrane
- Driven by Na⁺ moving down its gradient into cell

| Transporter | Location | Function |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **SGLT (Sodium-glucose co-transporter)** | Intestinal epithelial cells, renal proximal tubule | Glucose absorption against gradient (driven by Na⁺ gradient) |
| **Na⁺-amino acid co-transporters** | Intestine, kidney | Amino acid absorption |
| **Na⁺-I⁻ symporter (NIS)** | Thyroid follicular cells | Iodide uptake for thyroid hormone synthesis |
| **Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ symporter (NKCC)** | Thick ascending limb of loop of Henle | NaCl reabsorption; target of loop diuretics |

**Counter-transport (Antiport, Exchange)** :
- Substances move in opposite directions
- One moves down gradient, driving the other against its gradient

| Transporter | Location | Function |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX)** | Cardiac muscle, neurons | Exports Ca²⁺ (using Na⁺ gradient); contributes to relaxation |
| **Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE)** | Proximal tubule, many cells | Regulates intracellular pH; Na⁺ in, H⁺ out |
| **Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger (anion exchanger, AE)** | Red blood cells, kidney | Bicarbonate transport; involved in CO₂ transport and pH regulation |

#### 3.6 Comparison of Transport Mechanisms

| Property | Simple Diffusion | Facilitated Diffusion | Primary Active Transport | Secondary Active Transport |
|----------|------------------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Energy required** | No | No | Yes (ATP) | Yes (indirect, via ion gradient) |
| **Carrier protein** | No (lipid-soluble) or channel | Yes (carrier or channel) | Yes (pump) | Yes (co-transporter) |
| **Direction** | Down gradient | Down gradient | Against gradient | Against gradient |
| **Saturation** | No | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Competition** | No | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Specificity** | Low (channels selective) | High | High | High |
| **Examples** | O₂, CO₂, lipids | Glucose (GLUT), ions via channels | Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase, Ca²⁺ ATPase | SGLT, Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger |

### 4. Endocytosis and Exocytosis

Vesicular transport moves large molecules, particles, or bulk quantities across the membrane via membrane-bound vesicles.

#### 4.1 Exocytosis

Exocytosis is the process by which intracellular vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane and release their contents to the extracellular space.

**Steps of Exocytosis**:
1. **Vesicle formation**: Vesicles bud from Golgi apparatus or endosomes
2. **Transport**: Vesicles move to plasma membrane along microtubules (via motor proteins)
3. **Tethering and docking**: Vesicle interacts with specific membrane proteins
4. **Fusion**: Vesicle membrane fuses with plasma membrane (mediated by SNARE proteins)
5. **Release**: Contents expelled to extracellular space
6. **Membrane recycling**: Vesicle membrane components retrieved via endocytosis

**Functions of Exocytosis**:
- **Secretion**: Hormones (insulin), neurotransmitters, digestive enzymes, mucus
- **Membrane addition**: Adds lipids and proteins to plasma membrane (cell growth, repair)
- **Receptor recycling**: Returns receptors to surface after internalization

**Regulation**:
- Constitutive exocytosis: Continuous, unregulated (e.g., membrane proteins, extracellular matrix)
- Regulated exocytosis: Triggered by signal (Ca²⁺ influx in neurons, hormones in endocrine cells)

**SNARE Hypothesis**:
- **v-SNARE** (on vesicle): Vesicle-associated membrane protein (VAMP)
- **t-SNARE** (on target membrane): Syntaxin, SNAP-25
- Specific pairing ensures correct vesicle targeting

#### 4.2 Endocytosis

Endocytosis is the process by which cells internalize extracellular material by invaginating the plasma membrane to form vesicles.

**Types of Endocytosis**:

| Type | Description | Vesicle Size | Mechanism | Examples |
|------|-------------|--------------|-----------|----------|
| **Phagocytosis** | "Cell eating"; uptake of large particles | >250 nm | Actin-driven membrane extension; receptor-mediated | Macrophages, neutrophils engulf bacteria, debris |
| **Pinocytosis** | "Cell drinking"; uptake of fluid and dissolved solutes | ~100 nm | Constitutive; non-specific | Most cells; nutrient uptake |
| **Receptor-mediated endocytosis** | Specific uptake of ligands bound to receptors | ~100-150 nm | Clathrin-coated pits; ligand-receptor complexes | LDL (cholesterol), transferrin (iron), hormones, viruses |

**A. Phagocytosis**:
- Performed primarily by specialized cells (macrophages, neutrophils, dendritic cells)
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognition: Receptors bind target (opsonins like antibodies, complement)
  2. Engulfment: Pseudopods extend around particle
  3. Phagosome formation: Membrane encloses particle
  4. Phagosome-lysosome fusion: Forms phagolysosome
  5. Digestion: Hydrolytic enzymes degrade contents
  6. Exocytosis of debris
- **Function**: Defense against pathogens, clearance of apoptotic cells, tissue remodeling

**B. Pinocytosis**:
- Occurs in most cells continuously
- Non-specific uptake of extracellular fluid and dissolved solutes
- Membrane invaginates, pinches off, forms vesicles (caveolae or clathrin-independent)
- Provides nutrients, samples environment

**C. Receptor-Mediated Endocytosis (Clathrin-Mediated Endocytosis)** :
- Highly selective and efficient uptake of specific macromolecules
- **Steps**:
  1. **Ligand binding**: Ligands bind to specific cell surface receptors
  2. **Clustering**: Receptors cluster in clathrin-coated pits
  3. **Invagination**: Coated pit invaginates inward
  4. **Vesicle formation**: Dynamin pinches off vesicle (coated vesicle)
  5. **Uncoating**: Clathrin coat disassembles (recycled)
  6. **Early endosome**: Vesicle fuses with early endosome
  7. **Sorting**: Ligands and receptors sorted to different destinations

**Fates of Internalized Material**:

| Pathway | Destination | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Receptor recycling** | Receptors return to membrane | LDL receptor, transferrin receptor |
| **Ligand degradation** | Ligand to lysosome | LDL (cholesterol released) |
| **Transcytosis** | Transport across cell | Antibodies across epithelial cells (neonatal Fc receptor) |
| **Signaling** | Continued signaling from endosomes | Growth factor receptors |

**Clinical Relevance**:
- **Familial hypercholesterolemia**: Defective LDL receptors or internalization → high LDL cholesterol → premature atherosclerosis
- **Pathogen entry**: Many viruses (influenza, HIV) use receptor-mediated endocytosis to enter cells
- **Drug delivery**: Nanoparticles designed for endocytic uptake

### 5. Phagocytosis and Pinocytosis (Detailed)

#### 5.1 Phagocytosis (Cell Eating)

**Definition**: Phagocytosis is the process by which cells engulf large solid particles (>0.5 μm) such as bacteria, dead cells, or debris.

**Cells Capable of Phagocytosis (Professional Phagocytes)** :
- **Macrophages** (tissue) and monocytes (blood)
- **Neutrophils** (polymorphonuclear leukocytes)
- **Dendritic cells**
- **Eosinophils** (for parasitic infections)

**Mechanism in Detail**:

| Step | Description | Key Molecules |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Chemotaxis** | Phagocytes migrate toward chemical signals from pathogens, damaged cells | Chemokines, bacterial products (fMLP) |
| **Recognition and attachment** | Phagocyte receptors bind targets directly or via opsonins | Mannose receptors, scavenger receptors, complement receptors, Fc receptors (bind antibodies) |
| **Opsonization** | Coating of target with molecules that enhance phagocytosis | Antibodies (IgG), complement (C3b), lectins |
| **Signal transduction** | Receptor clustering triggers intracellular signals | Tyrosine kinases, Rho family GTPases |
| **Pseudopod extension** | Actin polymerization pushes membrane around particle | Actin filaments, Arp2/3 complex |
| **Phagosome formation** | Membrane encloses particle, pinches off | Dynamin, myosin |
| **Phagosome maturation** | Phagosome acidifies, fuses with endosomes and lysosomes | V-ATPase (proton pump), Rab GTPases |
| **Phagolysosome formation** | Fusion with lysosome exposes contents to hydrolytic enzymes | Cathepsins, lysozyme, reactive oxygen species |
| **Digestion and destruction** | Enzymes degrade pathogen; ROS kill microbes | NADPH oxidase (respiratory burst), myeloperoxidase |
| **Exocytosis** | Undigested remnants expelled | |

**Respiratory Burst (Oxidative Burst)** :
- Rapid consumption of oxygen by phagocytes during phagocytosis
- NADPH oxidase produces superoxide radical (O₂⁻)
- Superoxide converted to hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), hypochlorous acid (HOCl, by myeloperoxidase)
- Kills ingested microbes

**Clinical Correlation: Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD)** :
- Genetic defect in NADPH oxidase
- Phagocytes cannot produce reactive oxygen species
- Recurrent bacterial and fungal infections; granuloma formation

#### 5.2 Pinocytosis (Cell Drinking)

**Definition**: Pinocytosis is the non-specific uptake of extracellular fluid and dissolved solutes by small vesicles. It occurs constitutively in most cells.

**Types of Pinocytosis**:

| Type | Description | Vesicle | Mechanism |
|------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Macropinocytosis** | Large, irregular vesicles formed from membrane ruffles | >1 μm | Actin-driven; growth factor stimulated |
| **Clathrin-mediated endocytosis** | Small, uniform vesicles; receptor-mediated or fluid-phase | ~100 nm | Clathrin coat; constitutive or regulated |
| **Caveolae-mediated endocytosis** | Flask-shaped invaginations rich in caveolin | ~50-80 nm | Caveolin; lipid raft associated |
| **Clathrin-independent, caveolin-independent** | Various pathways | Variable | Less characterized |

**Functions of Pinocytosis**:
- **Nutrient uptake**: Absorbs dissolved nutrients
- **Membrane recycling**: Retrieves membrane added by exocytosis
- **Sampling environment**: Allows cells to monitor extracellular milieu
- **Antigen presentation**: Dendritic cells sample antigens via pinocytosis for presentation to T cells
- **Signal transduction**: Some receptors signal from endosomes

### 6. Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the series of events that cells undergo to grow and divide. It is tightly regulated to ensure accurate DNA replication and chromosome segregation.

#### 6.1 Phases of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is divided into interphase (cell growth and DNA replication) and mitotic phase (cell division).

**Interphase (90% of cell cycle)** :

| Phase | Duration | Key Events | Checkpoints |
|-------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **G₁ (Gap 1)** | Variable (hours to days) | Cell growth; protein synthesis; organelle duplication; preparation for DNA synthesis | G₁/S checkpoint (restriction point) – commits cell to division |
| **S (Synthesis)** | 6-8 hours | DNA replication (each chromosome duplicated); histone synthesis | Monitors DNA replication fidelity |
| **G₂ (Gap 2)** | 2-6 hours | Continued growth; preparation for mitosis; synthesis of microtubules, M-phase proteins | G₂/M checkpoint – checks DNA damage, completion of replication |

**M Phase (Mitosis and Cytokinesis)** :

| Phase | Duration | Key Events |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Mitosis** | ~1 hour | Division of nucleus |
| - Prophase | | Chromosomes condense; nuclear envelope breaks down; mitotic spindle forms |
| - Metaphase | | Chromosomes align at metaphase plate |
| - Anaphase | | Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles |
| - Telophase | | Chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelope reforms |
| **Cytokinesis** | Concurrent with telophase | Division of cytoplasm; contractile ring (actin, myosin) pinches cell into two |

**G₀ Phase (Quiescence)** :
- Cells exit cell cycle after mitosis or enter from G₁
- May remain quiescent temporarily or permanently (terminally differentiated cells)
- Can re-enter cycle if stimulated (e.g., lymphocytes, hepatocytes)
- Examples: Neurons, skeletal muscle cells are permanently in G₀ (do not divide)

#### 6.2 Cell Cycle Regulation

**Key Regulatory Molecules**:

| Molecule | Function |
|----------|----------|
| **Cyclins** | Regulatory proteins whose concentrations oscillate during cell cycle |
| **Cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs)** | Enzymes that phosphorylate target proteins when bound to cyclins |
| **CDK inhibitors (CKIs)** | Inhibit CDK activity (p16, p21, p27) |
| **Retinoblastoma protein (Rb)** | Tumor suppressor; binds E2F transcription factor, blocks S phase entry |
| **p53** | Tumor suppressor; transcription factor; arrests cell cycle in response to DNA damage |

**Cell Cycle Checkpoints**:

| Checkpoint | Location | Monitors | Mechanism if Problem Detected |
|------------|----------|----------|------------------------------|
| **G₁/S checkpoint** | End of G₁ | Cell size, nutrients, DNA damage, growth signals | Arrest in G₁; may enter G₀ |
| **S phase checkpoint** | During S phase | DNA replication fidelity, DNA damage | Slow replication; repair |
| **G₂/M checkpoint** | End of G₂ | DNA damage, completion of DNA replication | Arrest at G₂/M; prevent mitosis |
| **Spindle checkpoint (M phase)** | Metaphase | Chromosome attachment to spindle | Delay anaphase until all chromosomes attached |

**Regulation by Phosphorylation**:
- CDKs phosphorylate target proteins (e.g., Rb, nuclear lamins, condensins)
- Phosphorylation inactivates Rb, releasing E2F → transcription of S phase genes

**Regulation by Proteolysis**:
- Cyclins degraded by ubiquitin-proteasome pathway
- Anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C) triggers destruction of securin (allows sister chromatid separation) and cyclin B (exit from mitosis)

#### 6.3 Clinical Significance of Cell Cycle

| Application | Relevance |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Cancer** | Uncontrolled cell division due to mutations in cell cycle regulators (p53, Rb, cyclins, CDKs) |
| **Chemotherapy** | Drugs target rapidly dividing cells (e.g., methotrexate inhibits DNA synthesis; taxanes block mitosis) |
| **Tissue regeneration** | Understanding cell cycle helps in wound healing, stem cell therapy |
| **Teratogenesis** | Disruption of cell cycle during fetal development causes birth defects |

### 7. Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death)

Apoptosis is a genetically controlled, energy-dependent process of cell death that eliminates unwanted or damaged cells without inflammation. It is essential for normal development, tissue homeostasis, and immune function.

#### 7.1 Comparison: Apoptosis vs. Necrosis

| Feature | Apoptosis | Necrosis |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Cause** | Physiological or pathological stimuli (programmed) | Pathological injury (hypoxia, toxins, trauma) |
| **Energy requirement** | ATP-dependent (active process) | ATP-independent (passive) |
| **Cell size** | Shrinks (pyknosis) | Swells (oncosis) |
| **Membrane integrity** | Maintained until late | Lost early |
| **Organelles** | Intact initially | Swollen, disrupted |
| **DNA fragmentation** | Internucleosomal (ladder pattern on gel) | Random degradation (smear) |
| **Nuclear changes** | Chromatin condensation, nuclear fragmentation | Karyolysis, pyknosis, karyorrhexis |
| **Inflammation** | None (phagocytes remove apoptotic bodies) | Prominent (release of cellular contents) |
| **Tissue appearance** | Individual cells affected | Groups of contiguous cells affected |
| **Physiological role** | Development, homeostasis, immune regulation | Pathological cell death |

#### 7.2 Morphological Changes in Apoptosis

1. **Cell shrinkage**: Cytoplasm condenses, organelles compact
2. **Chromatin condensation**: Nucleus becomes pyknotic; chromatin aggregates under nuclear membrane
3. **Nuclear fragmentation**: Nucleus breaks into fragments (karyorrhexis)
4. **Apoptotic body formation**: Cell membrane buds, forming membrane-bound fragments containing organelles and nuclear material
5. **Phagocytosis**: Apoptotic bodies rapidly engulfed by neighboring cells or macrophages (no inflammation)

#### 7.3 Molecular Mechanisms of Apoptosis

Two main pathways lead to apoptosis: the extrinsic (death receptor) pathway and the intrinsic (mitochondrial) pathway. Both converge on activation of **caspases** (cysteine-aspartic proteases), which execute cell death.

**Caspases**:
- **Initiator caspases**: Caspase-2, -8, -9, -10 (activate executioner caspases)
- **Executioner caspases**: Caspase-3, -6, -7 (cleave cellular proteins, leading to death)
- **Inflammatory caspases**: Caspase-1, -4, -5, -11 (involved in inflammation, not apoptosis)

**A. Extrinsic (Death Receptor) Pathway**:

| Step | Description | Key Molecules |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Ligand binds death receptor on cell surface | FasL (Fas ligand) binds Fas (CD95); TNF binds TNFR1; TRAIL binds DR4/DR5 |
| 2 | Receptor clustering and adaptor protein recruitment | FADD (Fas-associated death domain) binds death domain of receptor |
| 3 | Formation of DISC (death-inducing signaling complex) | FADD recruits procaspase-8 via death effector domains (DED) |
| 4 | Caspase-8 activation | Procaspase-8 cleaved to active caspase-8 |
| 5 | Executioner caspase activation | Caspase-8 activates caspase-3, -6, -7 |
| 6 | Amplification (in some cells, Type II) | Caspase-8 cleaves Bid to tBid, linking to mitochondrial pathway |

**B. Intrinsic (Mitochondrial) Pathway**:

| Step | Description | Key Molecules |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Cellular stress signals: DNA damage, oxidative stress, growth factor withdrawal, hypoxia, toxins | Triggers pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family proteins |
| 2 | Mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP) | Bax, Bak oligomerize and form pores in outer mitochondrial membrane |
| 3 | Release of pro-apoptotic proteins from intermembrane space | Cytochrome c, Smac/DIABLO, Omi/HtrA2, AIF, EndoG |
| 4 | Formation of apoptosome | Cytochrome c + Apaf-1 + dATP/ATP → recruits procaspase-9 |
| 5 | Caspase-9 activation | Initiator caspase activates executioner caspases |
| 6 | Inhibition of IAPs (inhibitor of apoptosis proteins) | Smac/DIABLO neutralizes IAPs, promoting apoptosis |

**Bcl-2 Family Regulation**:

| Family Members | Function | Examples |
|----------------|----------|----------|
| **Anti-apoptotic** | Inhibit MOMP | Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, Mcl-1 |
| **Pro-apoptotic (effector)** | Promote MOMP (form pores) | Bax, Bak |
| **Pro-apoptotic (BH3-only)** | Activate effectors or inhibit anti-apoptotic proteins | Bid, Bad, Bim, Puma, Noxa |

**C. Perforin/Granzyme Pathway** (Cytotoxic T lymphocytes and NK cells):
- Perforin forms pores in target cell membrane
- Granzymes enter through pores and activate caspases (granzyme B activates caspase-3)

#### 7.4 Phagocytic Clearance of Apoptotic Cells

- Apoptotic cells display "eat-me" signals (e.g., phosphatidylserine externalized from inner to outer leaflet)
- Phagocytes have receptors recognizing these signals (e.g., MerTK, integrins, scavenger receptors)
- Engulfment via efferocytosis (specialized phagocytosis of apoptotic cells)
- Anti-inflammatory cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10) released to prevent inflammation

#### 7.5 Physiological and Pathological Roles of Apoptosis

| Role | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Embryonic development** | Removal of interdigital webs; formation of neural tube; elimination of excess neurons |
| **Tissue homeostasis** | Balancing cell proliferation and death in renewing tissues (skin, gut, bone marrow) |
| **Immune system** | Elimination of self-reactive lymphocytes (central and peripheral tolerance); killing of virus-infected cells by cytotoxic T cells |
| **Hormone-dependent involution** | Endometrial shedding during menstruation; regression of lactating breast after weaning |
| **Aging** | Increased apoptosis in post-mitotic tissues (heart, brain) |
| **Diseases with too little apoptosis** | Cancer (p53 mutations, Bcl-2 overexpression); autoimmune diseases (failure to delete self-reactive lymphocytes) |
| **Diseases with too much apoptosis** | Neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's); ischemic injury (stroke, myocardial infarction); AIDS (CD4+ T cell depletion); graft-versus-host disease |

#### 7.6 Clinical Relevance and Therapeutic Targeting

| Therapeutic Approach | Target | Drugs/Strategies |
|---------------------|--------|------------------|
| **Cancer therapy** | Induce apoptosis in tumor cells | Chemotherapy, radiation, BH3 mimetics (venetoclax targets Bcl-2) |
| **Neurodegeneration** | Inhibit excessive apoptosis | Neuroprotective agents (experimental) |
| **Ischemia-reperfusion injury** | Inhibit apoptosis | Caspase inhibitors (experimental) |
| **Autoimmune diseases** | Induce apoptosis of autoreactive lymphocytes | Immunosuppressive drugs |
