

## Unit 3: Circulation

### Introduction to Circulatory Physiology

The circulatory system is a closed-loop transport system responsible for the delivery of oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and immune cells to tissues, as well as the removal of metabolic waste products like carbon dioxide . It comprises two interconnected circuits: the pulmonary circulation and the systemic circulation . These circuits work in series to ensure continuous blood flow, driven by the heart’s pumping action. The system is not merely a set of passive pipes; it is a dynamic, regulated network where blood flow is constantly adjusted to meet the varying metabolic demands of different organs .

**Functions of the Circulatory System**:
- **Gas Exchange**: Oxygenation of blood in the lungs and delivery of O₂ to tissues; removal of CO₂.
- **Nutrient and Waste Transport**: Delivery of absorbed nutrients from the digestive tract and transport of metabolic wastes to the kidneys for excretion .
- **Hormonal Communication**: Transport of hormones from endocrine glands to target organs.
- **Immune Surveillance**: Distribution of white blood cells and antibodies.
- **Thermoregulation**: Adjustment of blood flow to the skin to regulate heat loss .
- **Fluid Homeostasis**: Capillary fluid exchange and lymphatic drainage prevent edema .

### 1. Physiological Anatomy of Blood Vessels

The vasculature is a branching network of tubes designed to transport blood under varying pressures and to facilitate exchange. The structure of each vessel type is precisely matched to its function .

#### 1.1 General Structure of Vessel Walls

Except for capillaries, blood vessel walls are composed of three distinct layers, or "tunics":

| Layer | Name | Composition | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tunica Intima** | Innermost layer | Endothelium (simple squamous epithelium) on a basement membrane; internal elastic lamina in arteries. | Provides a smooth, non-thrombogenic lining; regulates exchange; secretes paracrine factors. |
| **Tunica Media** | Middle layer | Smooth muscle cells arranged circularly; elastic and collagen fibers; external elastic lamina. | Regulates vessel diameter (vasoconstriction/vasodilation); provides structural support and elasticity. |
| **Tunica Adventitia** | Outer layer | Connective tissue (collagen and elastic fibers); contains vasa vasorum (vessels of the vessel) in large arteries and veins. | Anchors the vessel to surrounding tissues; provides structural support; contains nerves. |

#### 1.2 Types of Blood Vessels

The circulatory system is divided into two main circuits in series: the high-pressure systemic circulation and the low-pressure pulmonary circulation . The vessels can be categorized by their structure and function .

| Vessel Type | Structure | Function | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Elastic Arteries** (e.g., aorta, pulmonary artery) | Large diameter; thick walls with abundant elastic fibers in tunica media. | **Windkessel vessels**: Elasticity allows them to stretch during systole (storing pressure) and recoil during diastole, dampening pressure oscillations and maintaining continuous flow to downstream vessels . | High pressure, low resistance. |
| **Muscular Arteries** (e.g., femoral, renal arteries) | Medium diameter; tunica media contains more smooth muscle and fewer elastic fibers. | **Distributing vessels**: Capable of significant vasoconstriction and vasodilation to direct blood flow to specific organs. | |
| **Arterioles** | Small diameter (10-100 μm); 1-2 layers of smooth muscle. | **Resistance vessels**: Primary site of vascular resistance. By changing diameter, they regulate mean arterial pressure (MAP) and local blood flow to capillary beds . | Account for the largest drop in mean pressure. |
| **Capillaries** | Very small diameter (5-10 μm); single layer of endothelium and basement membrane. | **Exchange vessels**: Site of nutrient, gas, and waste exchange between blood and tissues . | Largest total cross-sectional area → slowest velocity (<1 mm/sec) to allow time for exchange . |
| **Venules** | Small, convergent vessels; thin walls with little smooth muscle. | **Collecting vessels**: Drain blood from capillaries and begin the return journey to the heart. | Serve as a site for white blood cell emigration. |
| **Veins** | Large diameter; thin walls with less smooth muscle than arteries; often contain valves. | **Capacitance vessels / Reservoirs**: High compliance allows them to hold up to 60-70% of the circulating blood volume at low pressure . Valves prevent backflow, especially in the limbs . | Low resistance, low pressure. |

#### 1.3 Capillary Bed Structure and Types

A capillary bed is a network of capillaries that arises from a metarteriole. A thoroughfare channel directly connects the arteriole to the venule. At the origin of each true capillary is a smooth muscle ring called the **precapillary sphincter**, which regulates blood flow into the capillary (capillary recruitment).

The endothelium of capillaries varies by location to suit the function of the tissue :

- **Continuous Capillaries**: Endothelial cells form a continuous lining with tight junctions. Small molecules (water, ions) can pass through intercellular clefts; large molecules must be transported via vesicles or channels. Found in muscle, lung, and adipose tissue.
- **Fenestrated Capillaries**: Endothelial cells have pores (fenestrae) that increase permeability. Found in tissues involved in rapid filtration or absorption, such as the kidneys, endocrine glands, and intestinal villi .
- **Discontinuous Sinusoids**: Large, irregular vessels with wide intercellular gaps and a discontinuous basement membrane. Allows passage of even large proteins and cells. Found in the liver, spleen, and bone marrow .

### 2. Hemodynamics

Hemodynamics is the study of the physical principles governing blood flow.

#### 2.1 Fundamental Relationships

- **Blood Flow (Q)** : The volume of blood passing a given point per unit time (e.g., L/min). In a closed system, flow is equal to cardiac output (CO). Flow is determined by the pressure difference between two points (\(\Delta P\)) and the resistance (R) to flow .
- **Ohm's Law of Hemodynamics**: \( Q = \Delta P / R \)
    - For the systemic circulation: \( CO = (MAP - RAP) / TPR \), where MAP is mean arterial pressure, RAP is right atrial pressure (~0 mmHg), and TPR is total peripheral resistance. Thus, \( MAP \approx CO \times TPR \) .
- **Velocity of Blood Flow (v)** : The speed of blood cells, which is inversely related to the cross-sectional area (A) .
    - \( v = Q / A \)
    - Velocity is highest in the aorta, slows dramatically in the capillaries (facilitating exchange), and increases again in the veins .

#### 2.2 Resistance to Flow

Resistance is the opposition to flow and is primarily determined by the radius of the vessel, particularly the arterioles . The **Poiseuille Equation** describes the factors governing resistance in a cylindrical tube:

\[ R = \frac{8\eta l}{\pi r^4} \]

Where:
- **\( \eta \)** = blood viscosity (determined primarily by hematocrit; increases in polycythemia, decreases in anemia) .
- **\( l \)** = length of the vessel (constant in the body).
- **\( r \)** = radius of the vessel.

**Clinical Correlate**: Because resistance is inversely proportional to the **fourth power of the radius (\(r^4\))**, even a small change in vessel diameter has a massive impact on resistance and flow. For example, vasodilation that doubles the radius decreases resistance by a factor of 16, dramatically increasing flow.

#### 2.3 Laminar and Turbulent Flow

- **Laminar Flow**: Blood flows in concentric layers (streamlines) with the fastest velocity in the center. This is silent and efficient.
- **Turbulent Flow**: Disorganized, chaotic flow that creates audible vibrations (murmurs or bruits). It occurs when flow velocity exceeds a critical point, such as across a stenotic valve, in a high-output state, or in an aneurysm.
- **Reynolds Number (\(N_R\))** : A dimensionless number that predicts the likelihood of turbulent flow .
    - \( N_R = (\rho v d) / \eta \)
    - Turbulence is more likely with high density (\( \rho \)), high velocity (\(v\)), large diameter (\(d\)), and low viscosity (\( \eta \)) .

### 3. The Systemic Circulation: Pressures and Volumes

Blood pressure is not constant throughout the circulation. It is highest in the aorta and large arteries and declines progressively as it overcomes the resistance of smaller vessels, reaching nearly zero by the time it enters the right atrium .

- **Systolic Pressure (SP)** : Peak pressure during ventricular ejection. It reflects stroke volume and aortic compliance .
- **Diastolic Pressure (DP)** : Minimum pressure during ventricular diastole, maintained by elastic recoil of arteries. It reflects total peripheral resistance (TPR) .
- **Pulse Pressure (PP)** : \( PP = SP - DP \). It is proportional to stroke volume and inversely proportional to arterial compliance .
- **Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP)** : The average pressure driving blood forward into the tissues.
    - \( MAP = DP + \frac{1}{3}(PP) \)
    - \( MAP \approx CO \times TPR \) .

### 4. Regulation of Systemic Circulation

Blood flow is regulated both globally (to maintain MAP) and locally (to match flow to metabolic need).

#### 4.1 Local (Intrinsic) Control

Tissues have the ability to autoregulate their own blood flow .
- **Metabolic Autoregulation**: Increased tissue metabolism (e.g., exercising muscle) leads to the accumulation of vasoactive metabolites (adenosine, CO₂, H⁺, K⁺, lactate). These cause vasodilation, increasing local blood flow and washing out the metabolites .
- **Myogenic Autoregulation**: Vascular smooth muscle constricts in response to being stretched by increased intravascular pressure and relaxes in response to decreased pressure. This helps maintain a relatively constant flow despite changes in perfusion pressure .
- **Specific Organs**:
    - **Brain**: Blood flow is highly sensitive to CO₂ (hypercapnia causes potent vasodilation) .
    - **Heart**: Coronary flow is regulated by local metabolites, especially adenosine, which is released during hypoxia .
    - **Kidneys**: Employ tubuloglomerular feedback to maintain a constant glomerular filtration rate (GFR).

#### 4.2 Nervous (Extrinsic) Control

The autonomic nervous system regulates vessel diameter, primarily via the sympathetic division.
- **Sympathetic Control**: Norepinephrine acts on adrenergic receptors .
    - **\( \alpha_1 \)-receptors**: Located on most vascular smooth muscle (skin, splanchnic, renal). Activation causes **vasoconstriction**, increasing TPR and decreasing flow to these beds .
    - **\( \beta_2 \)-receptors**: Located primarily on coronary and skeletal muscle arterioles. Activation causes **vasodilation**. These receptors are more sensitive to circulating epinephrine than to norepinephrine from nerve terminals .
- **Parasympathetic Control**: Has minimal direct effect on most vascular smooth muscle.

#### 4.3 Reflex Regulation

- **Baroreceptor Reflex**: The primary short-term regulator of blood pressure. Stretch-sensitive mechanoreceptors located in the carotid sinus and aortic arch monitor MAP .
    - **Increased MAP**: Increased stretch → increased afferent signals via glossopharyngeal (CN IX) and vagus (CN X) nerves to the medulla → increased parasympathetic (vagal) outflow to heart and decreased sympathetic outflow to heart and vessels. **Result**: Decreased HR, decreased contractility, vasodilation → MAP normalizes.
    - **Decreased MAP**: Decreased stretch → decreased afferent signals → increased sympathetic outflow and decreased parasympathetic outflow. **Result**: Increased HR, increased contractility, vasoconstriction → MAP normalizes .
- **Chemoreceptor Reflex**: Peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid and aortic bodies) sense decreases in arterial \(P_{O_2}\), increases in \(P_{CO_2}\), and decreases in pH. Their primary role is respiratory control, but severe hypoxia can trigger sympathetic activation and vasoconstriction .

#### 4.4 Hormonal Control

- **Epinephrine**: Released from adrenal medulla. At low doses, its \( \beta_2 \) effect can cause vasodilation; at high doses, its \( \alpha_1 \) effect causes vasoconstriction.
- **Angiotensin II**: A potent vasoconstrictor; part of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
- **Vasopressin (ADH)** : Released from the posterior pituitary in response to increased plasma osmolality or decreased blood volume. A potent vasoconstrictor at high concentrations .
- **Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP)** : Released from atrial myocytes in response to stretch (volume overload). Causes vasodilation and promotes sodium and water excretion .

### 5. The Pulmonary Circulation

The pulmonary circulation is a low-resistance, high-flow circuit that carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs for gas exchange .

**Pathway**: Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Trunk → Pulmonary Arteries → Pulmonary Capillaries (alveoli) → Pulmonary Veins → Left Atrium .

**Key Physiological Differences from Systemic Circulation**:

| Feature | Pulmonary Circulation | Systemic Circulation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pressure** | Low pressure (RV systolic ~25 mmHg; PA mean ~15 mmHg)  | High pressure (LV systolic ~120 mmHg) |
| **Resistance** | Low vascular resistance (PVR ~1/10th of TPR) | High vascular resistance (TPR) |
| **Autoregulation** | **Hypoxic Vasoconstriction**: Low alveolar \(P_{O_2}\) causes vasoconstriction to match perfusion to ventilation. | Hypoxia causes vasodilation to increase flow. |
| **Capillaries** | Alveolar capillaries are surrounded by air, making them prone to compression. | Capillaries are embedded in solid tissue. |

**Clinical Correlate**:
- **Pulmonary Embolism (PE)** : A clot (often from a DVT) lodges in the pulmonary arteries, increasing dead space and PVR. This can lead to right heart strain and failure .
- **Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH)** : Mean PA pressure >25 mmHg. Can be idiopathic or secondary to conditions like left heart failure (most common cause), hypoxia, or connective tissue disease .

### 6. The Lymphatic System

The lymphatic system is an accessory, one-way return system that collects interstitial fluid (lymph) and returns it to the venous circulation .

**Functions**:
- **Fluid Balance**: Returns ~8-12 L of fluid and leaked plasma proteins to the blood per day, preventing edema .
- **Lipid Absorption**: Lacteals in the intestinal villi absorb dietary fats and fat-soluble vitamins, transporting them as chyle .
- **Immunity**: Lymph nodes filter lymph, housing lymphocytes and antigen-presenting cells that mount immune responses .

**Structure and Physiology**:
- **Lymphatic Capillaries**: Blind-ended, highly permeable vessels with overlapping endothelial cells that act as one-way valves, allowing fluid and proteins to enter but not leave .
- **Collecting Lymphatics**: Have smooth muscle in their walls and intraluminal valves. They exhibit **intrinsic phasic contractions** to actively pump lymph forward . This "lymph pump" is crucial for transport.
- **Lymph Flow** is also aided by **extrinsic forces** such as skeletal muscle contraction, arterial pulsations, and respiratory movements .
- Lymph passes through **lymph nodes** before eventually emptying into the venous system via the right lymphatic duct or thoracic duct .

### 7. Fetal Circulation and Transition at Birth

Fetal circulation operates in parallel, with the placenta serving as the organ for gas exchange and nutrient/waste handling .

#### 7.1 Unique Features (Shunts)

- **Placenta**: Site of oxygenation. Oxygenated blood returns to the fetus via the **umbilical vein**.
- **Ductus Venosus**: Shunts a portion of umbilical vein blood past the liver, draining directly into the inferior vena cava (IVC) .
- **Foramen Ovale**: An opening in the interatrial septum. It allows most of the oxygenated blood entering the right atrium from the IVC to be shunted directly to the left atrium, bypassing the non-functioning lungs .
- **Ductus Arteriosus**: A connection between the pulmonary trunk and the aorta. It shunts blood from the right ventricle away from the high-resistance lungs and into the systemic circulation .

#### 7.2 Circulatory Changes at Birth

1.  **Clamping of the Umbilical Cord**: Removes the low-resistance placenta, abruptly increasing systemic vascular resistance (SVR).
2.  **First Breaths**: Lungs expand, and alveolar \(P_{O_2}\) rises, causing a dramatic drop in pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) .
3.  **Functional Closure of Shunts**:
    - The drop in PVR and rise in SVR reverses the pressure gradient, increasing left atrial pressure. This pushes the septum primum against the septum secundum, functionally closing the **foramen ovale**.
    - Increased \(P_{O_2}\) stimulates constriction of the ductus arteriosus smooth muscle, functionally closing it. This vessel eventually becomes the **ligamentum arteriosum** .

#### Table 1: Cardiovascular System Components

| Component | Primary Function | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
