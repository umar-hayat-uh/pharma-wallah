## Unit 7: Temperature Regulation

### Introduction to Temperature Regulation

Body temperature regulation is a classic example of homeostasis, where the body maintains its internal temperature within a narrow range despite wide fluctuations in environmental temperature. This regulation is critical because enzymatic reactions, membrane function, and other cellular processes are temperature-sensitive. Humans are homeotherms, maintaining a relatively constant body temperature regardless of environmental conditions.

**Importance of Temperature Regulation**:
- Optimal enzyme function (rate doubles for every 10°C increase, up to a point)
- Protein stability (denaturation at high temperatures)
- Membrane fluidity
- Metabolic rate
- Neural function (nerve conduction velocity affected by temperature)

---

### 1. Body Temperature: Normal Values and Variations

#### 1.1 Core vs. Shell Temperature

| Compartment | Definition | Normal Temperature | Characteristics |
|-------------|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Core temperature** | Temperature of deep tissues (brain, thorax, abdomen) | 36.5-37.5°C (97.7-99.5°F) | Tightly regulated; relatively constant |
| **Shell temperature** | Temperature of skin and subcutaneous tissues | 28-36°C (82.4-96.8°F) | Varies with environmental temperature; cooler than core |

**Measurement Sites and Normal Ranges**:

| Site | Normal Range | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------|--------------|------------|---------------|
| **Oral** | 36.3-37.1°C | Convenient | Affected by drinking, breathing |
| **Rectal** | 36.7-37.5°C | Most accurate core temperature | Inconvenient; lags during rapid changes |
| **Tympanic** | 35.8-37.6°C | Rapid; reflects core (close to hypothalamus) | Requires proper technique |
| **Axillary** | 35.5-37.0°C | Safe, non-invasive | Lower than core; less accurate |
| **Temporal artery** | Variable | Non-invasive | Accuracy varies |

#### 1.2 Normal Variations in Body Temperature

| Factor | Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Circadian rhythm** | Lowest in early morning (4-6 AM); highest in late afternoon (4-6 PM) | Endogenous rhythms; variation 0.5-1.0°C |
| **Age** | Infants: less stable regulation; elderly: lower baseline, reduced thermoregulatory capacity | Immature/declining thermoregulatory mechanisms |
| **Gender** | Women: 0.3-0.5°C higher than men; varies with menstrual cycle (progesterone increases temperature) | Hormonal effects |
| **Exercise** | Increases temperature (can rise to 39-40°C during intense exercise) | Heat production from muscle contraction |
| **Digestion** | Slight increase after meals | Specific dynamic action of food |
| **Emotion** | Slight increase | Sympathetic stimulation |
| **Environmental temperature** | Minimal effect on core (within thermoregulatory range) | Homeostatic mechanisms maintain core |

### 2. Mechanisms of Body Temperature Regulation

#### 2.1 Heat Production (Thermogenesis)

Heat is a byproduct of metabolic processes. The body produces heat through several mechanisms.

**Sources of Heat Production**:

| Source | Contribution at Rest | Contribution During Exercise | Mechanism |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------------|-----------|
| **Basal metabolic rate** | ~60% | Variable | Cellular metabolism (all organs) |
| **Muscle contraction** | ~25% (resting muscle tone) | Up to 90% | Shivering; voluntary muscle activity |
| **Liver** | ~10% | ~10% | Metabolic reactions |
| **Heart, kidneys, brain** | ~5% | ~5% | Continuous activity |
| **Non-shivering thermogenesis** | Minimal (adults); significant (infants, small mammals) | - | Metabolism in brown adipose tissue (uncoupling protein-1, UCP1) |

**Shivering Thermogenesis**:
- Involuntary, rhythmic muscle contractions
- Increases heat production 4-5 times above basal
- Controlled by hypothalamus (posterior region)
- Initiated by cold exposure

**Non-Shivering Thermogenesis**:
- **Brown adipose tissue (BAT)** : Specialized fat tissue rich in mitochondria and UCP1
- UCP1 uncouples oxidative phosphorylation → produces heat instead of ATP
- Important in infants (small body mass, limited shivering) and small mammals
- Adults have small amounts of BAT (in neck, supraclavicular, paravertebral regions)
- Stimulated by sympathetic nervous system (norepinephrine via β₃ receptors)

**Thyroid Hormone and Thermogenesis**:
- Increases basal metabolic rate (calorigenic effect)
- Enhances Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase activity
- Increases mitochondrial enzyme activity
- Chronic cold exposure increases thyroid hormone secretion

#### 2.2 Heat Loss Mechanisms

Heat must be lost to maintain constant temperature. Heat transfer occurs through physical mechanisms.

**Routes of Heat Loss**:

| Route | Percentage (at rest, comfortable environment) | Mechanism | Regulation |
|-------|----------------------------------------------|-----------|------------|
| **Radiation** | 60% | Emission of infrared electromagnetic waves | Cannot be regulated directly; depends on skin temperature and environmental temperature |
| **Conduction** | 3% | Direct transfer to cooler objects in contact | Minimal (except lying on cold surface) |
| **Convection** | 15% | Transfer to moving air or water molecules | Increased by air movement (wind chill); body can increase by piloerection (minimal in humans) |
| **Evaporation** | 22% | Heat loss when water evaporates from skin and respiratory tract | Highly regulated (sweating); major mechanism in hot environments |

**Evaporative Heat Loss**:
- Each mL of sweat evaporated removes ~0.58 kcal of heat
- **Insensible perspiration**: Continuous, non-visible water loss (skin, lungs); ~600 mL/day; not regulated
- **Sweating (sensible perspiration)** : Active secretion by eccrine sweat glands; regulated by sympathetic cholinergic fibers
  - Can lose up to 1-2 L/hour during intense exercise in heat
  - Sweat is hypotonic (water, Na⁺, Cl⁻, K⁺, urea, lactate)
  - Evaporation depends on humidity (ineffective in high humidity)

**Cutaneous Circulation and Heat Loss**:
- Skin blood flow varies from 0-30% of cardiac output
- **Vasodilation**: Increases heat loss (warm environments)
  - Active vasodilation (mediated by NO, bradykinin, VIP from sweat glands?)
  - Increases skin blood flow up to 7-8 L/min
- **Vasoconstriction**: Decreases heat loss (cold environments)
  - Sympathetic α-adrenergic vasoconstriction
  - Reduces skin blood flow to near zero
- **Arteriovenous anastomoses**: Direct connections between arterioles and venules in fingers, toes, ears, nose; open during vasodilation (increase heat loss)

**Countercurrent Heat Exchange**:
- In limbs, arteries and veins run close together
- Warm arterial blood heats cool venous blood returning from extremities
- Reduces heat loss from extremities; preserves core temperature
- Especially important in cold environments

#### 2.3 Thermal Balance Equation

Heat balance can be expressed as:

**Heat storage = Heat production - Heat loss**

- If heat production > heat loss → body temperature rises
- If heat production < heat loss → body temperature falls
- At equilibrium: Heat production = Heat loss

### 3. The Thermoregulatory System: The "Thermostat"

Body temperature is regulated by a control system centered in the hypothalamus, which functions like a thermostat.

#### 3.1 Components of the Thermoregulatory System

| Component | Structure | Function |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Sensors (thermoreceptors)** | Peripheral (skin) and central (hypothalamus, spinal cord, abdominal organs) | Detect temperature changes |
| **Integrator (controller)** | Hypothalamus (preoptic anterior hypothalamus – POAH) | Compares actual temperature to set point; coordinates responses |
| **Effectors** | Sweat glands, smooth muscle in skin vessels, skeletal muscle, endocrine organs | Execute responses to change temperature |

#### 3.2 Temperature Sensors

**Peripheral Thermoreceptors**:
- Located in skin (free nerve endings)
- **Cold receptors**: More numerous; in superficial skin layers; respond to 10-40°C; maximal firing at 25°C
- **Warm receptors**: Less numerous; deeper; respond to 30-45°C; maximal firing at 43°C
- Signals transmitted via Aδ (cold) and C fibers (warm) to spinal cord → spinothalamic tract → thalamus → hypothalamus and somatosensory cortex

**Central Thermoreceptors**:
- Located in hypothalamus (preoptic area), spinal cord, abdominal viscera, great veins
- Directly monitor core temperature
- Warm-sensitive neurons in POAH increase firing with warming → initiate heat loss
- Cold-sensitive neurons (less common) increase firing with cooling → initiate heat conservation/production

#### 3.3 The Hypothalamic Set Point

The set point is the target temperature the body attempts to maintain (normally ~37°C). It is determined by the balance of activity of warm-sensitive and cold-sensitive neurons in the preoptic anterior hypothalamus (POAH).

**How the Set Point Works**:
- If actual temperature < set point → heat conservation/production mechanisms activated
- If actual temperature > set point → heat loss mechanisms activated
- If actual temperature = set point → minimal thermoregulatory activity

**Neural Pathways**:

| Response | Pathway |
|----------|---------|
| **Vasoconstriction** | POAH → posterior hypothalamus → sympathetic (α-adrenergic) → cutaneous vessels |
| **Vasodilation** | POAH → inhibition of sympathetic tone (active vasodilation via NO, etc.) |
| **Sweating** | POAH → anterior hypothalamus → sympathetic cholinergic fibers to sweat glands |
| **Shivering** | POAH → posterior hypothalamus → lateral hypothalamic area → motor pathways (rubrospinal, reticulospinal) → α motor neurons |
| **Behavioral responses** | Hypothalamus → cerebral cortex → conscious sensation of temperature → behavioral adjustments |

#### 3.4 Responses to Cold Exposure

When body temperature falls below set point:

| Response | Mechanism | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Cutaneous vasoconstriction** | Sympathetic (α-adrenergic) → arterioles constrict | Reduces heat loss from skin |
| **Piloerection** | Sympathetic → arrector pili muscles contract (minimal in humans) | Traps insulating air layer (effective in furred animals) |
| **Shivering** | Rhythmic muscle contractions (10-12 Hz) | Increases heat production 4-5× |
| **Non-shivering thermogenesis** | Sympathetic (β₃) → BAT activation (UCP1) | Increases heat production (especially infants) |
| **Thyroid hormone secretion** | TRH → TSH → T₄/T₃ (chronic cold) | Increases BMR |
| **Behavioral responses** | Seeking warmth, adding clothing, curling up | Voluntary actions to reduce heat loss |
| **Curtailment of sweating** | Inhibition of sympathetic cholinergic outflow | Prevents evaporative heat loss |

#### 3.5 Responses to Heat Exposure

When body temperature rises above set point:

| Response | Mechanism | Effect |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Cutaneous vasodilation** | Inhibition of sympathetic tone; active vasodilation | Increases heat loss from skin |
| **Sweating** | Sympathetic cholinergic fibers to eccrine sweat glands | Evaporative heat loss |
| **Behavioral responses** | Seeking shade, removing clothing, reducing activity | Voluntary actions to reduce heat gain/increase loss |
| **Reduction in shivering** | Inhibition of shivering pathways | Prevents unnecessary heat production |
| **Reduction in heat production** | Decreased muscle tone; reduced voluntary activity | Minimizes metabolic heat |

### 4. Pathophysiology of Fever

Fever is an elevation of body temperature above the normal daily variation, occurring in conjunction with an increase in the hypothalamic set point. It is a common response to infection and inflammation.

#### 4.1 Definition and Distinction

| Term | Definition | Set Point | Mechanism |
|------|------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Fever** | Elevated body temperature due to raised set point | Increased | Cytokine-mediated |
| **Hyperthermia** | Elevated body temperature with normal set point | Normal | Heat gain exceeds heat loss (environmental, exertional, drug-induced) |
| **Hyperpyrexia** | Fever >41.5°C (106.7°F) | Markedly increased | Severe infection, CNS hemorrhage |
| **Hypothermia** | Core temperature <35°C (95°F) | Variable or overwhelmed | Cold exposure, impaired thermoregulation |

#### 4.2 Pyrogens: Fever-Inducing Substances

**Exogenous Pyrogens**:
- Microbial products that induce fever
- **Lipopolysaccharide (LPS, endotoxin)** from Gram-negative bacteria (most potent)
- Lipoteichoic acid (Gram-positive bacteria)
- Viral nucleic acids, viral proteins
- Fungi, protozoa products

**Endogenous Pyrogens**:
- Cytokines produced by host immune cells (macrophages, monocytes, others) in response to exogenous pyrogens
- **Major endogenous pyrogens**:
  - **Interleukin-1 (IL-1)** α and β
  - **Interleukin-6 (IL-6)**
  - **Tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α)**
  - Interferons (IFN-α, IFN-γ)
  - Ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF), oncostatin M

#### 4.3 Mechanism of Fever

**Steps in Fever Development**:

| Step | Process |
|------|---------|
| **1. Recognition** | Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) bind to pattern recognition receptors (TLRs, etc.) on macrophages |
| **2. Cytokine production** | Macrophages release IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α |
| **3. Cytokine transport** | Cytokines enter circulation; act on circumventricular organs (where blood-brain barrier is leaky) – organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis (OVLT) |
| **4. Prostaglandin synthesis** | Cytokines induce cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) in hypothalamic endothelial cells → PGE₂ synthesis |
| **5. Set point elevation** | PGE₂ acts on thermoregulatory neurons in POAH (EP3 receptors) → cAMP decreases in warm-sensitive neurons → inhibits their activity → set point rises |
| **6. Effector response** | Body perceives normal temperature as cold → activates heat conservation/production mechanisms |
| **7. Febrile response** | Vasoconstriction, shivering (rigors), seeking warmth → temperature rises to new set point |

**Stages of Fever**:

| Stage | Description | Clinical Features |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Prodrome** | Early phase before temperature rise | Malaise, aches |
| **Chill (rigor)** | Body temperature rising to new set point; feels cold despite normal or elevated temperature | Shivering, vasoconstriction, piloerection (goosebumps), cold extremities |
| **Febrile plateau** | Temperature stabilized at new set point | Warm, flushed skin; cessation of shivering |
| **Defervescence** | Set point returns to normal (crisis) | Sweating, vasodilation, feeling hot |

#### 4.4 Benefits and Harms of Fever

**Potential Benefits**:
- Enhances immune function (increased leukocyte motility, phagocytosis, proliferation)
- Inhibits growth of some pathogens
- Increases production of interferons, antibodies
- May enhance tissue repair

**Potential Harms**:
- Increased metabolic demand (10-12.5% increase per °C)
- Dehydration (increased fluid loss)
- Discomfort (myalgia, headache)
- Febrile seizures (in young children, usually benign)
- Extreme fever (>41°C) can cause protein denaturation, organ damage

#### 4.5 Antipyretic Therapy

Antipyretics reduce fever by lowering the set point (not by enhancing heat loss mechanisms like physical cooling).

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| **NSAIDs** | Inhibit COX-2 (and COX-1) → reduce PGE₂ synthesis | Ibuprofen, aspirin, naproxen |
| **Acetaminophen (paracetamol)** | Weak COX inhibitor; may act centrally (COX-3?); antioxidant effects | Acetaminophen |
| **Corticosteroids** | Inhibit cytokine production (not typically used for simple fever) | Prednisone, dexamethasone |

**Physical cooling** (sponging, ice packs) is not recommended for fever alone (causes shivering, discomfort, may increase metabolic demand). Used only in hyperthermia or extreme hyperpyrexia.

### 5. Pathophysiology of Hypothermia

Hypothermia is defined as core temperature below 35°C (95°F). It results from excessive heat loss, inadequate heat production, or impaired thermoregulation.

#### 5.1 Causes of Hypothermia

| Category | Causes |
|----------|--------|
| **Environmental exposure** | Cold water immersion, inadequate clothing in cold weather, homelessness |
| **Metabolic/endocrine** | Hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, hypoglycemia, starvation |
| **Neurological** | Stroke, spinal cord injury, hypothalamic lesions, Wernicke encephalopathy |
| **Drug-induced** | Ethanol (vasodilation, impaired judgment), sedatives, anesthetics, antipsychotics |
| **Sepsis** | Especially in elderly; impaired thermoregulation |
| **Trauma/burns** | Impaired thermoregulation, exposure |
| **Age extremes** | Infants (large surface area:mass ratio, limited shivering); elderly (decreased muscle mass, impaired vasoconstriction, medications) |

#### 5.2 Stages of Hypothermia

| Stage | Core Temperature | Clinical Features |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Mild** | 32-35°C (90-95°F) | Shivering, cold diuresis, tachycardia, tachypnea, impaired fine motor skills, confusion, ataxia |
| **Moderate** | 28-32°C (82-90°F) | Shivering ceases (at <32°C), bradycardia, bradypnea, hypotension, dilated pupils, hyporeflexia, stupor, hallucinations |
| **Severe** | <28°C (82°F) | Coma, areflexia, apnea, pulmonary edema, ventricular fibrillation (especially at <30°C), asystole; patient may appear dead |

**Cold Diuresis**:
- Vasoconstriction increases central blood volume
- Suppression of ADH
- Decreased tubular reabsorption
- Can lead to hypovolemia if not replaced

#### 5.3 Pathophysiology of Hypothermia

| System | Effects |
|--------|---------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Initial tachycardia → progressive bradycardia; decreased cardiac output; increased vascular resistance; arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation, ventricular fibrillation); Osborn J wave on ECG (positive deflection at J point) |
| **Respiratory** | Initial tachypnea → progressive bradypnea; decreased O₂ delivery; bronchorrhea; pulmonary edema |
| **Neurological** | Decreased cerebral blood flow; impaired consciousness; loss of reflexes; dilated pupils |
| **Renal** | Cold diuresis; impaired renal function |
| **Metabolic** | Decreased metabolic rate; acidosis (respiratory and metabolic); hyperglycemia (initially) then hypoglycemia; coagulopathy (enzyme inhibition) |
| **Hematological** | Increased blood viscosity; platelet dysfunction; disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) in severe cases |

**Osborn (J) Wave**: Positive deflection at the J point (QRS-ST junction); characteristic of moderate-severe hypothermia; not pathognomonic but highly suggestive.

#### 5.4 Rewarming Strategies

| Technique | Indication | Method |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| **Passive external rewarming** | Mild hypothermia (shivering present) | Remove wet clothing; insulate with blankets; warm environment |
| **Active external rewarming** | Mild-moderate hypothermia (no shivering) | Warm blankets, forced-air warming (Bair Hugger), warm water immersion |
| **Active internal (core) rewarming** | Severe hypothermia; cardiovascular instability | Warm IV fluids (40-42°C), warmed humidified oxygen, peritoneal lavage, pleural lavage, extracorporeal rewarming (cardiopulmonary bypass, ECMO) |

**Complications of Rewarming**:
- **Afterdrop**: Continued cooling of core due to return of cold blood from periphery
- **Rewarming shock**: Vasodilation → hypotension if volume not maintained
- **Arrhythmias**: May occur during rewarming (especially if rapid)

### 6. Clinical Applications and Pharmaceutical Relevance

#### 6.1 Drugs Affecting Temperature Regulation

| Drug Class | Effect on Temperature | Mechanism |
|------------|----------------------|-----------|
| **Antipyretics (NSAIDs, acetaminophen)** | Lower fever | Inhibit COX → reduce PGE₂ → lower set point |
| **Antipsychotics (phenothiazines)** | Can cause hypothermia or hyperthermia | Block dopamine; impair hypothalamic regulation; peripheral α-blockade (vasodilation) |
| **Anesthetics** | Hypothermia | Impair thermoregulation; vasodilation; decreased metabolism |
| **Succinylcholine** | Hyperthermia (malignant hyperthermia) | Triggers uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from SR in susceptible individuals |
| **Thyroid hormone** | Hyperthermia (excess) | Increases BMR; calorigenic effect |
| **Sympathomimetics (amphetamines, cocaine, MDMA)** | Hyperthermia | Increased activity; vasoconstriction; impaired heat dissipation |
| **Anticholinergics (atropine, tricyclics)** | Hyperthermia | Inhibit sweating (decreased heat loss) |
| **Alcohol** | Hypothermia (acute) | Vasodilation; impaired judgment; decreased shivering |
| **Beta-blockers** | May impair cold-induced vasodilation | Block β-receptors |

#### 6.2 Malignant Hyperthermia

**Definition**: Life-threatening condition triggered by volatile anesthetics (halothane, isoflurane, sevoflurane) or succinylcholine in susceptible individuals (RYR1 mutation, CACNA1S mutation).

**Pathophysiology**:
- Uncontrolled release of Ca²⁺ from sarcoplasmic reticulum via abnormal ryanodine receptors (RyR1)
- Sustained muscle contraction (rigidity)
- Massive heat production
- Rhabdomyolysis, acidosis, hyperkalemia

**Clinical Features**:
- Masseter muscle rigidity (early sign)
- Tachycardia, tachypnea
- Hyperthermia (rapid rise, up to 1°C every 5 minutes)
- Metabolic acidosis, respiratory acidosis
- Hyperkalemia, elevated CK, myoglobinuria

**Treatment**:
- Dantrolene (inhibits RyR; reduces Ca²⁺ release)
- Discontinue triggering agents
- Hyperventilation with 100% O₂
- Active cooling
- Treat acidosis, hyperkalemia

#### 6.3 Heat Stroke

**Definition**: Severe hyperthermia (core >40°C) with neurological dysfunction, due to failure of thermoregulation.

**Types**:

| Type | Mechanism | Features |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Classic (nonexertional)** | Environmental heat exposure; impaired heat loss (elderly, chronic illness, medications) | Gradual onset; anhidrotic skin; high mortality |
| **Exertional** | Strenuous exercise in hot/humid environment | Rapid onset; sweating may be present; younger individuals |

**Pathophysiology**:
- Core temperature exceeds thermoregulatory capacity
- Direct cellular toxicity (protein denaturation, membrane damage)
- Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS)
- Endothelial injury → disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
- Multi-organ failure (CNS, liver, kidney, heart)

**Clinical Features**:
- Hyperthermia (>40°C)
- CNS dysfunction (confusion, seizures, coma)
- Hot, dry skin (classic) or sweating (exertional)
- Cardiovascular collapse
- Hepatic necrosis, acute kidney injury
- DIC, rhabdomyolysis

**Treatment**:
- Rapid cooling (ice water immersion, evaporative cooling, cold IV fluids, cold peritoneal lavage)
- Supportive care (airway, breathing, circulation)
- Treat complications (DIC, seizures, AKI)
