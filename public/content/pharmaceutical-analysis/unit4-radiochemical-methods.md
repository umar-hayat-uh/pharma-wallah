### **4.1 Introduction to Radiopharmaceuticals**
Radiopharmaceuticals are medicinal formulations containing **radioisotopes (radionuclides)** used in nuclear medicine for diagnosis or therapy. They combine a **pharmaceutical compound** (which determines localization in the body) with a **radioactive atom** (which provides the detectable signal or therapeutic effect).

#### **Key Concepts & Principles**
1.  **Radioactive Decay:** The spontaneous disintegration of an unstable atomic nucleus, emitting radiation.
    *   **Types of Emission:**
        *   **Alpha (α) particles:** Helium nuclei (²He⁴⁺). High ionizing power, low tissue penetration.
        *   **Beta (β) particles:** High-speed electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺). Moderate penetration.
        *   **Gamma (γ) rays:** Electromagnetic radiation. High penetration, ideal for external detection.
    *   **Positron Emission:** A β⁺ particle annihilates with an electron, producing two 511 keV gamma photons emitted at 180° (basis for PET imaging).
2.  **Half-life (t₁/₂):** Time required for half the radioactive atoms in a sample to decay. Critical for usage and safety.
    *   **Physical Half-life:** Intrinsic to the radionuclide.
    *   **Biological Half-life:** Time for half the compound to be eliminated biologically.
    *   **Effective Half-life:** Combined effect: `1/T_eff = 1/T_physical + 1/T_biological`.
3.  **Specific Activity:** Radioactivity per unit mass (e.g., Ci/g, Bq/mol). High specific activity is often required for receptor-binding studies.
4.  **Common Radionuclides:**
    *   **Diagnostic:** Technetium-99m (⁹⁹ᵐTc, γ, t₁/₂=6h), Fluorine-18 (¹⁸F, β⁺, t₁/₂=110 min), Iodine-123 (¹²³I, γ, t₁/₂=13h).
    *   **Therapeutic:** Iodine-131 (¹³¹I, β⁻, γ, t₁/₂=8 days), Yttrium-90 (⁹⁰Y, β⁻, t₁/₂=64h), Lutetium-177 (¹⁷⁷Lu, β⁻, γ, t₁/₂=6.7 days).

#### **Components of a Radiopharmaceutical**
1.  **Radionuclide:** Provides the signal or effect.
2.  **Ligand/Vector Molecule:** Determines biodistribution (e.g., MDP for bone, MIBG for adrenergic tissue, antibodies, peptides).
3.  **Linker/Complexing Agent:** Chemically binds the radionuclide to the ligand (e.g., DTPA, DOTA chelators for metals).

### **4.2 Analysis and Quality Control of Radiopharmaceuticals**
QC is paramount and must be rapid due to short half-lives. It encompasses both **pharmaceutical quality** (sterility, apyrogenicity) and **radiochemical quality**.

#### **Radiochemical Purity (RCP)**
The percentage of total radioactivity present as the desired chemical form.
*   **Analytical Techniques:**
    1.  **Instant Thin-Layer Chromatography (ITLC):** Most common. Uses silica gel strips and specific solvent systems to separate species (e.g., free pertechnetate [⁹⁹ᵐTcO₄⁻] from ⁹⁹ᵐTc-MDP). Radioactivity distribution is measured with a **Radio-TLC Scanner**.
    2.  **High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC):** With radio-detection (e.g., NaI scintillation detector). Provides better separation for complex mixtures (peptides, antibodies).
    3.  **Solid-Phase Extraction (Mini-columns):** Quick separation of bound vs. free radionuclide.

#### **Chemical Purity & Radionuclidic Purity**
*   **Chemical Purity:** Freedom from non-radioactive chemicals (e.g., Al³⁺ ions in ⁹⁹ᵐTc generators which can cause colloid formation). Analyzed by standard methods (AAS, colorimetric tests).
*   **Radionuclidic Purity:** Percentage of total radioactivity from the desired radionuclide. Checked via **Gamma Spectrometry** using a **High-Purity Germanium (HPGe) Detector** to identify and quantify impurity radionuclides by their characteristic gamma energies.

#### **Pharmaceutical Quality Control**
*   **Sterility Testing:** Essential but results are retrospective. Use of rapid microbiological methods is encouraged.
*   **Apyrogenicity (Bacterial Endotoxin Test):** *Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL)* test. Must be completed before release.
*   **pH Measurement:** Using pH paper or a micro-electrode.
*   **Radiation Dose Calibration:** Using a **Calibrated Ionization Chamber (Dose Calibrator)**. This is a mandatory test for each prepared dose to ensure the administered activity is accurate.

### **4.3 Stability and Applications in Medicine**

#### **Stability Considerations**
Stability is influenced by both chemical and radioactive factors.
1.  **Radiochemical Stability:** Decomposition of the desired compound due to:
    *   **Radiolysis:** Radiation damage from the decaying atom itself ("autoradiolysis") or from other molecules in solution. Mitigated by using antioxidants (ascorbic acid, gentisic acid) and radical scavengers.
    *   **Chemical Hydrolysis/Oxidation:** Of the ligand or the bond to the radionuclide.
2.  **Shelf-Life:** Dictated by the radionuclide's **physical half-life** and the **radiochemical stability**. A product is usable only while its RCP remains above specification (e.g., >90%).
3.  **Storage:** Often requires lead shielding. Some kits are stored frozen to slow radiolysis.

#### **Applications in Medicine**
1.  **Diagnostic Imaging:**
    *   **Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT):** Uses gamma emitters (⁹⁹ᵐTc, ¹²³I). Provides 3D functional information (e.g., ⁹⁹ᵐTc-MDP for bone scans, ⁹⁹ᵐTc-sestamibi for cardiac perfusion).
    *   **Positron Emission Tomography (PET):** Uses positron emitters (¹⁸F, ¹¹C, ⁶⁸Ga). Provides high-resolution metabolic/functional images. **¹⁸F-FDG** is the most common tracer for oncology (imaging glucose metabolism).
2.  **Therapeutic Applications:**
    *   **Targeted Radionuclide Therapy:** Delivers radiation directly to diseased cells.
        *   **¹³¹I-Sodium Iodide:** Treatment of hyperthyroidism and thyroid cancer.
        *   **¹⁷⁷Lu/⁹⁰Y-labelled Peptides (e.g., DOTATATE):** For neuroendocrine tumors.
        *   **²²³Ra-Dichloride (Xofigo®):** Alpha emitter for bone metastases in prostate cancer.
    *   **Radiosynoviorthesis:** Intra-articular injection of β-emitters (e.g., ⁹⁰Y) to treat inflammatory arthritic joints.
3.  **Non-Imaging Diagnostic Use:**
    *   **Renal Function:** ⁹⁹ᵐTc-DTPA or ⁹⁹ᵐTc-MAG3 for glomerular filtration rate (GFR) measurement.
    *   **Schilling Test:** (Historically with ⁵⁸Co/⁵⁷Co-B12) for vitamin B12 absorption.

### **Key References & Source Material**
The content above is synthesized from the principles outlined in the following recommended texts:

1.  **Skoog, Holler & Nieman. *Principles of Instrumental Analysis*, 5th/6th Edition.** – Provides foundational principles of radiation detection, gamma spectrometry, and the physics behind radiochemical analysis.
2.  **Ewing, G.W. *Instrumental Methods of Chemical Analysis*, 5th Edition (1985).** – Contains detailed sections on radiochemical techniques, measurement of radioactivity, and principles of tracer methods.
3.  **The *United States Pharmacopeia (USP)* General Chapters <821> "Radiopharmaceuticals – Preparation, Compounding, Dispensing, and Repackaging" and <823> "Radiopharmaceuticals for Positron Emission Tomography – Compounding"** – These are the primary regulatory sources defining the stringent QC tests, analytical procedures, and release criteria for radiopharmaceuticals.
4.  **Specialized Nuclear Medicine Textbooks** (e.g., *The Chemistry of Radiopharmaceuticals*) – For in-depth chemistry of labeling and stability mechanisms.
