### **1.1 Titrimetric Methods (Volumetric Analysis)**
Titrimetry involves determining the concentration of an analyte by reacting it with a standard solution of known concentration (titrant). The point at which the reaction is complete is the **endpoint**, detected by an indicator or instrument.

#### **1.1.1 Acid-Base Titrations**
*   **Principle:** Neutralization reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.
    *   `Acid + Base → Salt + H₂O`
*   **Procedure:** A standard solution of strong acid (e.g., HCl) is used to titrate a base, or vice-versa.
*   **Indicators:** Change color at a specific pH.
    *   **Strong Acid-Strong Base:** Phenolphthalein (colorless → pink, pH 8.2-10.0) or Methyl Orange (red → yellow, pH 3.1-4.4).
    *   **Weak Acid-Strong Base:** Phenolphthalein.
    *   **Weak Base-Strong Acid:** Methyl Orange.
*   **Pharmaceutical Applications:**
    *   Assay of **aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid)** after hydrolysis to salicylic acid.
    *   Determination of the alkalinity of **bicarbonates**.
    *   Analysis of **amine salts** in drug formulations.

#### **1.1.2 Oxidation-Reduction Titrations (Redox Titrations)**
*   **Principle:** Involves transfer of electrons between the titrant (oxidizing agent) and the analyte (reducing agent), or vice-versa.
*   **Common Types:**
    1.  **Permanganometry:** Titrant = Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄). Self-indicating (pink endpoint). Used for assay of **hydrogen peroxide**, **iron(II) salts**.
    2.  **Iodimetry & Iodometry:**
        *   **Iodimetry:** Direct titration with iodine (I₂) solution. Analyte is a reducing agent (e.g., Vitamin C/Ascorbic acid). Starch indicator (blue-black endpoint).
        *   **Iodometry:** Indirect method. Analyte is an oxidizing agent which liberates iodine from KI; the liberated I₂ is then titrated with sodium thiosulphate (Na₂S₂O₃). Used for analysis of **chlorine**, **copper salts**, and **peroxides**.
    3.  **Cerimetry:** Titrant = Ceric ammonium sulphate. Used for drugs like **ferrous sulphate** and some organic compounds.

#### **1.1.3 Complexometric Titrations**
*   **Principle:** Formation of a stable, water-soluble complex between a metal ion (analyte) and a complexing agent (titrant).
*   **Titrant:** **EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid)**. It forms 1:1 complexes with most di-, tri-, and tetravalent metal ions.
*   **Indicators:** Metal ion indicators (e.g., Eriochrome Black T, Murexide) which themselves are complexing agents and change color when free metal ions are sequestered by EDTA.
*   **Pharmaceutical Applications:**
    *   Determination of **calcium** and **magnesium** in antacids or mineral supplements.
    *   Assay of **zinc** in ointments.
    *   Analysis of **aluminum** in gels.

### **1.2 Gravimetric Methods**
*   **Principle:** The analyte is converted into an insoluble precipitate of known composition. This precipitate is filtered, washed, dried/ignited to constant weight, and the mass is used to calculate the amount of the original analyte.
*   **Key Steps:**
    1.  **Precipitation:** The analyte is precipitated under conditions favoring pure, filterable crystals (e.g., by controlling pH, temperature, and rate of addition).
    2.  **Digestion:** The precipitate is heated in the mother liquor to improve particle size and purity (Ostwald ripening).
    3.  **Filtration & Washing:** To remove impurities.
    4.  **Drying/Ignition:** To convert the precipitate into a stable, weighable form.
*   **Pharmaceutical Applications:**
    *   Determination of **sulphate as barium sulphate (BaSO₄)**.
    *   Assay of **chloride as silver chloride (AgCl)**.
    *   **Ash Content** determination of herbal drugs (inorganic residue after combustion).
    *   **Limitation:** Time-consuming, requires careful technique to avoid co-precipitation of impurities.

### **1.3 Solvent Extraction Methods (Liquid-Liquid Extraction)**
*   **Principle:** Based on the **Nernst Distribution Law**: A solute distributes itself between two immiscible solvents so that the ratio of its concentrations is constant at a given temperature.
    *   **Partition Coefficient (K) = [C]₁ / [C]₂**, where [C]₁ and [C]₂ are concentrations in solvent 1 and 2 at equilibrium.
*   **Theory:** The efficiency of extraction depends on K and the volume ratio of solvents. Multiple extractions with small volumes are more efficient than a single extraction with a large volume.
*   **Common Solvent Pairs:** Water-Organic solvent (e.g., Ether, Chloroform, Ethyl acetate).
*   **Pharmaceutical Applications:**
    1.  **Isolation of Active Constituents:** Extraction of **alkaloids** (e.g., from cinchona bark) from aqueous alkaline medium into an organic solvent.
    2.  **Purification:** Removing impurities or separating mixtures based on differential solubility.
    3.  **Concentration:** Enriching a trace analyte from a large aqueous volume into a smaller organic phase for analysis.
    4.  **Drug Assay:** Used as a preliminary step in the assay of drugs like **steroids** or **vitamins** before spectrophotometric or chromatographic analysis. For example, extracting fat-soluble vitamins from an emulsion.
    5.  **Bioanalysis:** Extraction of drugs and metabolites from biological fluids (plasma, urine) prior to HPLC or GC analysis.

### **Key References & Source Material**
The content above is synthesized and explained based on the principles outlined in the following recommended texts:

1.  **Watson, D.G. *Pharmaceutical Analysis* (1999).** – For foundational principles of titrimetry, gravimetry, and their pharmaceutical applications.
2.  **Vogel’s *Textbook of Quantitative Organic Chemistry*, 6th Ed. (2006).** – For detailed procedures, theory of redox and complexometric titrations, and practical aspects of gravimetric analysis.
3.  **Pott, L.W. *Quantitative Analysis: Theory and Practice* (1987).** – For in-depth theoretical treatment of equilibrium in acid-base and complexometric systems, and solvent extraction theory.
4.  **General Pharmaceutical Chemistry Principles** from standard pharmacopoeias (USP/BP) which underpin the official assay methods for many drugs using these techniques.
