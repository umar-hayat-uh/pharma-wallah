# Sealing the Dose: The Science of Encapsulation & Coating

Two of the most visually recognizable dosage forms are capsules and coated tablets. While one is a container and the other is a coated core, both processes—**encapsulation** and **coating**—are sophisticated unit operations designed to deliver medication effectively, stably, and palatably. They represent the final stages of assembly and finishing in solid dosage form manufacturing.

##  1: Encapsulation – The Precision Container

Encapsulation involves enclosing a drug formulation—be it powder, granules, pellets, or even liquid—within a soluble shell, primarily made of gelatin. It offers superior bioavailability for many drugs (as the shell ruptures quickly) and is ideal for masking unpleasant tastes or odors.

### A Tale of Two Capsules: Hard vs. Soft Gelatin

#### **Hard Gelatin Capsules (Two-Piece)**
*   **Composition:** The shell is made of relatively rigid, pre-formed gelatin (often with additives like colorants or opacifiers) in two parts: a **body** and a slightly longer **cap**.
*   **Manufacturing:** Gelatin solution is prepared, colored, and then dipped onto precision stainless steel pins (molds). The dipped pins are rotated through a series of drying kilns to form hard, uniform shells. The bodies and caps are trimmed, removed from the pins, and joined loosely.
*   **Filling:** The two pieces are separated. The body is filled with the formulation, and the cap is pressed back on. The joint is often sealed (e.g., with a gelatin band) for tamper-evidence or to lock in low-moisture contents.

#### **Soft Gelatin Capsules (One-Piece)**
*   **Composition:** The shell is plasticized gelatin, making it flexible and hermetically sealable. It contains more moisture and often a preservative like sorbitol or glycerin.
*   **Manufacturing & Filling – A Simultaneous Process:** This occurs in one step on a **rotary die machine**. Two continuous gelatin ribbons are fed from overhead tanks. As the ribbons converge between a pair of rotating dies, a metered volume of liquid or paste fill material is injected between them. The dies cut and hermetically seal the gelatin around the fill, forming a single, seamless capsule. This is ideal for **oils, suspensions, or potent drugs**.

### Capsule Filling Machines: The Heart of Production

For hard gelatin capsules, high-speed filling is achieved by automated machines that perform several sequential operations:

1.  **Rectification:** Capsules are oriented all in the same direction (body down, cap up).
2.  **Separation:** The cap is lifted from the body.
3.  **Filling:** The open body passes under a dosator that fills it. Key methods include:
    *   **Auger Fill:** A simple screw feeds powder (suited for low-speed machines).
    *   **Tamping Pins (Dosator):** Pins dip into a powder bed, compacting a "plug" into a bore, which is then ejected into the capsule body. This ensures better weight uniformity for powders.
    *   **Vibratory Fill:** Used for free-flowing granules or pellets.
4.  **Joining & Ejection:** The cap is replaced on the filled body, and the closed capsule is ejected. Sealing or polishing may follow.

**Critical Quality Control for Capsules:**
*   **Weight Variation:** The most critical test, as it directly correlates with dose uniformity. Tight control of powder flow and fill mechanism is essential.
*   **Content Uniformity:** Ensures the active ingredient is evenly distributed.
*   **Disintegration & Dissolution:** Capsules must release their contents promptly.
*   **Moisture Content:** Crucial for physical stability (too dry=brittle, too wet=deformed).

##  2: Tablet Coating – The Functional Finish

Coating applies a thin, uniform layer to tablet cores, serving multiple critical functions beyond simple color.

### The Three Main Types of Coatings

#### 1. Sugar Coating (The Traditional Art)
*   **Process:** A multi-stage, labor-intensive process in traditional coating pans involving sealing, sub-coating, smoothing, coloring, and polishing.
*   **Purpose:** Primarily to mask taste/odor and improve appearance. Creates a thick, elegant finish.
*   **Drawback:** Significantly increases tablet weight (30-50%), obscures any embossed identification, and is nearly obsolete in modern pharmaceutical manufacturing due to inefficiency.

#### 2. Film Coating (The Modern Workhorse)
*   **Process:** Spraying a thin polymer solution (typically 2-5% of tablet weight) onto tumbling tablets in a pan or fluidized bed.
*   **Coating Formulation:** Contains:
    *   **Polymer** (e.g., Hydroxypropyl methylcellulose - HPMC, Povidone): Forms the film.
    *   **Plasticizer** (e.g., PEG, glycerin): Prevents film cracking.
    *   **Pigments/Opacifiers** (e.g., titanium dioxide): For color and hiding power.
    *   **Solvent** (Water or organic): Vehicle for application.
*   **Purpose:** **Taste masking, moisture protection, color for branding, improved swallowability.** It is fast, efficient, and doesn't obscure tablet markings.

#### 3. Enteric Coating (The Acid Shield)
*   **Process:** A specialized type of film coating using **pH-sensitive polymers** (e.g., Cellulose Acetate Phthalate - CAP, Methacrylic acid copolymers).
*   **Purpose:** To **prevent drug release in the acidic stomach.** The coating remains intact at gastric pH (~1-3) but dissolves rapidly in the higher pH of the small intestine (pH >5.5). This is used to:
    *   Protect acid-sensitive drugs (e.g., proton pump inhibitors like omeprazole).
    *   Prevent gastric irritation from drugs (e.g., aspirin, NSAIDs).
    *   Target drug release to the intestine.

### Coating Equipment & The Science of the Spray

Modern coating is a controlled balance of **atomization, deposition, spreading, and drying**.

**Primary Equipment:**
*   **Perforated Coating Pan (e.g., Accela-Cota):** The industry standard. Tablets tumble in a rotating drum with perforated walls. Heated air is drawn through the tablet bed for efficient drying while coating solution is sprayed onto the surface. This provides excellent mixing and drying efficiency.
*   **Fluidized Bed Coater:** Tablets are suspended by an upward stream of air, and coating is sprayed on. Extremely efficient for heat and mass transfer, ideal for heat-sensitive products or functional coatings on pellets.

**Critical Process Parameters:**
*   **Spray Rate:** Must be balanced with drying capacity to avoid over-wetting and tablet sticking.
*   **Airflow & Temperature:** Control the evaporation rate of the solvent. Optimal temperature ensures film formation without damaging the core.
*   **Pan Speed:** Governs tablet tumbling and mixing for uniformity.
*   **Atomization Air Pressure:** Creates the spray droplet size. Fine droplets give a smooth coat; too fine can cause spray drying (loss to exhaust).
