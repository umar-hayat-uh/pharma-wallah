## Unit 6: Micromeritics

### Introduction and Definition

Micromeritics is the science and technology of small particles. The term encompasses the study of the fundamental and derived properties of individual particles and their assemblies, including particle size, particle size distribution, particle shape, surface area, porosity, packing properties, and flow behavior . Knowledge of micromeritics is essential in pharmaceutical sciences because the behavior of powders and granular materials directly influences manufacturing processes, dosage form performance, and therapeutic efficacy.

---

### Particle Size and Its Distribution

#### Particle Size Concepts

Particle size is a linear dimension used to characterize the size of a particle. However, most pharmaceutical particles are irregular in shape, making the assignment of a single dimension challenging. Therefore, various equivalent diameters are used to describe particle size based on the measurement principle .

**Common Equivalent Diameters**:

| Diameter Type | Definition | Measurement Method |
|---------------|------------|---------------------|
| **Volume diameter** | Diameter of a sphere having the same volume as the particle | Electrical sensing zone, laser diffraction |
| **Surface diameter** | Diameter of a sphere having the same surface area as the particle | Permeametry, adsorption |
| **Projected area diameter** | Diameter of a sphere having the same projected area as the particle when viewed normal to a plane | Microscopy, image analysis |
| **Feret's diameter** | Distance between two parallel tangents on opposite sides of the particle | Microscopy |
| **Martin's diameter** | Length of the chord that divides the particle's projected area into two equal halves | Microscopy |
| **Stokes' diameter** | Diameter of a sphere having the same terminal settling velocity as the particle in a fluid | Sedimentation, elutriation |
| **Aerodynamic diameter** | Diameter of a unit-density sphere having the same terminal settling velocity as the particle in air | Cascade impaction, aerosol studies |

#### Particle Size Distribution

A powder sample typically contains particles of various sizes. The particle size distribution (PSD) describes the relative amounts of particles present according to size. PSD can be represented as:

- **Frequency distribution**: Number or mass of particles in each size class
- **Cumulative distribution**: Total percentage of particles below or above a certain size

**Important Distribution Parameters**:

| Parameter | Definition | Significance |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| **D10** | Size below which 10% of the particles (by volume or number) fall | Indicates fines content |
| **D50** | Median particle size; size below which 50% of particles fall | Central tendency |
| **D90** | Size below which 90% of particles fall | Indicates coarse fraction |
| **Span** | (D90 - D10)/D50 | Measure of distribution width |
| **Uniformity** | Degree of similarity in particle sizes | Affects packing and flow |

**Types of Distributions**:
- **Monomodal**: Single peak
- **Bimodal**: Two distinct peaks
- **Polymodal**: Multiple peaks

**Distribution Basis**:
- **Number distribution**: Count of particles in each size class
- **Volume (mass) distribution**: Volume or mass contribution of particles in each size class

Conversion between number and volume distributions requires assumptions about particle shape and density.

### Texture and Morphological Characteristics

#### Particle Shape

Particle shape significantly influences powder behavior. Common shape descriptors include:

| Shape Term | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Spherical** | Rounded, equal dimensions |
| **Rounded** | Somewhat spherical but not perfect |
| **Acicular** | Needle-shaped |
| **Tabular** | Plate-like, flat |
| **Flaky** | Thin, flat plates |
| **Irregular** | No symmetry |
| **Fibrous** | Thread-like |
| **Dendritic** | Branched, tree-like |

**Quantitative Shape Factors**:

| Factor | Definition | Formula |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Aspect ratio** | Ratio of longest to shortest dimension | Length/Width |
| **Circularity** | How close projected area resembles a circle | (4π × Area) / Perimeter² |
| **Roundness** | Sharpness of corners and edges | (Average radius of corners) / (Radius of inscribed circle) |
| **Sphericity (ψ)** | Ratio of surface area of sphere of equal volume to actual surface area | ψ = (Surface area of sphere of same volume) / (Actual surface area) |

#### Surface Characteristics

**Surface Roughness**:
- Microscale irregularities on particle surface
- Affects interparticle friction, flow, and adhesion
- Can be quantified by profilometry or atomic force microscopy

**Porosity**:
- Presence of pores within particles
- Intraparticle porosity affects density, dissolution, and adsorption
- Measured by mercury intrusion porosimetry or gas adsorption

**Crystallinity**:
- Crystalline particles have ordered internal structure
- Amorphous regions may exist on surfaces
- Affects surface energy and interaction with excipients

### Role and Importance of Micromeritics in Pharmacy

Particle size and related properties influence virtually every aspect of pharmaceutical development and performance.

#### 1. Dissolution and Bioavailability

According to the Noyes-Whitney equation, dissolution rate is directly proportional to the surface area of the drug. Reducing particle size increases surface area, thereby enhancing dissolution rate and potentially improving bioavailability of poorly soluble drugs.

**Biopharmaceutics Classification System (BCS) Implications**:
- **Class II (low solubility, high permeability)**: Particle size reduction is a key strategy to enhance absorption
- **Class IV (low solubility, low permeability)**: Particle size reduction may help but other strategies often needed

#### 2. Content Uniformity

In solid dosage forms (tablets, capsules), particle size affects the homogeneity of powder blends. Large particle size differences between drug and excipients can lead to segregation and content uniformity problems.

**Requirement**: To ensure uniform drug distribution, the drug particle size should be small enough and the particle size distribution narrow to prevent demixing during processing.

#### 3. Flow Properties

Powder flow is critical for successful manufacturing operations such as hopper discharge, die filling, and capsule filling. Particle size, shape, and surface characteristics directly influence flow:

- Larger particles (>200 μm) generally flow well
- Fine particles (<100 μm) tend to be cohesive and flow poorly
- Spherical particles flow better than irregular or needle-shaped particles
- Surface roughness increases interparticle friction, reducing flow

#### 4. Suspension Stability

In pharmaceutical suspensions, particle size affects sedimentation rate (Stokes' law), redispersibility, and physical stability. Smaller particles sediment more slowly but may form compact cakes if too fine. Optimal particle size (typically 1-50 μm) balances stability and redispersibility.

#### 5. Inhalation Therapy

For pulmonary delivery, particle size determines the site of deposition within the respiratory tract:

| Particle Size | Deposition Site |
|---------------|------------------|
| >10 μm | Mouth and throat (oropharyngeal) |
| 5-10 μm | Upper airways (tracheobronchial) |
| 1-5 μm | Deep lung (alveolar) |
| <1 μm | May be exhaled |

#### 6. Parenteral Formulations

Injectable suspensions and emulsions require strict particle size control to avoid capillary blockade and ensure safety. Particulate matter limits are specified in pharmacopoeias.

#### 7. Tablet Compaction

Particle size affects compressibility, tablet strength, and disintegration. Fine particles may produce harder tablets but can also lead to capping or lamination. Optimal particle size distribution improves compaction properties.

#### 8. Coating Processes

Particle size influences the efficiency and uniformity of coating operations. Size distribution affects the surface area to be coated and the amount of coating material required.

#### 9. Stability

Particle size can affect chemical and physical stability. Smaller particles have higher surface energy and may be more reactive (oxidation, hydrolysis). In suspensions, particle growth (Ostwald ripening) occurs when particle size distribution is wide.

#### 10. Taste Masking

Particle size reduction can increase the perception of bitter taste due to increased surface area and faster dissolution on the tongue. Conversely, larger particles or coated particles may reduce taste issues.

### Methods of Particle Size Analysis

Numerous techniques are available for particle size measurement, each with its own principles, advantages, and limitations. Selection depends on particle size range, sample characteristics, and information required.

#### 1. Sieving

**Principle**:
Sieving is the oldest and most widely used method for particle size analysis. Particles are separated by passing them through a series of woven-wire sieves with progressively smaller openings. The mass retained on each sieve is weighed, and the size distribution is calculated.

**Sieve Types**:
- **Woven-wire sieves**: Standard mesh sizes (e.g., ASTM, BS, ISO)
- **Electroformed sieves**: Precision micro-sieves for finer sizes (down to 5 μm)

**Procedure**:
1. Stack sieves with the largest opening on top
2. Place a known weight of powder on the top sieve
3. Agitate (mechanically or by air jet) for a specified time
4. Weigh the material retained on each sieve and the pan

**Advantages**:
- Simple, inexpensive, and reproducible
- Covers a wide size range (20 μm to 125 mm)
- Provides both size distribution and physical fractions for further testing
- No complex calibration required

**Disadvantages**:
- Lower limit ~20-50 μm (sieves smaller than 20 μm are fragile and clog easily)
- Time-consuming
- Not suitable for cohesive or very fine powders
- Shape-dependent: elongated particles may pass end-on or be retained

**Applications**:
- Quality control of granules and coarse powders
- Determination of particle size distribution for tableting blends
- USP/EP method for many pharmacopoeial tests

#### 2. Microscopy

**Principle**:
Microscopy allows direct visualization and measurement of individual particles. Particles are dispersed on a slide, and their dimensions are measured using a calibrated eyepiece graticule or image analysis software.

**Types**:
- **Optical microscopy**: Suitable for particles >0.5 μm
- **Electron microscopy (SEM, TEM)**: For submicron and nanoscale particles

**Measurement Parameters**:
- Projected area diameter, Feret's diameter, Martin's diameter
- Shape assessment (aspect ratio, circularity)
- Agglomeration state

**Advantages**:
- Direct measurement of size and shape
- Ability to assess particle morphology, agglomeration, and contamination
- Small sample size required
- Absolute method (no calibration needed for size)

**Disadvantages**:
- Limited number of particles counted (statistically less representative)
- Time-consuming and operator-dependent
- Requires careful sample preparation to avoid bias
- 2D projection of 3D particles

**Automated Image Analysis**:
Modern systems combine microscopy with digital image processing, enabling rapid measurement of thousands of particles and providing comprehensive size and shape data.

#### 3. Sedimentation Methods

**Principle**:
Particles suspended in a fluid settle at rates dependent on their size (Stokes' law). Larger particles settle faster. The change in concentration or mass of sediment with time is used to determine size distribution.

**Stokes' Law**:
v = (d² (ρ_p - ρ_f) g) / (18 η)

where:
- v = terminal settling velocity
- d = Stokes' diameter
- ρ_p, ρ_f = particle and fluid densities
- g = gravitational acceleration
- η = fluid viscosity

**Techniques**:
- **Gravitational sedimentation**: Particles settle under gravity; suitable for 1-100 μm
- **Centrifugal sedimentation**: Centrifugal force accelerates settling for submicron particles (0.01-10 μm)
- **Pipette method** (Andreasen pipette): Samples withdrawn at specific depths and times
- **Sedimentation balance**: Weight of sediment collected on a pan recorded over time

**Advantages**:
- Measures Stokes' diameter, relevant to suspension behavior
- Relatively inexpensive equipment
- Can provide mass-based distribution

**Disadvantages**:
- Time-consuming (especially for fine particles)
- Requires density information and dispersion stability
- Assumes spherical particles and laminar flow
- Not suitable for particles that interact or agglomerate

#### 4. Laser Diffraction (Low-Angle Laser Light Scattering, LALLS)

**Principle**:
Particles passing through a laser beam scatter light at angles inversely proportional to their size. The angular intensity distribution of scattered light is measured by detectors and converted to a particle size distribution using Mie theory or Fraunhofer approximation.

**Size Range**: 0.01 μm to several millimeters (depending on instrument configuration)

**Modes**:
- **Wet dispersion**: Particles suspended in a liquid (with or without surfactant/sonication)
- **Dry dispersion**: Particles dispersed in air stream

**Advantages**:
- Rapid (measurement in seconds)
- Wide dynamic range
- Excellent reproducibility
- Requires minimal sample preparation
- Suitable for both wet and dry samples
- Can be automated for high throughput

**Disadvantages**:
- Results depend on optical model (refractive index, absorption)
- Assumes spherical particles; shape affects accuracy
- Cannot distinguish between individual particles and agglomerates without proper dispersion
- Relatively expensive instrumentation

**Applications**:
- Routine quality control of pharmaceutical powders
- Characterization of inhalation products
- Monitoring milling and micronization processes

#### 5. Electrical Sensing Zone (Coulter Principle)

**Principle**:
Particles suspended in an electrolyte solution are drawn through a small aperture. As each particle passes, it displaces its own volume of electrolyte, causing a change in electrical resistance proportional to particle volume. The number and magnitude of pulses are counted and sized.

**Size Range**: 0.4 μm to 1200 μm (aperture-dependent)

**Advantages**:
- Measures absolute number and volume of individual particles
- High resolution and accuracy
- Not dependent on optical properties
- Provides both number and volume distributions

**Disadvantages**:
- Requires conductive electrolyte
- Aperture blockage possible with large or agglomerated particles
- Limited to particles that are insoluble in the electrolyte
- Relatively slow compared to laser diffraction

**Applications**:
- Counting and sizing of parenteral particulates
- Blood cell counting (hematology analyzers)
- Calibration standard measurements

#### 6. Photon Correlation Spectroscopy (PCS) / Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS)

**Principle**:
Particles in suspension undergo Brownian motion. The intensity fluctuations of scattered laser light are analyzed to determine the diffusion coefficient, from which particle size (hydrodynamic diameter) is calculated using the Stokes-Einstein equation.

**Size Range**: 0.3 nm to 10 μm (optimal for submicron and nanoparticles)

**Advantages**:
- Suitable for nanoparticles and macromolecules
- Rapid measurement
- Small sample volume
- High sensitivity

**Disadvantages**:
- Intensity-weighted distribution (converted to volume/number using assumptions)
- Cannot resolve multimodal distributions well
- Sensitive to dust and aggregates
- Requires knowledge of viscosity and temperature

**Applications**:
- Nanoparticle formulations (liposomes, polymeric nanoparticles, nanocrystals)
- Protein aggregation studies
- Quality control of nano-sized drug delivery systems

#### 7. Acoustic Spectroscopy

**Principle**:
Ultrasonic waves are passed through a particle suspension. The attenuation and velocity of sound are measured as functions of frequency, and particle size distribution is derived from acoustic theory.

**Advantages**:
- Can measure concentrated suspensions (up to 50% v/v) without dilution
- No dilution artifacts
- Suitable for opaque systems

**Disadvantages**:
- Complex data interpretation
- Requires knowledge of physical properties (density, etc.)
- Less common in pharmaceutical labs

#### 8. Cascade Impaction

**Principle**:
Particles in an air stream are separated based on their aerodynamic diameter by passing through a series of stages with decreasing jet sizes. Particles inertially impact on collection plates at each stage depending on their size.

**Size Range**: 0.1 μm to 30 μm

**Advantages**:
- Measures aerodynamic diameter, directly relevant to inhalation
- Provides mass-based distribution
- Pharmacopoeial method for inhalation products

**Disadvantages**:
- Time-consuming and labor-intensive
- Requires chemical assay of drug collected on each stage
- Limited resolution

**Applications**:
- Characterization of metered-dose inhalers (MDIs)
- Dry powder inhalers (DPIs)
- Nebulizers

#### 9. Permeametry (Air Permeability)

**Principle**:
The resistance of a packed powder bed to fluid flow is measured and related to the surface area (or average particle size) using Kozeny-Carman equation. Not a true particle size distribution method; gives specific surface area.

**Advantages**:
- Simple and rapid
- Inexpensive
- Measures surface area relevant to dissolution

**Disadvantages**:
- Assumes uniform packing and spherical particles
- Provides only average size (not distribution)
- Affected by particle shape and porosity

**Applications**:
- Quality control for materials where surface area is critical

#### Comparison of Particle Size Analysis Methods

| Method | Size Range | Basis | Output | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|------------|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| Sieving | >20 μm | Mass | Mass distribution | Simple, inexpensive, fractions obtained | Limited to coarse particles, shape bias |
| Microscopy | >0.5 μm | Number | Number distribution, shape | Direct visualization, absolute | Small sample size, time-consuming |
| Sedimentation | 0.1-100 μm | Mass (Stokes) | Mass distribution | Relevant to suspensions | Slow, density required |
| Laser diffraction | 0.01-3500 μm | Volume | Volume distribution | Rapid, wide range, reproducible | Optical properties needed, shape bias |
| Electrical sensing zone | 0.4-1200 μm | Volume | Number and volume | High resolution, absolute | Electrolyte required, aperture blockage |
| DLS | 0.3 nm-10 μm | Intensity | Intensity distribution | Nanoparticle sizing | Poor for multimodal, dust sensitive |
| Cascade impaction | 0.1-30 μm | Mass (aerodynamic) | Mass distribution | Relevant to inhalation | Laborious, assay required |

### Flow Properties of Powders

Powder flow is a critical property affecting manufacturing processes such as hopper discharge, die filling, tablet compression, and capsule filling. Poor flow can lead to weight variation, content non-uniformity, and production downtime.

#### Factors Affecting Powder Flow

| Factor | Influence |
|--------|-----------|
| **Particle size** | Larger particles (>200 μm) flow well; fines (<100 μm) are cohesive |
| **Particle shape** | Spherical flow best; irregular, needle, or flaky particles interlock |
| **Surface texture** | Smooth surfaces flow better than rough |
| **Moisture content** | Increased moisture increases cohesion (capillary forces) |
| **Electrostatic charge** | Static can cause aggregation or wall adhesion |
| **Density** | Higher density may improve flow (gravity-driven) |
| **Hygroscopicity** | Moisture uptake alters surface properties |
| **Additives (glidants)** | Small amounts (0.1-2%) of fine powders (silica, talc) can improve flow |

#### Quantitative Assessment of Flow Properties

**1. Angle of Repose (θ)**

**Definition**: The maximum angle that can be formed between the free surface of a powder heap and the horizontal plane. It reflects interparticle friction and cohesion.

**Measurement**: Powder is poured through a funnel onto a horizontal surface, and the height (h) and radius (r) of the conical heap are measured.

**Calculation**: tan θ = h / r

**Interpretation**:

| Angle of Repose (degrees) | Flow Character |
|---------------------------|-----------------|
| <25 | Excellent |
| 25-30 | Good |
| 30-40 | Passable |
| 40-45 | Cohesive (poor) |
| >45 | Very cohesive (very poor) |

**Limitations**:
- Sensitive to method (funnel diameter, height, base material)
- Not reliable for very cohesive powders that do not form a stable heap
- Subjective interpretation

**2. Carr's Index (Compressibility Index)**

**Definition**: Measures the relative difference between tapped and bulk densities, reflecting the powder's tendency to consolidate.

**Measurement**:
- **Bulk density (ρ_bulk)**: Mass of powder divided by unsettled apparent volume
- **Tapped density (ρ_tapped)**: Mass divided by volume after mechanical tapping (standardized number of taps, e.g., 500 or 1250)

**Calculation**:
Carr's Index (%) = [(ρ_tapped - ρ_bulk) / ρ_tapped] × 100

**Interpretation**:

| Carr's Index (%) | Flow Character |
|------------------|-----------------|
| <10 | Excellent |
| 11-15 | Good |
| 16-20 | Fair |
| 21-25 | Passable |
| 26-31 | Poor |
| 32-37 | Very poor |
| >38 | Extremely poor |

**3. Hausner's Ratio**

**Definition**: Ratio of tapped density to bulk density. It indicates interparticle friction.

**Calculation**:
Hausner's Ratio = ρ_tapped / ρ_bulk

**Interpretation**:

| Hausner's Ratio | Flow Character |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| <1.25 | Good flow |
| 1.25-1.4 | Moderate flow |
| >1.4 | Poor flow (cohesive) |

**Relationship**: Carr's Index and Hausner's Ratio are related; both derived from the same density measurements.

**4. Flow Rate through an Orifice**

**Definition**: Time required for a specified mass of powder to flow through a standardized funnel orifice.

**Measurement**: Powder is placed in a funnel with a specific orifice diameter, and the time for complete discharge is measured.

**Interpretation**:
- Shorter time = better flow
- Powders that do not flow (arching, rat-holing) are considered very cohesive

**Limitations**:
- Not all powders will flow through an orifice
- Orifice diameter must be chosen appropriately (typically >6× particle diameter)
- Sensitive to funnel geometry and operator technique

**5. Shear Cell Testing**

**Principle**: Measures the shear stress required to initiate flow in a consolidated powder bed under a given normal stress. Provides fundamental parameters like cohesion, angle of internal friction, and flow function.

**Parameters**:
- **Flow function coefficient (ffc)**: Ratio of consolidation stress to unconfined yield strength
- **Classification**:
  - ffc < 1: Not flowing
  - 1 < ffc < 2: Very cohesive
  - 2 < ffc < 4: Cohesive
  - 4 < ffc < 10: Easy flowing
  - ffc > 10: Free-flowing

**Advantages**:
- More fundamental and reproducible than empirical tests
- Applicable to cohesive powders
- Provides data for hopper design

**Disadvantages**:
- More complex and expensive equipment
- Requires skilled operator

#### Pharmaceutical Significance of Flow Properties

| Process | Flow Requirement |
|---------|------------------|
| **Hopper discharge** | Must flow without arching or flooding |
| **Tablet compression (die filling)** | Uniform, rapid filling to ensure weight consistency |
| **Capsule filling** | Consistent flow into dosing chamber |
| **Mixing** | Good flow promotes blend uniformity; poor flow may cause segregation |
| **Packaging** | Flow into containers must be consistent |

#### Improving Flow Properties

| Method | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Granulation** | Agglomerates fine particles into larger, free-flowing granules |
| **Addition of glidants** | Fine particles (0.1-2%) coat surfaces, reduce interparticle friction (e.g., colloidal silica, talc, starch) |
| **Spheronization** | Rounds irregular particles |
| **Controlled particle size distribution** | Optimize to minimize fines and achieve good packing |
| **Drying** | Reduce moisture content |
| **Anti-static agents** | Reduce electrostatic charging |
