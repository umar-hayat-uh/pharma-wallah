## Unit 5: Ionization and Buffers

### Strong vs. Weak Electrolytes

#### Definition and Classification of Electrolytes

An electrolyte is a substance (an acid, a base, or a salt) that in an aqueous solution ionizes to form positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions) . For example, sodium chloride (NaCl) in aqueous solution forms Na⁺ (a cation) and Cl⁻ (an anion), creating two independent entities per molecule .

Electrolytes exhibit three important properties that differentiate them from nonelectrolytes :
- They exhibit anomalous colligative properties compared with nonelectrolytes
- They can conduct an electrical current
- They tend to show rapid chemical reactions compared with nonelectrolyte solutions

**Nonelectrolytes** are substances that do not ionize in water at all and therefore do not conduct an electric current in solution. Examples include sucrose, fructose, urea, and glycerol .

#### Strong Electrolytes

**Definition**: Strong electrolytes are substances that are completely ionized in water . Their degree of ionization is pH-independent and essentially 100% under normal conditions.

**Characteristics**:
- Complete dissociation in aqueous solution
- High electrical conductivity
- Maximum colligative property effects (van't Hoff factor i equals number of ions)
- No equilibrium between ionized and unionized forms

**Examples of Strong Electrolytes**:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Strong acids | Hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), nitric acid (HNO₃) |
| Strong bases | Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), potassium hydroxide (KOH) |
| Salts | Sodium chloride (NaCl), potassium chloride (KCl), most soluble salts |

#### Weak Electrolytes

**Definition**: Weak electrolytes ionize only partly in water . Most drugs are weak electrolytes, including atropine, phenobarbital, and sulfadiazine .

**Characteristics**:
- Partial dissociation (typically <50%)
- Existence of equilibrium between ionized and unionized forms
- Degree of ionization depends on pH of the environment
- Lower electrical conductivity than strong electrolytes
- van't Hoff factor i less than theoretical maximum

**Examples of Weak Electrolytes**:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Weak acids | Acetic acid, benzoic acid, phenobarbital, aspirin |
| Weak bases | Ammonia, atropine, ephedrine, most alkaloids |
| Some salts | Mercuric chloride, lead acetate |

#### Pharmaceutical Significance of Electrolyte Classification

The distinction between strong and weak electrolytes is critically important in pharmacy because :

1. **Drug Absorption**: Many drugs are organic weak electrolytes. Their degree of ionization is an important physicochemical property with extensive physiologic implications . Depending on the pH of the environment, weak electrolytes can exist in either ionized or unionized forms, which dramatically affects their ability to cross biological membranes.

2. **Formulation Development**: The ionization behavior determines appropriate pH adjustments, buffer selection, and salt form decisions.

3. **Stability Considerations**: Ionized and unionized forms may have different chemical stability profiles.

4. **Solubility**: Weak electrolytes show pH-dependent solubility, which can be exploited in formulation design.

### pH, pKa, and Buffer Systems

#### The pH Scale

pH is defined as the negative logarithm (base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration:

**pH = -log [H⁺]**

The pH scale typically ranges from 0 to 14:
- pH < 7: Acidic solution
- pH = 7: Neutral solution
- pH > 7: Alkaline (basic) solution

#### Ionization Constants (Ka and pKa)

**Acid Ionization Constant (Ka)**:
For a weak acid (HA) that ionizes as: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻

The equilibrium constant is: **Ka = [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA]**

**Base Ionization Constant (Kb)**:
For a weak base (B) that ionizes as: B + H₂O ⇌ BH⁺ + OH⁻

The equilibrium constant is: **Kb = [BH⁺][OH⁻] / [B]**

**pKa and pKb**:
pKa = -log Ka
pKb = -log Kb

The pKa value indicates the pH at which a drug is 50% ionized and 50% unionized.

#### Relationship Between pKa, pKb, and pKw

For a conjugate acid-base pair: **pKa + pKb = pKw = 14** (at 25°C)

Where pKw is the negative logarithm of the ion product of water (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C).

#### Factors Affecting pKa

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Molecular structure | Electron-withdrawing groups decrease pKa of acids; electron-donating groups increase pKa |
| Temperature | pKa values change slightly with temperature |
| Ionic strength | High ionic strength can affect ionization constants |
| Solvent composition | Organic solvents alter pKa compared to water |

#### Buffer Systems

**Definition**: A buffer solution resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added or when dilution occurs .

**Components of a Buffer**:
A buffer consists of a mixture of :
- A weak acid and its conjugate base (salt of the weak acid), OR
- A weak base and its conjugate acid (salt of the weak base)

**Mechanism of Buffer Action**:

When acid (H⁺) is added to a buffer containing weak acid (HA) and its conjugate base (A⁻):
- The added H⁺ combines with A⁻ to form HA
- The pH change is minimized because H⁺ is removed from solution

When base (OH⁻) is added:
- The added OH⁻ reacts with HA to form A⁻ and H₂O
- The pH change is minimized because OH⁻ is consumed

**Pharmaceutical Applications of Buffers** :
- Most pharmaceutical solutions are pH-controlled using an appropriate buffer system
- Buffers maintain drug stability by preventing pH-induced degradation
- Buffers ensure patient comfort (e.g., ophthalmic solutions must have appropriate pH)
- Buffers optimize drug absorption by maintaining the drug in its absorbable form

### Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

#### Derivation of the Equation

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation is derived from the acid dissociation constant expression .

Starting with: Ka = [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA]

Rearranging: [H⁺] = Ka × [HA] / [A⁻]

Taking negative logarithms: -log [H⁺] = -log Ka - log ([HA]/[A⁻])

Therefore: **pH = pKa + log ([A⁻]/[HA])**

For a weak base: **pH = pKa + log ([B]/[BH⁺])**

Where pKa in this case refers to the pKa of the conjugate acid (BH⁺).

#### The Equation Forms

| System | Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| Weak acid and its salt | pH = pKa + log ([salt]/[acid]) |
| Weak base and its salt | pH = pKa + log ([base]/[salt]) |
| For any buffer | pH = pKa + log ([conjugate base]/[weak acid]) |

#### Applications in Drug Formulation

**1. Calculating Percent Ionization**

For weak acids: % ionized = 100 / [1 + 10^(pKa - pH)]
For weak bases: % ionized = 100 / [1 + 10^(pH - pKa)]

**2. Determining the Ratio of Ionized to Unionized Forms**

Rearranging the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:

For weak acids: [A⁻]/[HA] = 10^(pH - pKa)
For weak bases: [B]/[BH⁺] = 10^(pH - pKa)

**3. pH Partition Hypothesis**

The pH-partition hypothesis states that :
- The unionized form of a drug is preferentially absorbed across biological membranes
- The absorption rate depends on the fraction of unionized drug at the absorption site
- The lipid-water partition coefficient of the unionized form determines its membrane permeability

**Physiologic Implications** :
- Weak acids are better absorbed from acidic environments (stomach) where they are unionized
- Weak bases are better absorbed from basic environments (intestine) where they are unionized
- This principle applies to drug absorption, transport, and excretion

**4. Formulating Buffer Solutions**

To prepare a buffer with a specific pH:
- Select a weak acid with pKa close to desired pH (ideally within ±1 pH unit)
- Calculate required salt/acid ratio using Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
- Weigh appropriate amounts of weak acid and its salt

**Example Calculation**:
To prepare an acetic acid buffer at pH 5.0 (pKa of acetic acid = 4.76):

log ([salt]/[acid]) = pH - pKa = 5.0 - 4.76 = 0.24
[salt]/[acid] = 10^0.24 = 1.74

Thus, the ratio of sodium acetate to acetic acid should be 1.74:1.

#### Limitations of the Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

- Assumes ideal behavior (activity coefficients = 1)
- Most accurate for dilute solutions
- Does not account for ionic strength effects
- Limited accuracy at extremes of pH or concentration
- Does not consider temperature effects on pKa

### Buffer Capacity

#### Definition

Buffer capacity (β) is defined as the magnitude of the resistance of a buffer to pH changes . Quantitatively, it is the ratio of the increment of strong acid (or base) added to the change in pH produced:

**β = dC / dpH**

Where:
- β = buffer capacity
- dC = small increment of acid or base added (in equivalents per liter)
- dpH = corresponding small change in pH

#### Factors Affecting Buffer Capacity

| Factor | Effect on Buffer Capacity |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Concentration | Higher concentration = greater buffer capacity |
| Ratio of components | Maximum when [salt] = [acid] (ratio = 1:1) |
| pKa relative to pH | Maximum within ±1 pH unit of pKa |
| Temperature | Minor effect through pKa changes |
| Ionic strength | May affect activity coefficients |

#### Buffer Capacity Range

A buffer is most effective when the pH is within ±1 unit of the pKa of the weak acid . Outside this range, buffer capacity decreases significantly.

**Relative Buffer Capacity at Different Ratios**:

| [Salt]/[Acid] Ratio | pH relative to pKa | Relative Buffer Capacity |
|---------------------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| 10:1 | pKa + 1 | 0.48 |
| 5:1 | pKa + 0.7 | 0.65 |
| 2:1 | pKa + 0.3 | 0.90 |
| 1:1 | pKa | 1.00 (maximum) |
| 1:2 | pKa - 0.3 | 0.90 |
| 1:5 | pKa - 0.7 | 0.65 |
| 1:10 | pKa - 1 | 0.48 |

#### Pharmaceutical Significance of Buffer Capacity

Recent research has demonstrated the critical importance of buffer capacity in physiological systems. A study investigating drug precipitation in physiological bicarbonate buffer solutions (BCB) found that :

- **pH and buffer capacity affect drug precipitation**: As buffer capacity (β) was increased, drug precipitation in BCB became faster 

- **Physiological range**: In the small intestines, β values range from 2.2 to 17.6 mM/ΔpH 

- **Surface pH effects**: At pH values where a drug is ionizable, the surface pH of precipitating particles can differ from bulk pH due to slow hydration process of CO₂ 

- **Buffer selection matters**: Drug precipitation in phosphate buffer was faster than in bicarbonate buffer and less affected by pH and buffer capacity 

These findings emphasize that buffer capacity is not just a theoretical concept but has direct implications for drug performance in physiological environments.

### Hypo, Hyper, and Isotonic Solutions

#### Fundamental Concepts

**Tonicity** refers to the effect a solution has on cell volume when cells are immersed in the solution. It is determined by the concentration of non-penetrating solutes that cannot cross the cell membrane.

**Osmotic Pressure**: The pressure required to prevent the flow of solvent across a semipermeable membrane. The osmotic pressure relationship between solutions and biological fluids determines the isotonic nature of pharmaceutical preparations .

**Osmolarity vs. Tonicity**:
- **Osmolarity**: Total solute concentration expressed as osmoles per liter of solution (theoretical)
- **Tonicity**: Effective osmotic pressure exerted by non-penetrating solutes (practical)

#### Classification of Solutions Based on Tonicity

| Solution Type | Definition | Effect on Cells |
|---------------|------------|-----------------|
| **Isotonic** | Same effective osmotic pressure as body fluids | No net movement of water; cells maintain normal shape |
| **Hypotonic** | Lower effective osmotic pressure than body fluids | Water enters cells; cells swell and may lyse (hemolysis) |
| **Hypertonic** | Higher effective osmotic pressure than body fluids | Water leaves cells; cells shrink (crenation) |

#### Isotonic Solutions

**Definition**: Isotonic solutions have the same osmotic pressure as body fluids, particularly blood plasma and tears . When administered, they cause no net movement of water across cell membranes.

**Reference Values**:
- Blood plasma has osmotic pressure corresponding to 0.9% NaCl solution
- 0.9% NaCl is isotonic with blood and tissue fluids
- 5% dextrose solution is also isotonic (dextrose is metabolized, leaving hypotonic solution)

**Common Isotonic Pharmaceutical Solutions** :

| Solution | Composition | Characteristics |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| 0.9% Sodium Chloride (Normal Saline) | NaCl 9 g/L | Acidifying solution; high chloride content (154 mEq/L) |
| Lactated Ringer's Solution (LRS) | Na⁺ 130, K⁺ 4, Ca²⁺ 3, Cl⁻ 109, lactate 28 mEq/L | Alkalinizing; uses lactate as bicarbonate precursor |
| Normosol-R / Plasmalyte 148 | Contains acetate and gluconate as buffers | Alkalinizing; contains magnesium |
| 5% Dextrose in Water (D5W) | Dextrose 50 g/L | Initially isotonic; dextrose metabolized leaving free water |

**Clinical Applications of Isotonic Solutions** :
- Fluid resuscitation in shock
- Rehydration therapy
- Replacement of isotonic fluid losses
- Vehicle for drug administration

#### Hypotonic Solutions

**Definition**: Hypotonic solutions have lower osmotic pressure than body fluids .

**Examples** :
- 0.45% Sodium Chloride (half-strength saline)
- 0.45% Sodium Chloride with 2.5% Dextrose
- Normosol-M
- D5W (after dextrose metabolism)

**Characteristics** :
- Rapidly distribute within total body water
- Leave very little in intravascular space
- Used for replacing free water loss (hypernatremia)
- Used for maintenance fluid therapy

**Contraindications** :
- Should never be used for fluid resuscitation from shock
- Do not provide meaningful expansion of intravascular volume

#### Hypertonic Solutions

**Definition**: Hypertonic solutions have higher osmotic pressure than body fluids .

**Examples**:
- 3% Sodium Chloride
- 7% Sodium Chloride (Hypertonic Saline)
- 23% Sodium Chloride (MUST be diluted before use)
- Solutions with high dextrose concentrations (>5%)

**Characteristics of Hypertonic Saline (7% NaCl)** :
- Used for rapid expansion of intravascular volume
- Pulls fluid primarily from interstitial compartment
- Small volumes required to increase intravascular volume quickly
- Volume expansion is short-lived as sodium redistributes
- Anti-inflammatory effects documented

**Clinical Applications** :
- Treatment of head trauma patients
- Shock resuscitation
- Should be administered no faster than 1 mL/kg/min (risk of vagally mediated bradycardia)

#### Pharmaceutical Applications of Tonicity Control

**1. Parenteral Formulations**
- Must be isotonic to prevent pain at injection site
- Prevent hemolysis of red blood cells
- Ensure patient safety during IV administration

**2. Ophthalmic Preparations**
- Must be isotonic with tear fluid (equivalent to 0.9% NaCl)
- Prevent ocular tissue damage and discomfort
- Maintain corneal integrity

**3. Nasal Preparations**
- Isotonic solutions prevent ciliary damage
- Maintain normal mucociliary clearance

**4. Parenteral Nutrition**
- Tonicity must be considered for peripheral vs. central administration
- Hypertonic solutions require central venous access

#### Calculation Methods for Isotonic Solutions

**Method 1: Sodium Chloride Equivalent Method**

The sodium chloride equivalent (E-value) is the amount of sodium chloride that produces the same osmotic effect as 1 gram of the drug.

E-value = (Molecular weight of NaCl × i factor of drug) / (Molecular weight of drug × i factor of NaCl)

Where i is the van't Hoff factor (approximately 1.86 for NaCl, 1.0 for nonelectrolytes).

**Steps**:
1. Calculate the NaCl equivalent of all ingredients
2. Add these values to find total NaCl represented
3. Subtract from 0.9 g (NaCl required for 100 mL isotonic solution)
4. Add the difference as NaCl or other adjusting substance

**Method 2: Freezing Point Depression Method**

Solutions isotonic with blood and tears have freezing point depression of -0.52°C.

The amount of adjusting substance needed = (0.52 - a) / b

Where:
- a = freezing point depression of the drug solution
- b = freezing point depression of 1% adjusting substance

**Method 3: White-Vincent Method**

This method uses the volume of isotonic solution that can be prepared from a given amount of drug.

V = W × E × 111.1

Where:
- V = volume of isotonic solution that can be prepared (mL)
- W = weight of drug (g)
- E = sodium chloride equivalent
- 111.1 = volume of isotonic solution prepared by 1 g NaCl
