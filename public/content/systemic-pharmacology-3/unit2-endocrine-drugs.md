# PART 1: PANCREATIC HORMONES
## INSULIN

### Introduction and Physiology
Insulin is a polypeptide hormone produced by the β-cells of the pancreatic islets of Langerhans. It consists of two chains (A chain with 21 amino acids, B chain with 30 amino acids) linked by disulfide bridges .

**Biosynthesis Pathway:**
Preproinsulin → Proinsulin → Insulin + C-peptide

*Clinical Pearl:* C-peptide measurement reflects endogenous insulin production and is NOT affected by exogenous insulin administration .

### Normal Insulin Secretion Physiology
| Parameter | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Basal secretion** | Continuous low-level secretion; diurnal pattern (lowest midnight, highest early morning) |
| **Bolus secretion** | Biphasic response to meals: rapid first-phase + sustained second-phase |
| **Glucose sensing** | GLUT-2 transporters on β-cells; glucokinase acts as glucose sensor |
| **Stimulus-secretion coupling** | Glucose → ATP ↑ → K-ATP channels close → depolarization → Ca⁺⁺ influx → exocytosis |

### Pharmacologic Modification of Insulin
Insulin can be modified to alter pharmacokinetics :

| Modification | Effect | Examples |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Zinc buffer | Stabilizes into dimers/hexamers | Regular, NPH |
| Phosphate buffer | Stabilizes hexamers | NPH |
| Protamine addition | Slows dissociation | NPH, premixed insulins |
| Amino acid substitution | Alters dissociation rate | Lispro, Aspart, Glargine, Detemir |
| pH change | Acidic insulin aggregates | Glargine |
| Fatty acid acylation | Binds albumin, slows diffusion | Detemir, Degludec |

### Types of Insulin

#### Rapid-Acting Insulins (Analogues)
| Property | Insulin Lispro | Insulin Aspart | Insulin Glulisine |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Onset** | 5-15 minutes | 5-15 minutes | 5-15 minutes |
| **Peak** | 30-90 minutes | 30-90 minutes | 30-90 minutes |
| **Duration** | 3-5 hours | 3-5 hours | 3-5 hours |
| **Modification** | B28 proline→lysine, B29 lysine→proline | B28 proline→aspartic acid | B3 asparagine→lysine, B29 lysine→glutamic acid |
| **Administration** | Within 15 min before meals | Within 15 min before meals | Within 15 min before meals |

#### Short-Acting Insulin (Regular)
- **Onset:** 30-60 minutes
- **Peak:** 2-3 hours
- **Duration:** 6-8 hours
- **Administration:** Must be given 30-45 minutes before meals
- **Note:** Only regular and NPH remain available among original human insulins 

#### Intermediate-Acting Insulin (NPH)
- **Onset:** 1-4 hours
- **Peak:** 4-10 hours
- **Duration:** 10-16 hours
- **Properties:** Cloudy suspension; contains protamine and phosphate buffer
- **Uses:** Basal coverage (usually 2-3 times daily) or prandial coverage (dosed 4-6 hours before meal)

#### Long-Acting Insulins (Analogues)
| Property | Insulin Glargine | Insulin Detemir |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Onset** | 1-2 hours | 1-2 hours |
| **Peak** | Peakless | Minimal peak |
| **Duration** | ~24 hours | 12-24 hours (dose-dependent) |
| **Mechanism** | Precipitates at injection site (acidic pH); slow release | Binds to albumin via fatty acid side chain |
| **Administration** | Once daily (usually bedtime) | Once or twice daily |

#### Premixed Insulins
| Product | Composition | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Humulin 70/30 | 70% NPH + 30% Regular | 30-60 min | Dual | 10-16 hr |
| Humulin 50/50 | 50% NPH + 50% Regular | 30-60 min | Dual | 10-16 hr |
| Humalog 75/25 | 75% NPL + 25% Lispro | 5-15 min | Dual | 10-16 hr |
| Novolog 70/30 | 70% NPL + 30% Aspart | 5-15 min | Dual | 10-16 hr |

### Mechanism of Action
1. Insulin binds to insulin receptor (tyrosine kinase receptor)
2. Autophosphorylation and activation of receptor
3. Phosphorylation of insulin receptor substrates (IRS)
4. Activation of PI3K-Akt pathway and MAP kinase pathway
5. Translocation of GLUT4 transporters to cell membrane
6. Glucose uptake into cells (muscle, adipose)

**Metabolic Effects:**
- **Liver:** ↑ Glycogenesis, ↓ Gluconeogenesis, ↓ Glycogenolysis
- **Muscle:** ↑ Glucose uptake, ↑ Glycogen synthesis, ↑ Protein synthesis
- **Adipose:** ↑ Glucose uptake, ↑ Lipogenesis, ↓ Lipolysis

### Adverse Effects
- **Hypoglycemia:** Most common and serious 
- **Weight gain:** Especially with intensive therapy
- **Lipodystrophy:** At injection sites (lipohypertrophy or lipoatrophy)
- **Immunologic reactions:** Local or systemic (rare with human insulins)
- **Peripheral edema:** During intensive insulin therapy
- **Insulin resistance:** Particularly in type 2 diabetes

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Initiating insulin therapy:** Usually start with basal insulin, titrate, then add mealtime insulin 
2. **Correction scale:** Additional short-acting insulin for hyperglycemia
3. **Storage:** Do not freeze; protect from heat and light
4. **Mixing:** Clear before cloudy (Regular/NPH); analogues may have specific mixing guidelines

## GLUCAGON

### Overview
Polypeptide hormone produced by pancreatic α-cells; physiologic antagonist of insulin.

### Mechanism of Action
- Activates G-protein coupled receptors → increases cAMP
- **Liver:** Stimulates glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis
- **Other effects:** Relaxes smooth muscle (GI tract), increases myocardial contractility

### Clinical Uses
| Indication | Details |
|:---|:---|
| Severe hypoglycemia | When IV glucose unavailable; patient unconscious/unable to take oral glucose |
| Diagnostic aid | Radiologic examination of GI tract (relaxes smooth muscle) |
| Beta-blocker overdose | Positive inotropic effect |
| Anaphylaxis refractory to epinephrine | Limited evidence |

### Pharmacokinetics
- **Route:** IM, SC, IV
- **Onset:** 5-10 minutes (IM/SC)
- **Duration:** 10-20 minutes
- **Metabolism:** Hepatic and renal

### Adverse Effects
- Nausea, vomiting (dose-related)
- Hypokalemia
- Tachycardia, hypertension
- Allergic reactions

### Contraindications
- Pheochromocytoma (may stimulate catecholamine release)
- Insulinoma (may cause rebound hypoglycemia)

## DETAILED DRUG PROFILES

### 1. BIGUANIDES (Metformin)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Activates AMP-kinase
- ↓ Hepatic gluconeogenesis
- ↓ Intestinal glucose absorption
- ↑ Peripheral insulin sensitivity (muscle, adipose)

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Bioavailability: ~60%
- Not metabolized
- Renal elimination (active drug)
- Half-life: ~6 hours

**Indications:**
- First-line therapy for type 2 diabetes
- May be used in type 1 diabetes (adjunctive)
- PCOS, prediabetes, gestational diabetes (off-label)

**Dosing:** 500mg-2g daily (divided doses)

**Adverse Effects :**
- **GI:** Nausea, diarrhea, metallic taste (common; dose-related)
- **Metabolic:** Vitamin B12 deficiency (long-term use)
- **Rare but serious:** Lactic acidosis (especially in renal impairment, alcoholism, hypoxia)

**Contraindications :**
- Renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min)
- Hepatic impairment
- Alcoholism
- Conditions predisposing to lactic acidosis (sepsis, heart failure, hypoxia)
- IV contrast administration (temporary discontinuation recommended)

**Advantages :**
- No weight gain (weight neutral or mild weight loss)
- No hypoglycemia (when used alone)
- No hyperinsulinemia
- Cardiovascular benefits (UKPDS trial)

---

### 2. SULFONYLUREAS

**Generations :**

| Generation | Examples | Duration |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **First** | Tolbutamide, Chlorpropamide | Short (tolbutamide); Very long (chlorpropamide) |
| **Second** | Glipizide, Glyburide (Glibenclamide), Gliclazide, Glimepiride | Intermediate to long |

**Mechanism of Action :**
1. **Primary:** Bind to SUR1 receptor on β-cells → close K-ATP channels → depolarization → Ca⁺⁺ influx → insulin secretion
2. **Secondary:** May increase peripheral insulin sensitivity, reduce hepatic glucose production

**Pharmacokinetics :**
- Bioavailability: ~80%
- Extensively bound to albumin
- Hepatic metabolism
- Renal elimination (active drug and metabolites)
- Cross placenta 

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes (especially non-obese patients)
- Often used when metformin not tolerated or contraindicated

**Adverse Effects :**
- **Hypoglycemia** (especially long-acting agents, elderly, irregular meals)
- **Weight gain**
- **GI:** Nausea, cholestasis (rare)
- **Allergic:** Rash, photosensitivity
- **CVS:** May affect ischemic preconditioning (controversial)

**Contraindications:**
- Type 1 diabetes
- Pregnancy (crosses placenta; risk of fetal hypoglycemia)
- Severe hepatic/renal impairment
- Sulfa allergy (cross-reactivity possible)

---

### 3. MEGLITINIDES (Repaglinide, Nateglinide)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Same as sulfonylureas (close K-ATP channels)
- **Key difference:** Rapid onset, short duration → target postprandial glucose

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Very fast absorption
- Taken immediately before meals
- Short half-life (1-1.5 hours)
- Hepatic metabolism (CYP3A4, CYP2C8)

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes
- Alternative for sulfonylurea-allergic patients 
- Patients with irregular meal schedules

**Adverse Effects :**
- Hypoglycemia (less frequent than sulfonylureas)
- Weight gain

**Dosing:** Take 0-30 minutes before meals; skip if meal skipped

---

### 4. THIAZOLIDINEDIONES (Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Activate PPARγ (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma)
- Increase transcription of insulin-responsive genes
- ↑ Insulin sensitivity in adipose, muscle, liver
- ↑ Glucose uptake, ↓ hepatic glucose production

**Pharmacokinetics :**
- High bioavailability
- Slow onset (weeks for full effect)
- Extensive hepatic metabolism (CYP2C8)
- Renal and fecal elimination

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes
- Often used as second/third-line when other agents fail 

**Adverse Effects :**
- **Fluid retention** → peripheral edema, CHF exacerbation
- **Weight gain**
- **Hepatotoxicity** (rare; monitoring required)
- **Bone fractures** (women)
- **Bladder cancer** concern (pioglitazone; controversial)
- **CVS:** Rosiglitazone associated with increased MI risk (restricted use)

**Contraindications:**
- Heart failure (NYHA III-IV)
- Hepatic impairment
- Pregnancy, lactation 

---

### 5. α-GLUCOSIDASE INHIBITORS (Acarbose, Miglitol)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Competitively inhibit intestinal α-glucosidase enzymes
- Delay carbohydrate digestion and absorption
- Reduce postprandial glucose spikes

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Minimal systemic absorption (acarbose)
- Acts locally in GI tract

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes
- Postprandial hyperglycemia

**Adverse Effects:**
- **GI:** Flatulence, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort (very common)
- Hepatotoxicity (rare, acarbose)

**Dosing:** Taken with first bite of meal

---

### 6. DPP-4 INHIBITORS (Gliptins: Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin, Saxagliptin, Linagliptin)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Inhibit dipeptidyl peptidase-4 enzyme
- Increase endogenous GLP-1 and GIP levels
- ↑ Glucose-dependent insulin secretion
- ↓ Glucagon secretion

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Oral bioavailability good
- Variable elimination (renal for most; linagliptin hepatic)

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes (monotherapy or combination)
- Elderly patients (low hypoglycemia risk)

**Adverse Effects:**
- Generally well-tolerated
- Upper respiratory infection
- Headache
- Nasopharyngitis
- Pancreatitis (rare)
- Arthralgia (recent warning)

---

### 7. GLP-1 RECEPTOR AGONISTS (Incretin Mimetics)

**Examples:**
- **Short-acting:** Exenatide (twice daily), Lixisenatide
- **Long-acting:** Liraglutide (daily), Exenatide ER (weekly), Dulaglutide (weekly), Semaglutide (weekly, oral)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Activate GLP-1 receptors
- **Glucose-dependent insulin secretion** (only when glucose elevated)
- **Suppress glucagon secretion**
- **Slow gastric emptying**
- **Increase satiety** (CNS effect)

**Pharmacokinetics :**
- Subcutaneous injection (except oral semaglutide)
- Peak: 2-3 hours (short-acting)
- Renal elimination (caution in renal impairment)

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes
- Obesity/weight management (liraglutide 3.0 mg, semaglutide)
- CV risk reduction in diabetic patients (liraglutide, semaglutide)

**Adverse Effects :**
- **GI:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (common; dose-related)
- Hypoglycemia (rare unless combined with sulfonylureas/insulin)
- Pancreatitis (rare)
- Gallbladder disease
- Thyroid C-cell tumors (rodents; human relevance unclear)
- Heart rate increase

**Contraindications:**
- Personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma
- MEN2 syndrome
- Severe gastroparesis

**Drug Interactions :**
- Delay absorption of oral medications (due to slowed gastric emptying)
- Oral contraceptives, antibiotics should be taken 1 hour before exenatide

---

### 8. SGLT2 INHIBITORS (Gliflozins: Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin, Canagliflozin)

**Mechanism of Action :**
- Inhibit sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 in proximal renal tubule
- Reduce glucose reabsorption → glucosuria
- Independent of insulin

**Pharmacokinetics :**
- Bioavailability >75%
- Extensive hepatic metabolism (UGT)
- Renal elimination (inactive metabolites)
- Half-life: 10-13 hours

**Indications:**
- Type 2 diabetes
- Heart failure (reduced EF)
- Chronic kidney disease (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin)
- CV risk reduction (empagliflozin, canagliflozin)

**Adverse Effects :**
- **GU infections:** Urinary tract infections, genital mycotic infections
- **Volume depletion:** Hypotension, dizziness (especially elderly, diuretic users)
- **Euglycemic DKA:** Ketoacidosis with normal/near-normal glucose 
- **Bone fractures** (canagliflozin)
- **Lower limb amputations** (canagliflozin; warning)
- **Fournier's gangrene** (rare, serious)

**Contraindications :**
- Renal impairment (eGFR <30-45 mL/min depending on agent)
- Type 1 diabetes (off-label; risk of DKA)
- Pregnancy, lactation

# PART 3: THYROID HORMONES

## THYROID PHYSIOLOGY OVERVIEW

**Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis:**
- Hypothalamus → TRH → Anterior pituitary → TSH → Thyroid gland → T4 and T3
- Negative feedback: T3 inhibits TRH and TSH

**Thyroid Hormone Synthesis:**
1. Iodide trapping (Na/I symporter)
2. Oxidation (thyroid peroxidase)
3. Organification (MIT, DIT formation)
4. Coupling (MIT+DIT=T3; DIT+DIT=T4)
5. Storage (thyroglobulin in colloid)
6. Release (proteolysis)

---

## LEVOTHYROXINE (T4, L-Thyroxine)

**Overview :**
Synthetic form of endogenous thyroxine; the principal product secreted by thyroid gland.

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Acts as a **prohormone** - converted to active T3 in peripheral tissues by deiodinases
- T3 enters nucleus, binds thyroid hormone receptors
- Regulates gene transcription → increased metabolic rate, protein synthesis, O₂ consumption

**Pharmacokinetics :**
- **Absorption:** Variable (40-80%), taken on empty stomach
- **Half-life:** ~7 days (once-daily dosing)
- **Metabolism:** Hepatic (deiodination, glucuronidation)
- **Excretion:** Bile (fecal), renal

**Indications :**
| Condition | Rationale |
|:---|:---|
| Primary hypothyroidism | Replacement therapy |
| Secondary hypothyroidism | Replacement therapy |
| Myxedema coma | IV emergency replacement |
| TSH suppression in thyroid cancer | Prevents TSH-stimulated tumor growth |
| Euthyroid goiter | Suppress TSH to reduce goiter size |

**Dosing Principles:**
- **Starting dose:** ~1.6 mcg/kg/day (typical: 50-100 mcg)
- **Elderly/CVD:** Start low (12.5-25 mcg), titrate slowly
- **Timing:** 30-60 minutes before breakfast with water
- **Monitoring:** TSH every 6-8 weeks until stable
- **Goal:** TSH within reference range 

**Adverse Effects:**
- **Overtreatment:** Tachycardia, palpitations, anxiety, insomnia, tremor, heat intolerance, diarrhea, weight loss, osteoporosis (chronic)
- **Undertreatment:** Symptoms of hypothyroidism persist

**Drug Interactions:**
| Drug | Effect |
|:---|:---|
| Iron, Calcium | ↓ Absorption (chelation) |
| Bile acid sequestrants | Bind LT4 in gut |
| PPIs | ↓ Absorption (increased gastric pH) |
| Estrogens | ↑ TBG →可能需要 ↑ LT4 dose |
| Rifampin, Phenytoin | ↑ Hepatic metabolism |
| Sucralfate | ↓ Absorption |

**Pregnancy:**
- Dose typically increases (↑ TBG)
- Monitor TSH each trimester

## LIOTHYRONINE (T3)

**Comparison with Levothyroxine:**

| Parameter | Liothyronine (T3) | Levothyroxine (T4) |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Onset | Rapid (hours) | Slow (days) |
| Half-life | ~1 day | ~7 days |
| Dosing frequency | 2-3 times daily | Once daily |
| Potency | 4× more potent | - |
| Monitoring | Free T3 levels | TSH |

**Clinical Use:**
- **Limited role** - rarely used long-term
- Myxedema coma (sometimes with T4)
- Short-term suppression testing
- Patients with T4→T3 conversion defects (rare)

# PART 4: ANTITHYROID DRUGS

## THIONAMIDES

### Methimazole (MMI) and Propylthiouracil (PTU)

**Mechanism of Action:**
1. **Inhibit thyroid peroxidase (TPO)** → block organification and coupling (both drugs)
2. **Inhibit peripheral conversion of T4→T3** (PTU only)

**Comparison :**

| Feature | Methimazole | Propylthiouracil |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Potency** | 10× more potent | - |
| **Half-life** | 4-6 hours | 1-2 hours |
| **Dosing** | Once daily | 2-3 times daily |
| **Onset** | Slower | Faster (blocks T4→T3) |
| **Hepatotoxicity** | + (cholestatic) | +++ (fulminant; black box) |
| **Vasculitis** | Rare | Reported (ANCA-associated) |
| **Pregnancy** | Second/third trimester | First trimester preferred  |
| **Thyroid storm** | Second-line | First-line |

**Indications :**
- Graves' disease (first-line for most patients)
- Preparation for thyroidectomy or radioiodine
- Thyroid storm (PTU preferred)

**Dosing :**
- **Methimazole:** Start 10-30 mg/day (mild-moderate), up to 60 mg/day (severe)
- **PTU:** Start 100-300 mg/day divided q8h, up to 600 mg/day

**Adverse Effects :**
| Effect | Frequency | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Rash, urticaria** | 5% | Most common |
| **Agranulocytosis** | 0.3-0.6% | **Medical emergency**; fever + sore throat → check CBC |
| **Hepatotoxicity** | Rare (PTU > MMI) | PTU has black box warning for hepatic failure |
| **ANCA vasculitis** | Rare | PTU more common |
| **Arthralgia** | 1-5% | - |

**Monitoring:**
- Baseline CBC, LFTs
- Instruct patients: Stop drug and call if fever/sore throat develops
- Thyroid function tests every 4-6 weeks

## IODINE AND IODIDES

**Drugs:** Potassium iodide (SSKI), Lugol's solution

**Mechanism:**
- **Low doses:** Substrate for hormone synthesis
- **High doses:** Wolff-Chaikoff effect (inhibits organification); decreases iodide trapping; inhibits hormone release; decreases gland vascularity 

**Clinical Uses :**
| Use | Dose/Duration | Rationale |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Thyroidectomy preparation** | 50-100 mg/day × 7-10 days | Decreases vascularity, firmness of gland |
| **Thyroid storm** | SSKI 5 drops q6h | Rapidly inhibits hormone release |
| **Radiation exposure** | Potassium iodide | Blocks thyroid uptake of radioactive iodine |

**Adverse Effects:**
- **Acute:** Metallic taste, burning mouth
- **Chronic:** Iodism (rash, rhinitis, salivary swelling)
- **Hypersensitivity:** Angioedema, serum sickness
- **Fetal:** Fetal goiter (avoid in pregnancy except radiation emergency)

---

## RADIOACTIVE IODINE (¹³¹I)

**Mechanism:**
- Taken up by thyroid follicular cells via Na/I symporter
- Beta emission destroys follicular cells (radiation thyroiditis)

**Indications :**
- Graves' disease (definitive treatment)
- Toxic multinodular goiter
- Thyroid cancer (post-surgical ablation)

**Considerations in Special Populations :**
| Population | Recommendation |
|:---|:---|
| Children >10 years | Effective and relatively safe |
| Children 5-10 years | May be considered with caution |
| Children <5 years | Avoid; surgery preferred |
| Pregnancy | Contraindicated |
| Breastfeeding | Contraindicated |

**Advantages:**
- Single oral dose
- 80-90% cure rate
- No surgery risks

**Disadvantages:**
- **Delayed hypothyroidism** (inevitable; requires lifelong LT4)
- Radiation precautions for days-weeks
- May worsen Graves' ophthalmopathy (especially smokers)

---

## β-ADRENERGIC ANTAGONISTS

**Drugs:** Propranolol (non-selective), Atenolol, Metoprolol, Nadolol, Esmolol 

**Mechanism :**
- Block β-receptors to control **sympathetic symptoms** of hyperthyroidism
- Propranolol also inhibits T4→T3 conversion (high doses)

**Indications :**
- Symptomatic relief while awaiting thionamide effect
- Thyroid storm (high-dose propranolol, esmolol IV)
- Preoperative preparation
- Children with HR >100 bpm

**Clinical Pearls :**
- **Atenolol:** Most used due to cardioselectivity (less bronchospasm risk)
- **Nadolol:** May be given to asthmatics with mild COPD
- **Esmolol:** Preferred for ICU patients with thyroid storm
- **Contraindications:** Bronchospastic asthma (relative; use cardioselective agents with caution)

# PART 5: PARATHYROID HORMONE & CALCIUM REGULATORS

## CALCIUM HOMEOSTASIS OVERVIEW 

**Key Regulators:**
1. **Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)** - increases serum calcium
2. **Vitamin D (calcitriol)** - increases serum calcium
3. **Calcitonin** - decreases serum calcium

**Physiologic Roles of Calcium :**
- Muscle contraction
- Nerve excitability
- Bone and teeth formation
- Blood coagulation
- Cell membrane integrity
- Neurotransmitter release

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Calcium citrate** | 21% | Better absorption in achlorhydria, elderly |

## VITAMIN D 

**Forms:**
- **Ergocalciferol (D₂):** Plant source
- **Cholecalciferol (D₃):** Animal source, skin synthesis
- **Calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D₃):** Active form

**Metabolism:**
Skin (7-dehydrocholesterol + UVB) → Cholecalciferol → Liver (25-hydroxylase) → Calcidiol → Kidney (1α-hydroxylase) → Calcitriol

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Increases intestinal calcium and phosphate absorption
- Promotes bone mineralization
- Enhances renal calcium reabsorption

**Indications:**
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Rickets, osteomalacia
- Osteoporosis (with calcium)
- Hypoparathyroidism (with calcium)
- Renal osteodystrophy (calcitriol)

**Adverse Effects:**
- Hypercalcemia
- Hypercalciuria
- Nephrocalcinosis, nephrolithiasis

---

## BISPHOSPHONATES 

**Examples:**
- **Oral:** Alendronate, Risedronate, Ibandronate
- **IV:** Zoledronic acid, Pamidronate, Ibandronate

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption
- Bind to hydroxyapatite; internalized by osteoclasts → induce apoptosis

**Indications:**
- Osteoporosis (prevention and treatment)
- Paget's disease of bone
- Malignancy-associated hypercalcemia
- Metastatic bone disease
- Osteogenesis imperfecta

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Very low oral bioavailability (<1%)
- Must take on empty stomach with plain water
- Remain upright for 30-60 minutes
- Avoid food, drink, other medications for 30-60 min

**Adverse Effects:**
- **GI:** Esophagitis, esophageal ulcers (oral)
- **Acute phase reaction:** Fever, myalgia (IV, first dose)
- **Musculoskeletal pain**
- **Osteonecrosis of jaw** (especially IV, dental procedures)
- **Atypical femoral fractures** (long-term use)
- **Hypocalcemia**
- **Renal toxicity** (IV, infusion rate dependent)

**Contraindications:**
- Hypocalcemia
- Esophageal abnormalities (achalasia, stricture)
- Inability to stand/sit upright for 30-60 min
- Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30-35 mL/min)

---

## CALCITONIN 

**Source:**
- Thyroid parafollicular cells (C-cells)
- Synthetic: Salmon calcitonin (more potent, longer-acting)

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Inhibits osteoclast activity
- Increases renal calcium excretion
- (Minor) Decreases intestinal calcium absorption

**Indications:**
- Osteoporosis (second-line; nasal spray)
- Paget's disease
- Malignancy-associated hypercalcemia
- Analgesic for vertebral fracture pain

**Adverse Effects:**
- Nausea, vomiting
- Flushing of face and hands
- Local reactions at injection site
- Nasal irritation (nasal spray)

---

## PARATHYROID HORMONE and ANALOGS

**Examples:**
- **Teriparatide** (PTH 1-34) - recombinant human PTH fragment
- **Abaloparatide** (PTHrP analog)

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Intermittent exposure → stimulates osteoblast activity → **net bone formation** (anabolic effect)
- Contrast with continuous PTH elevation (as in hyperparathyroidism) which causes bone resorption

**Indications:**
- Severe osteoporosis (high fracture risk)
- Osteoporosis in men
- Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis
- Hypoparathyroidism (off-label)

**Dosing:**
- Daily subcutaneous injection
- Limited to 2 years lifetime use (rodent osteosarcoma risk)

**Adverse Effects:**
- Hypercalcemia
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Leg cramps
- Nausea, dizziness

**Contraindications:**
- Paget's disease
- Unexplained elevated alkaline phosphatase
- Bone metastases or skeletal malignancies
- Prior radiation therapy to skeleton
- Hypercalcemia
- Pregnancy, lactation

## GLUCOCORTICOIDS

### Classification

| Potency | Examples | Relative Anti-inflammatory Potency | Equivalent Dose (mg) |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Short-acting** | Hydrocortisone (Cortisol) | 1 | 20 |
| | Cortisone | 0.8 | 25 |
| **Intermediate-acting** | Prednisolone | 4 | 5 |
| | Prednisone | 4 | 5 |
| | Methylprednisolone | 5 | 4 |
| | Triamcinolone | 5 | 4 |
| **Long-acting** | Dexamethasone | 25-30 | 0.75 |
| | Betamethasone | 25-30 | 0.6-0.75 |

### Mechanism of Action 
- Diffuse across cell membrane
- Bind cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptors
- Receptor-ligand complex translocates to nucleus
- Bind glucocorticoid response elements (GRE) on DNA
- Regulate gene transcription (transactivation and transrepression)

**Genomic effects:**
- ↑ Lipocortin (inhibits phospholipase A2 → ↓ prostaglandins, leukotrienes)
- ↓ Cytokine production (IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ)
- ↓ Inflammatory enzyme synthesis (COX-2, iNOS)
- ↑ Anti-inflammatory proteins (IκBα, IL-10)

**Non-genomic effects:** Rapid effects (minutes) via membrane receptors

### Pharmacologic Effects

| System | Effects |
|:---|:---|
| **Metabolic** | ↑ Gluconeogenesis, ↑ glycogen deposition, ↑ protein catabolism, ↑ lipolysis, redistribute fat (central) |
| **Anti-inflammatory** | ↓ Inflammatory cell migration, ↓ mediator release, ↓ capillary permeability, stabilize lysosomes |
| **Immunosuppressive** | ↓ Lymphocyte proliferation, ↓ antibody production, ↓ cell-mediated immunity |
| **CVS/Renal** | ↑ BP (mineralocorticoid activity), ↑ sodium retention, ↑ potassium excretion |
| **Musculoskeletal** | ↑ Muscle catabolism, ↓ bone formation, ↓ calcium absorption |
| **CNS** | Mood changes, euphoria, depression, psychosis |
| **Hematologic** | ↑ Neutrophils, ↓ lymphocytes, ↓ eosinophils, ↓ monocytes |

### Pharmacokinetics
- **Absorption:** Well absorbed orally; IV, IM, intra-articular routes available
- **Distribution:** Bound to corticosteroid-binding globulin (CBG) and albumin
- **Metabolism:** Hepatic (CYP3A4)
- **Elimination:** Renal (as glucuronides)

### Clinical Indications
| Category | Examples |
|:---|:---|
| **Replacement therapy** | Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease), Congenital adrenal hyperplasia |
| **Inflammatory disorders** | Rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, asthma, COPD exacerbation |
| **Allergic disorders** | Anaphylaxis, angioedema, severe drug reactions |
| **Autoimmune diseases** | SLE, vasculitis, autoimmune hemolytic anemia, ITP |
| **Organ transplantation** | Immunosuppression (prevent rejection) |
| **Dermatologic** | Atopic dermatitis, psoriasis (topical) |
| **Ophthalmic** | Uveitis, optic neuritis |
| **Malignancy** | Lymphoma, leukemia (part of chemotherapy regimens) |
| **Cerebral edema** | Dexamethasone (brain tumors) |
| **Antiemetic** | Dexamethasone (chemotherapy-induced) |
| **Fetal lung maturation** | Betamethasone (preterm labor) |

### Adverse Effects (Chronic Use)

**Immediate/Early:**
- Mood changes, insomnia
- Increased appetite, weight gain
- Fluid retention
- Glucose intolerance

**Long-term (Cushing's syndrome):**
| System | Effects |
|:---|:---|
| **Metabolic** | Diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, myopathy |
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypertension, accelerated atherosclerosis |
| **GI** | Peptic ulcer disease (with NSAIDs), pancreatitis |
| **Ocular** | Cataracts (posterior subcapsular), glaucoma |
| **Dermatologic** | Skin thinning, striae, acne, hirsutism, easy bruising |
| **Immunologic** | Increased infection risk, reactivation of latent infections (TB, hepatitis B) |
| **Endocrine** | Adrenal suppression, growth suppression (children) |
| **CNS** | Psychiatric disturbances, cognitive impairment |

### Adrenal Suppression 
- **Mechanism:** Exogenous glucocorticoids inhibit CRH and ACTH → adrenal atrophy
- **Risk factors:** Dose >20 mg/day prednisone equivalent >3 weeks; evening dosing; long-acting agents
- **Prevention:** Use lowest effective dose; alternate-day therapy (if possible); morning dosing
- **Recovery:** May take 6-12 months after cessation
- **Stress dosing:** During illness, surgery, trauma (adrenal crisis prevention)

### Withdrawal Syndrome
| Type | Timing | Features |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Adrenal insufficiency** | Delayed | Hypotension, hypoglycemia, fatigue, nausea |
| **Steroid withdrawal syndrome** | Immediate | Anorexia, lethargy, myalgia, arthralgia, fever (without adrenal insufficiency) |

### Contraindications/Precautions
- Systemic fungal infections
- Live virus vaccines
- Tuberculosis (reactivation risk)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hypertension
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Osteoporosis
- Glaucoma
- Pregnancy (use if clearly needed)

### Drug Interactions
| Drug | Interaction |
|:---|:---|
| **NSAIDs** | ↑ GI bleeding risk |
| **Diuretics** | ↑ Hypokalemia |
| **Antidiabetic agents** | ↓ Efficacy (↑ glucose) |
| **Anticoagulants** | Altered response |
| **CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin)** | ↓ Steroid efficacy |
| **CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, ritonavir)** | ↑ Steroid effects |

## MINERALOCORTICOIDS

### Fludrocortisone

**Overview :**
Synthetic mineralocorticoid; primary drug for mineralocorticoid replacement.

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Binds mineralocorticoid receptors in renal collecting duct
- Increases sodium reabsorption, potassium and hydrogen excretion
- Regulated by renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system

**Indications:**
- **Adrenal insufficiency** (Addison's disease) - with glucocorticoid
- **Salt-losing forms of congenital adrenal hyperplasia**
- **Autonomic neuropathy** with orthostatic hypotension (off-label)

**Dosing:**
- 0.05-0.2 mg daily
- Monitor electrolytes, blood pressure, edema

**Adverse Effects:**
- Hypertension
- Hypokalemia
- Edema
- Cardiac hypertrophy (with overdose)

**Monitoring:**
- Serum electrolytes (K⁺, Na⁺)
- Blood pressure
- Edema
- Plasma renin activity (to guide dosing)

# PART 7: PITUITARY HORMONES

## GROWTH HORMONE (Somatropin, Somatrem)

**Physiology:**
- Secreted by anterior pituitary
- Stimulated by GHRH, inhibited by somatostatin
- Acts via GH receptors → IGF-1 production (liver)

**Indications:**
| Category | Examples |
|:---|:---|
| **Pediatric** | GH deficiency, Turner syndrome, Prader-Willi syndrome, Chronic renal insufficiency, Small for gestational age with catch-up failure |
| **Adult** | GH deficiency (pituitary disease), HIV-associated wasting (historical) |

**Adverse Effects:**
- Fluid retention (peripheral edema)
- Arthralgia, myalgia
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Hyperglycemia
- Intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri)
- Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (children)
- Leukemia risk (controversial)

**Contraindications:**
- Active malignancy
- Diabetic retinopathy
- Critical illness (increased mortality)
- Prader-Willi syndrome with severe obesity/respiratory impairment

## SOMATOSTATIN ANALOGS

**Examples:** Octreotide, Lanreotide

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Activate somatostatin receptors (SSTR2, SSTR5)
- Inhibit GH, TSH, insulin, glucagon secretion
- Inhibit GI hormones and secretions

**Indications:**
- Acromegaly
- Carcinoid syndrome (symptom control)
- VIPomas, glucagonomas
- Esophageal varices bleeding (octreotide)
- Diarrhea in AIDS/HIV (octreotide)
- TSH-secreting pituitary tumors

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Subcutaneous or IM (long-acting depot)
- Short half-life (1.5-2 hours; short-acting)

**Adverse Effects:**
- **GI:** Nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea, steatorrhea
- **Gallbladder:** Cholelithiasis (bile stasis)
- **Metabolic:** Hyper/hypoglycemia (altered insulin/glucagon)
- **Cardiac:** Bradycardia, conduction abnormalities
- **Injection site reactions**

## VASOPRESSIN AND ANALOGS

### Vasopressin (ADH)

**Physiologic Roles:**
- V1 receptors: Vascular smooth muscle (vasoconstriction)
- V2 receptors: Renal collecting duct (water reabsorption)
- V3 receptors: Pituitary (ACTH release)

**Indications:**
- Diabetes insipidus (central)
- GI bleeding (esophageal varices)
- Cardiac arrest (alternative to epinephrine)
- Vasodilatory shock (septic shock)

### Desmopressin (DDAVP)

**Synthetic analog with modifications:**
- Deaminated → longer half-life
- Substituted arginine → selective for V2 receptors

**Advantages over Vasopressin:**
- Longer duration of action (6-24 hours)
- No V1 activity → no vasoconstriction/hypertension
- Intranasal, oral, parenteral routes available

**Indications:**
| Use | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Central diabetes insipidus** | V2-mediated water reabsorption |
| **Nocturnal enuresis** | Reduces nighttime urine output |
| **Hemophilia A** | ↑ Factor VIII release |
| **von Willebrand disease** | ↑ vWF release |
| **Nocturia in adults** | Multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injury |

**Adverse Effects:**
- Hyponatremia (water retention → dilutional hyponatremia)
- Headache, nausea
- Nasal irritation (intranasal)
- Abdominal cramps

**Monitoring:**
- Serum sodium
- Urine output, osmolality
- Fluid intake

**Contraindications:**
- Hyponatremia
- Psychogenic polydipsia
- Moderate to severe renal impairment
- Type 2B von Willebrand disease (may cause platelet aggregation)

## OXYTOCIN

**Physiology:**
- Synthesized in hypothalamus, stored in posterior pituitary
- Stimulates uterine contractions (labor)
- Stimulates milk ejection (let-down reflex)

**Indications:**
| Obstetric Use | Details |
|:---|:---|
| Induction/augmentation of labor | IV infusion with careful monitoring |
| Postpartum hemorrhage | IV or IM (prevents/treats uterine atony) |
| Incomplete abortion | Facilitates uterine emptying |

**Adverse Effects:**
- **Maternal:** Uterine hyperstimulation → uterine rupture, water intoxication (high doses, prolonged infusion), hypotension (rapid IV)
- **Fetal:** Fetal distress, bradycardia, arrhythmias

**Contraindications:**
- Fetal distress
- Cephalopelvic disproportion
- Abnormal fetal presentation
- Placenta previa
- Cord prolapse
- Previous uterine surgery (classical C-section)

**Monitoring:**
- Uterine activity (contraction frequency, duration, resting tone)
- Fetal heart rate
- Blood pressure
- Fluid balance

# KEY CLINICAL PEARLS FOR EXAMS

1. **Metformin first-line** for T2DM unless contraindicated 
2. **Sulfonylureas:** Most common cause of drug-induced hypoglycemia
3. **GLP-1 agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors** have CV and renal benefits
4. **SGLT2 inhibitors:** Risk of euglycemic DKA (educate patients) 
5. **Levothyroxine:** Take on empty stomach; separate from iron, calcium, PPIs 
6. **PTU:** Black box for hepatotoxicity; avoid in children unless intolerant to MMI 
7. **Agranulocytosis warning:** Fever + sore throat on thionamides → stop drug, check CBC 
8. **Glucocorticoids:** Never stop abruptly after >3 weeks therapy; taper slowly
9. **Desmopressin:** Monitor sodium; risk of hyponatremia
10. **Bisphosphonates:** Take on empty stomach with water; remain upright 30-60 min
