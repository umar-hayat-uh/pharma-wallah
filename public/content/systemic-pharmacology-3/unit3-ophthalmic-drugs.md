# PART 3: OCULAR PHARMACOLOGY

## SECTION A: ANATOMY AND PHYSIOLOGY OF THE EYE

### Key Structures Relevant to Drug Action

| Structure | Function | Pharmacological Relevance |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Cornea** | Transparent anterior structure; refracts light | Barrier to drug penetration; topical drugs must cross epithelium (lipophilic) and stroma (hydrophilic) |
| **Conjunctiva** | Mucous membrane covering sclera and inner eyelids | Site of absorption; inflammation in conjunctivitis; vascular bed can absorb drugs systemically |
| **Aqueous humor** | Fluid in anterior chamber; maintains intraocular pressure (IOP) | Drainage via trabecular meshwork and uveoscleral pathway; target for glaucoma drugs |
| **Ciliary body** | Produces aqueous humor; contains muscles for accommodation | β-receptors stimulate aqueous production; muscarinic receptors control accommodation |
| **Iris** | Controls pupil size (sphincter and dilator muscles) | Site of action for miotics (constrict) and mydriatics (dilate) |
| **Trabecular meshwork** | Primary drainage pathway for aqueous humor | Target for drugs increasing outflow (miotics, Rho kinase inhibitors) |
| **Retina** | Light-sensitive neural tissue | Site of action for drugs affecting vision; potential toxicity (e.g., hydroxychloroquine) |
| **Lens** | Focuses light | Site of cataract formation (steroid-induced) |

### Aqueous Humor Dynamics

**Production:**
- Produced by ciliary epithelium (non-pigmented layer)
- Rate: ~2-3 μL/min
- Processes: Active secretion (major), ultrafiltration, diffusion
- Regulation: β₂-adrenergic receptors stimulate production; carbonic anhydrase essential

**Outflow Pathways:**
| Pathway | % Outflow | Mechanism | Drug Target |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Trabecular (conventional)** | 80-90% | Pressure-dependent; through trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal | Miotics (contract ciliary muscle), Rho kinase inhibitors |
| **Uveoscleral (unconventional)** | 10-20% | Pressure-independent; through ciliary muscle into supraciliary space | Prostaglandin analogs (relax ciliary muscle) |

### Routes of Ocular Drug Administration

| Route | Advantages | Disadvantages | Examples |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Topical (drops)** | Convenient; non-invasive; patient self-administration | Low bioavailability (1-10%); rapid drainage; requires patient compliance | Most ophthalmic drugs (antibiotics, glaucoma meds) |
| **Topical (ointments)** | Longer contact time; prolonged effect | Blurred vision; patient discomfort | Antibiotics, lubricants, night-time use |
| **Subconjunctival** | Higher intraocular levels; bypasses corneal barrier | Invasive; injection required; uncomfortable | Corticosteroids, antibiotics for severe infections |
| **Intravitreal** | Direct access to posterior segment; high vitreous levels | Invasive; risk of endophthalmitis, retinal detachment | Anti-VEGF agents (ranibizumab), antivirals (foscarnet) |
| **Systemic** | Treat posterior segment; bilateral disease | Systemic side effects; poor ocular penetration | Acetazolamide (glaucoma), some antibiotics |
| **Periocular (sub-Tenon, retrobulbar)** | Depot effect; high local levels | Invasive; complications rare | Corticosteroids (uveitis), anesthetics |
| **Intracameral** | Direct into anterior chamber | Surgical setting only | Antibiotics (cataract surgery), anesthetics |

### Factors Affecting Ocular Drug Absorption

| Factor | Effect |
|:---|:---|
| **Corneal permeability** | Lipophilic drugs penetrate epithelium better; hydrophilic drugs need stromal penetration |
| **Drug concentration** | Higher concentration → more absorption (but may cause toxicity) |
| **Contact time** | Longer contact → more absorption (viscosity agents, ointments increase contact) |
| **Nasolacrimal drainage** | 80% of drop drains in 2 minutes → systemic absorption (can reduce with punctal occlusion) |
| **Tear turnover** | Normal: 1 μL/min; increases with irritation → dilutes drug |
| **Protein binding** | Bound drug inactive; tears contain proteins that bind drugs |
| **Metabolism** | Esterases in cornea and conjunctiva can degrade drugs (e.g., dipivefrin prodrug) |

**Clinical Pearl:** **Punctal occlusion** (pressing on inner corner of eye for 1-2 minutes after instillation) increases ocular absorption by 30-50% and reduces systemic side effects.

## SECTION B: AUTONOMIC AGENTS IN OPHTHALMOLOGY

### Autonomic Innervation of the Eye

| Structure | Sympathetic (α/β) | Parasympathetic (Muscarinic) |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Iris dilator muscle** | α₁ → Contraction (mydriasis) | No innervation |
| **Iris sphincter muscle** | No innervation | M₃ → Contraction (miosis) |
| **Ciliary muscle** | β₂ → Relaxation (far vision) | M₃ → Contraction (accommodation for near vision) |
| **Ciliary epithelium** | β₂ → ↑ Aqueous production | No direct effect |
| **Trabecular meshwork** | Indirect effects | Contraction → ↑ Outflow facility |
| **Lacrimal gland** | α → ↓ secretion; β → ↑ protein secretion | M₃ → ↑ tear secretion |
| **Blood vessels** | α → vasoconstriction | No innervation |

## 1. MIOTICS (Parasympathomimetics)

These drugs constrict the pupil (miosis) and increase aqueous humor outflow by contracting the ciliary muscle.

### A. Direct-Acting Muscarinic Agonists

#### PILOCARPINE

**Overview:**
- Naturally occurring alkaloid from *Pilocarpus* species
- Prototype miotic agent

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Direct agonist at muscarinic (M₃) receptors on:
  - **Iris sphincter muscle** → contraction → **miosis** (pupil constriction)
  - **Ciliary muscle** → contraction → **accommodation** (near vision focus) and **trabecular meshwork opening** → ↑ aqueous outflow

**Pharmacological Effects:**
| Effect | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Miosis** | 10-30 min | 30 min | 4-8 hours |
| **IOP reduction** | 1-2 hours | 2-4 hours | 4-8 hours |
| **Accommodation** | Within 30 min | 1-2 hours | 2-4 hours |

**IOP Reduction:** 20-30% from baseline

**Concentrations Available:**
- Solutions: 0.5%, 1%, 2%, 3%, 4%
- Gel: 4% (prolonged effect)
- Ocular inserts: Ocusert® (slow release over 1 week)

**Indications:**

| Indication | Rationale | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Open-angle glaucoma** | ↑ Trabecular outflow | Historical first-line; now second/third-line due to side effects |
| **Angle-closure glaucoma (acute)** | Miosis pulls iris away from trabecular meshwork | Emergency use (before laser iridotomy) |
| **To reverse mydriasis** | After diagnostic fundoscopy | Counteracts effects of mydriatics |
| **Accommodative esotropia** | Induces accommodation → convergence | In children (off-label) |

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Good corneal penetration
- Effects last 4-8 hours (drops), up to 24 hours (gel)
- Metabolized locally and systemically

**Adverse Effects:**

| Ocular Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Blurred vision** | Due to induced myopia (ciliary spasm) and miosis (especially in bright light) |
| **Brow ache** | Ciliary muscle spasm; common initially; may decrease with continued use |
| **Night vision problems** | Miosis reduces light entry; difficulty driving at night |
| **Retinal detachment** | Rare; theoretical risk in predisposed patients (high myopia) |
| **Lens changes** | Long-term use may accelerate cataract formation |

| Systemic Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Sweating** | Muscarinic stimulation of sweat glands |
| **Salivation** | Increased salivation |
| **Bronchospasm** | In asthmatics (M₃ receptors in bronchi) |
| **Bradycardia, hypotension** | Cardiac muscarinic effects (with excessive use/systemic absorption) |
| **Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea** | GI muscarinic stimulation |

**Contraindications:**
| Absolute/Relative | Reason |
|:---|:---|
| **Uveitis (iritis)** | Miosis may worsen inflammation; can cause posterior synechiae |
| **Retinal detachment risk** | High myopia, history of detachment |
| **Asthma** | Risk of bronchospasm |
| **Peptic ulcer disease** | May exacerbate |
| **Angle-closure glaucoma** | Use with extreme caution (only in acute attack before definitive treatment) |

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **First-dose effect:** Patients often experience brow ache and blurred vision initially; tolerance develops
- **Night driving warning:** Advise patients about difficulty with night vision
- **Pilocarpine gel (4%):** Used at bedtime for prolonged effect; causes less blurred vision during day
- **Reverse pupillary block:** In acute angle closure, pilocarpine 2% q15min for first hour, then q4h until laser
- **Not first-line now:** Due to side effects and availability of better-tolerated drugs (prostaglandins, beta-blockers)

#### CARBACHOL

**Overview:**
- Synthetic choline ester
- More potent than pilocarpine

**Mechanism of Action:**
- **Direct:** Muscarinic receptor agonist
- **Indirect:** Weak inhibitor of cholinesterase

**Properties:**
| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Potency** | 100× more potent than pilocarpine |
| **Duration** | 6-8 hours |
| **IOP reduction** | 20-30% |
| **Corneal penetration** | Poor; requires good epithelial integrity or used intraocularly |

**Indications:**
- Glaucoma (refractory to other agents)
- **Intraoperative miosis** during cataract surgery (intracameral use)

**Advantages:**
- Longer duration than pilocarpine
- More potent miotic effect

**Disadvantages:**
- More side effects
- Poor corneal penetration (unless epithelial compromised)
- Refrigeration required (unstable)

**Adverse Effects:**
- Similar to pilocarpine but more intense
- Higher risk of systemic effects
- More brow ache and induced myopia

#### ECHOTHIOPHATE IODIDE (Phospholine Iodide®)

| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Mechanism** | Irreversible organophosphate cholinesterase inhibitor |
| **Concentration** | 0.03%, 0.06%, 0.125%, 0.25% |
| **Onset** | 30-60 min |
| **Peak** | 24 hours |
| **Duration** | Up to 2 weeks (!) |
| **IOP reduction** | 30-40% |

- **Rarely used today** due to significant toxicity
- Cataract formation almost inevitable with long-term use
- Only used when all other agents fail
- Must inform anesthesiologist if patient on echothiophate (prolonged apnea with succinylcholine)

## 2. MYDRIATICS AND CYCLOPLEGICS

These drugs dilate the pupil (mydriasis) and may paralyze accommodation (cycloplegia).

### A. Antimuscarinic Agents

**Mechanism:**
- Block muscarinic M₃ receptors on:
  - **Iris sphincter muscle** → relaxation → **mydriasis**
  - **Ciliary muscle** → relaxation → **cycloplegia** (loss of accommodation)

#### Comparison of Antimuscarinic Agents

| Drug | Mydriasis | Cycloplegia | Duration | Strength | Clinical Use |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **ATROPINE** | Complete | Complete | 7-14 days | Strongest | Cycloplegic refraction (children); uveitis; amblyopia |
| **HOMATROPINE** | Moderate | Moderate | 1-3 days | Moderate | Alternative to atropine; shorter duration |
| **SCOPOLAMINE** | Complete | Complete | 3-7 days | Strong | Similar to atropine; more CNS effects |
| **CYCLOPENTOLATE** | Good | Good | 6-24 hours | Strong | Routine cycloplegic refraction; fundoscopy |
| **TROPICAMIDE** | Good | Minimal | 4-6 hours | Weak | Routine fundoscopy; short procedures |

#### Detailed Drug Profiles

##### ATROPINE SULFATE

**Overview:**
- Naturally occurring belladonna alkaloid
- Prototype antimuscarinic agent

**Mechanism:**
- Competitive antagonist at muscarinic receptors
- Non-selective (M₁, M₂, M₃, etc.)

**Pharmacological Effects:**
| Effect | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Mydriasis** | 30-40 min | 1-2 hours | 7-14 days |
| **Cycloplegia** | 1-2 hours | 2-4 hours | 7-12 days |

**Concentrations:** 0.5%, 1% solution; 0.5%, 1% ointment

**Indications:**

| Indication | Rationale | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Cycloplegic refraction (children)** | Complete cycloplegia for accurate refraction | Especially in children <5 years; use 1% tid for 3 days before |
| **Uveitis (iritis, iridocyclitis)** | Prevents posterior synechiae (adhesions between iris and lens); reduces pain from ciliary spasm | Dilates pupil and immobilizes iris |
| **Amblyopia treatment** | Occludes vision in good eye to force use of amblyopic eye | Atropine penalization (alternative to patching) |
| **Preoperative mydriasis** | For certain eye surgeries | Often combined with phenylephrine |
| **Ciliary block (malignant) glaucoma** | Relaxes ciliary muscle | Rare indication |

**Adverse Effects:**

| Ocular Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Photophobia** | Due to mydriasis (excessive light entry) |
| **Blurred near vision** | Due to cycloplegia (loss of accommodation) |
| **Increased IOP** | Can precipitate angle-closure glaucoma in anatomically predisposed patients |
| **Stinging on instillation** | Common |
| **Follicular conjunctivitis** | With prolonged use |

| Systemic Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Atropine "flush"** (children) | Fever, flushing, dry skin, tachycardia, irritability - due to systemic absorption |
| **Dry mouth, thirst** | Antimuscarinic effect on salivary glands |
| **Constipation, urinary retention** | Antimuscarinic effects on GI and bladder |
| **Confusion, agitation, hallucinations** | CNS effects (especially in elderly, children) |
| **Tachycardia, palpitations** | Cardiac effects |

**Contraindications:**

| Contraindication | Reason |
|:---|:---|
| **Angle-closure glaucoma** | May precipitate acute attack |
| **Anatomically narrow angles** | Same |
| **Down syndrome** | Extreme sensitivity to atropine (exaggerated pupillary dilation and systemic effects) |
| **Infants** | Risk of systemic toxicity (use lowest concentration, compress lacrimal sac) |
| **Prostatic hyperplasia** | Urinary retention risk |
| **Myasthenia gravis** | May worsen symptoms |

**Prevention of Systemic Effects:**
- **Punctal occlusion** (digital pressure on inner canthus for 1-2 minutes after instillation)
- Use lowest effective concentration
- Wipe away excess drops from cheek
- Use ointment at night (less systemic absorption)

**Pediatric Dosing:**
- Infants: Use 0.5% rather than 1%
- Monitor for systemic toxicity: fever, flushed skin, tachycardia
- Instruct parents to stop drug and seek medical help if these occur

### B. Sympathomimetics (Adrenergic Agonists)

#### PHENYLEPHRINE HYDROCHLORIDE

**Overview:**
- Selective α₁-adrenergic agonist
- Produces mydriasis WITHOUT cycloplegia

**Mechanism:**
- Contracts iris dilator muscle (α₁ receptors) → mydriasis
- No effect on ciliary muscle → accommodation preserved

**Pharmacological Effects:**
| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Concentration** | 2.5% (routine), 5%, 10% (strong; rarely used) |
| **Onset** | 15-30 minutes |
| **Peak** | 30-60 minutes |
| **Duration** | 3-6 hours |
| **Mydriasis** | Moderate to marked |
| **Cycloplegia** | None |

**Indications:**

| Indication | Rationale | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Fundoscopy** | Rapid mydriasis without cycloplegia | Often combined with tropicamide for synergistic effect |
| **Preoperative mydriasis** | For cataract and other eye surgeries | Combined with cycloplegic |
| **Adjunct in uveitis** | To break posterior synechiae | Combined with cycloplegic |
| **Diagnostic** | Horner's syndrome testing (denervation hypersensitivity) | - |
| **To reduce conjunctival injection** | Vasoconstriction | In minor eye irritation |

**Advantages:**
- No cycloplegia → patient can accommodate (near vision preserved)
- Rapid onset
- Synergistic with antimuscarinics (combine for maximum mydriasis)

**Adverse Effects:**

| Ocular Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Burning/stinging** | Common on instillation |
| **Rebound miosis** | When effect wears off |
| **Pigment dispersion** | In lens; with prolonged use |
| **Conjunctival blanching** | Vasoconstriction |

| Systemic Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Hypertension** | α-mediated vasoconstriction; significant with 10%, especially in elderly |
| **Reflex bradycardia** | Baroreceptor response |
| **Tachycardia, arrhythmias** | Rare |
| **Myocardial infarction** | Rare case reports, especially with 10% |
| **Pulmonary edema** | Rare (cardiogenic) |

**Contraindications:**

| Contraindication | Reason |
|:---|:---|
| **Severe hypertension** | May cause hypertensive crisis |
| **Aneurysms** | Risk of rupture |
| **Elderly with cardiovascular disease** | Use 2.5% only; avoid 10% |
| **Infants** | Avoid 10% (severe hypertension, pulmonary edema reported) |
| **Patients on MAO inhibitors** | Hypertensive crisis risk |
| **Patients on β-blockers** | Unopposed α effects → severe hypertension |

**Drug Interactions:**

| Drug | Interaction |
|:---|:---|
| **MAO inhibitors** | Hypertensive crisis |
| **Tricyclic antidepressants** | Enhanced pressor response |
| **β-blockers (systemic)** | Unopposed α effects → severe hypertension |
| **Atropine/antimuscarinics** | Synergistic mydriasis (useful clinically) |

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **NEVER use 10% in:** Infants, elderly, cardiovascular disease, hypertension
- **Punctal occlusion** essential to reduce systemic absorption
- **Combine with tropicamide** for rapid, maximal mydriasis (ophthalmology standard)
- **Rebound miosis:** Warn patients that pupils may become smaller than normal after drug wears off

## 3. BETA-BLOCKERS IN GLAUCOMA

### Overview
Beta-blockers are a mainstay of glaucoma therapy, reducing IOP by decreasing aqueous humor production.

### Mechanism of Action
- Block β₂-adrenergic receptors on **ciliary epithelium**
- ↓ Cyclic AMP → ↓ aqueous humor production
- IOP reduction: 20-30% from baseline
- No effect on outflow

### Classification

| Type | Examples | Properties |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Non-selective (β₁ + β₂)** | Timolol, Levobunolol, Carteolol (with ISA), Metipranolol | Greater IOP reduction; more systemic side effects (bronchospasm) |
| **β₁-selective (cardioselective)** | Betaxolol | Less IOP reduction; safer in asthma/COPD |

#### TIMOLOL MALEATE - Prototype

**Overview:**
- Non-selective β-blocker
- Most widely studied and prescribed ophthalmic beta-blocker

**Pharmacological Effects:**
| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Concentration** | 0.25%, 0.5% solution; 0.25%, 0.5% gel-forming solution (Timoptic-XE®) |
| **Onset** | 30-60 minutes |

| **IOP reduction** | 20-30% |

**Mechanism:**
- Blocks β₂ receptors on ciliary epithelium → ↓ aqueous production (20-50% reduction)
- No effect on outflow

**Indications:**
| Indication | Notes |
|:---|:---|
| **Chronic open-angle glaucoma** | First-line monotherapy or adjunct |
| **Ocular hypertension** | To prevent glaucoma onset |
| **Pediatric glaucoma** | Off-label; effective |
| **Post-laser IOP spike prevention** | Short-term use |

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Good corneal penetration
- Significant systemic absorption via nasolacrimal duct
- Peak plasma levels similar to oral β-blocker doses
- Hepatic metabolism; renal excretion

**Adverse Effects:**

| Ocular Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Burning, stinging** | Common, especially with solutions |
| **Blurred vision** | Transient |
| **Dry eye** | Decreased tear production |
| **Superficial punctate keratitis** | Corneal toxicity |
| **Allergic reactions** | Rare |
| **Cystoid macular edema** | Rare; in aphakic/pseudophakic patients |

| Systemic Effects | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Cardiovascular** | Bradycardia, heart block, hypotension, worsening CHF, syncope |
| **Respiratory** | Bronchospasm, dyspnea (especially in asthmatics/COPD) |
| **CNS** | Fatigue, depression, confusion, hallucinations, dizziness, weakness |
| **Metabolic** | Mask hypoglycemia symptoms (tachycardia); may worsen hypoglycemia in diabetics |
| **Sexual** | Decreased libido, impotence |
| **Other** | Alopecia, worsening of myasthenia gravis, psoriasis |

| Absolute | Relative |

| Cardiogenic shock | Diabetes (monitor glucose) |
| Uncompensated heart failure | Myasthenia gravis |
| Reactive airway disease (asthma, severe COPD) | Depression |
| Hypersensitivity | |

**Drug Interactions:**

| Drug | Interaction |
|:---|:---|
| **Oral β-blockers** | Additive systemic effects (bradycardia, hypotension) |
| **Calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem)** | Additive cardiac depression, bradycardia |
| **Digoxin** | Additive bradycardia |
| **Epinephrine** | Hypertension (unopposed α effects with non-selective β-blockers) |
| **Insulin/oral hypoglycemics** | Mask hypoglycemia symptoms; may worsen hypoglycemia |

**Clinical Pearls:**

| Pearl | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Punctal occlusion** | Essential to reduce systemic absorption; digital pressure for 1-2 minutes after drops |
| **Gel formulation** | Timolol gel (Timoptic-XE®) once daily; better compliance; less systemic absorption? |
| **"Additive" therapy** | Beta-blockers + prostaglandins or alpha-agonists or CAIs have additive IOP reduction |
| **Contraindication in asthma** | Even betaxolol can cause bronchospasm; use with extreme caution or avoid |
| **Masking hypoglycemia** | Warn diabetic patients that β-blockers may mask tachycardia (but not sweating) of hypoglycemia |
| **Check pulse** | Monitor for bradycardia; consider holding if pulse <50-55 bpm |
| **Therapeutic failure** | If IOP not controlled, consider poor compliance, tachyphylaxis (rare), or need for additional agents |

### A. Prostaglandin Analogs

**Drugs:**
- **Latanoprost** (Xalatan®) - 0.005%
- **Bimatoprost** (Lumigan®) - 0.03%, 0.01%
- **Travoprost** (Travatan®) - 0.004%
- **Tafluprost** (Zioptan®) - 0.0015% (preservative-free)

- Activate FP (prostaglandin F) receptors on ciliary muscle cells

| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Route** | Topical ophthalmic |
| **Dosing** | **Once daily** (evening preferred - possibly better efficacy) |
| **Onset** | 2-4 hours |
| **Peak effect** | 8-12 hours |
| **Duration** | >24 hours |
| **Metabolism** | Corneal esterases; systemic metabolism minimal |

**Comparative Potency:**
- All prostaglandins similar efficacy

**Adverse Effects:**

| Ocular Effects | Details |

| **Conjunctival hyperemia** | Red eye; most common (10-50%); mild-moderate; dose-related; bimatoprost > latanoprost/travoprost |

| **Dry eye** | - |

### B. Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAIs)

#### Topical CAIs

**Drugs:**
- **Dorzolamide** (Trusopt®) - 2% (tid)

- Inhibit carbonic anhydrase (isoenzyme II) in ciliary epithelium

**IOP Reduction:** 15-20% (less than beta-blockers)

**Pharmacokinetics:**
| Parameter | Dorzolamide | Brinzolamide |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Formulation** | Solution (2%) | Suspension (1%) |

| **Onset** | 2 hours | 2 hours |
| **Peak** | 3 hours | 3 hours |
| **Duration** | 8-12 hours | 8-12 hours |
| **Systemic absorption** | Minimal | Minimal |

**Indications:**
- Adjunct to beta-blockers or prostaglandins
- Patients intolerant to systemic CAIs

**Adverse Effects:**

| Dorzolamide | Brinzolamide |
|:---|:---|
| **Burning/stinging** (common) | **Less stinging** (suspension) |
| **Bitter taste** (30% - due to nasolacrimal drainage) | Less bitter taste |

| **Superficial punctate keratitis** | Similar |
| **Allergic reactions** (lid edema, conjunctivitis) | Similar |
| **Iritis, uveitis** (rare) | Similar |

| Systemic Effects (rare) | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Sulfa allergy** | Cross-reactivity possible (both are sulfonamide derivatives) |
| **Headache** | Mild |
| **Fatigue** | Rare |
| **Metabolic acidosis** | Not with topical |

- Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Sulfa allergy:** Use with caution in sulfa-allergic patients; systemic CAIs contraindicated but topical may be used with extreme caution
- **Bitter taste:** Due to drug in nasopharynx; punctal occlusion helps

## SECTION C: CHEMOTHERAPY IN EYE DISEASES

### Principles of Ocular Anti-Infective Therapy

**Factors Affecting Drug Selection:**
| Factor | Considerations |
|:---|:---|
| **Organism** | Bacterial, viral, fungal, parasitic - identification through culture, Gram stain, PCR |
| **Location of infection** | External (conjunctiva, cornea) vs. intraocular (vitreous, retina) - determines route |
| **Severity** | Mild conjunctivitis vs. vision-threatening endophthalmitis |
| **Host factors** | Contact lens wearer, immunocompromised, previous eye surgery |
| **Resistance patterns** | Local resistance data important |
| **Ocular penetration** | Topical vs. systemic vs. intravitreal |

**Barriers to Therapy:**
| Barrier | Impact |
|:---|:---|
| **Blood-ocular barrier** | Tight junctions in retinal vessels and ciliary epithelium limit systemic drug penetration into vitreous and aqueous |
| **Corneal epithelium** | Tight junctions limit topical penetration of hydrophilic drugs |
| **Corneal stroma** | Hydrophilic layer limits lipophilic drug penetration |
| **Vitreous** | Avascular; drugs must diffuse; no blood flow |
| **Tear film** | Dilutes and washes away topical drugs |

**Routes of Administration for Ocular Infections:**

| Route | Indications | Examples |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Topical (drops)** | External infections (conjunctivitis, blepharitis); mild keratitis | Most antibiotics, antivirals |
| **Topical (ointments)** | Same; overnight therapy; pediatric | Erythromycin, bacitracin |
| **Fortified topical drops** | Severe bacterial keratitis | Cefazolin (5%) + tobramycin (1.4%) |
| **Subconjunctival** | Severe keratitis; adjunct in endophthalmitis | Cefazolin, vancomycin, gentamicin |
| **Intravitreal** | Endophthalmitis (primary treatment) | Vancomycin, ceftazidime, amphotericin B |
| **Systemic** | Endophthalmitis (adjunct); orbital cellulitis; uveitis | Fluoroquinolones, antivirals, antifungals |

## 1. ANTIBACTERIAL AGENTS

### Classification of Topical Antibiotics

#### A. Fluoroquinolones

| Generation | Drugs | Spectrum | Clinical Use |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Second** | Ciprofloxacin, Ofloxacin | Broad (Gram+, Gram-) | Bacterial conjunctivitis, keratitis |
| **Third** | Levofloxacin | Enhanced Gram+ | Same; better penetration |
| **Fourth** | **Moxifloxacin, Gatifloxacin, Besifloxacin** | Broader Gram+, anaerobes, resistant organisms | **Drug of choice** for bacterial keratitis; surgical prophylaxis |

**Mechanism:** Inhibit DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV → bacterial DNA synthesis inhibition

**Moxifloxacin (Vigamox®) - 0.5%**
- Fourth-generation fluoroquinolone
- No preservative (used in many formulations)
- Excellent corneal penetration
- Broad spectrum: Gram+ (including resistant pneumococci), Gram-, atypical
- Dosing: Conjunctivitis: tid; Keratitis: frequent (q1h around clock)

**Gatifloxacin (Zymar®, Zymaxid®) - 0.3%, 0.5%**
- Similar to moxifloxacin
- Preserved with benzalkonium chloride

**Besifloxacin (Besivance®) - 0.6%**
- Fourth-generation, specifically for ophthalmic use
- Suspension; less systemic exposure
- Indicated for bacterial conjunctivitis only (not keratitis)

**Ciprofloxacin (Ciloxan®) - 0.3%**
- Second-generation
- Good Gram- coverage (Pseudomonas)
- Less Gram+ coverage
- **Adverse Effects:** White corneal precipitates with frequent dosing

#### B. Aminoglycosides

**Drugs:**
- **Tobramycin** (Tobrex®) - 0.3%
- **Gentamicin** - 0.3%
- **Amikacin** - (fortified preparations)

**Mechanism:** Bind 30S ribosome → inhibit protein synthesis

**Spectrum:**
- **Excellent Gram- coverage** (including Pseudomonas)
- **Limited Gram+ coverage** (some Staph, Strep)
- **No anaerobe coverage**

**Indications:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis
- Bacterial keratitis (especially Gram-)
- Often combined with cefazolin for fortified drops

**Adverse Effects:**
- **Corneal toxicity** (epithelial toxicity) with prolonged use
- Burning, stinging
- Conjunctival hyperemia
- Contact dermatitis (with neomycin more than tobramycin/gentamicin)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Tobramycin** preferred over gentamicin (less resistance, less toxic)
- **Fortified tobramycin** (1.4%) for severe keratitis
- Avoid prolonged use due to corneal toxicity

---

#### C. Macrolides

**Drugs:**
- **Erythromycin** - 0.5% ointment
- **Azithromycin** (AzaSite®) - 1% solution

**Mechanism:** Bind 50S ribosome → inhibit protein synthesis

**Spectrum:**
- **Gram+** (Staph, Strep)
- Some Gram-
- **Chlamydia trachomatis**
- Atypicals

**Indications:**
| Drug | Indications |
|:---|:---|
| **Erythromycin ointment** | **Neonatal conjunctivitis prophylaxis** (at birth); bacterial conjunctivitis; alternative in sulfa allergy; chlamydial conjunctivitis (with oral) |
| **Azithromycin** | Bacterial conjunctivitis; trachoma (topical adjunct); less frequent dosing (bid x 2 days, then daily) |

**Adverse Effects:**
- Burning, stinging
- Blurred vision (ointment)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- **Azithromycin gel**: Uses DuraSite® vehicle - increases contact time; bid for 2 days, then daily

---

#### D. Polypeptides

**Drugs:**
- **Bacitracin** - 500 U/g ointment
- **Polymyxin B** - 10,000 U/mL (often in combination)

**Mechanism:**
- **Bacitracin**: Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis
- **Polymyxin B**: Disrupts bacterial cell membrane (Gram-)

**Spectrum:**
| Drug | Spectrum |
|:---|:---|
| **Bacitracin** | Gram+ only |
| **Polymyxin B** | Gram- only |

**Indications:**
- Often in combination products (e.g., Polysporin® = bacitracin + polymyxin B)
- Bacterial conjunctivitis, blepharitis
- Prophylaxis

**Adverse Effects:**
- Minimal; contact dermatitis

---

#### E. Sulfonamides

**Drug:** **Sulfacetamide sodium** (Bleph-10®) - 10%, 30%

**Mechanism:** Competitive inhibitor of bacterial dihydropteroate synthase (folate synthesis)

**Spectrum:**
- Broad but **significant resistance** limits use

**Indications:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis (mild; cheap alternative)
- Adjunct in trachoma
- Inclusion conjunctivitis

**Adverse Effects:**
- Burning, stinging
- Allergic reactions (common; sulfa allergy cross-reactivity)
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare)

**Clinical Pearl:** Rarely used now due to high resistance rates

---

#### F. Chloramphenicol

**Drug:** Chloramphenicol - 0.5% drops, 1% ointment

**Mechanism:** Binds 50S ribosome → inhibits protein synthesis

**Spectrum:** Broad (Gram+, Gram-, atypicals, anaerobes)

**Indications:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis, keratitis
- **Widely used in some countries** (UK, Australia) but NOT in US

**Adverse Effects:**
- **Aplastic anemia** (rare, 1:50,000 to 1:100,000) - **systemic** from topical use (theoretical risk; very low)
- Bone marrow suppression
- Gray baby syndrome (systemic)

**Clinical Status:**
- **Not available in US** (FDA withdrew due to aplastic anemia risk)
- Used elsewhere due to efficacy and low cost
- Risk of aplastic anemia from topical is extremely low but present

**Pharmacokinetics:**
| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|

| **Dosing** | 1 drop q2h while awake (max 9 drops/day) for epithelial keratitis |
| **Duration** | 7-14 days |

**Adverse Effects:**
| Effect | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Punctate keratitis** | Most common; transient |
| **Conjunctival hyperemia** | - |

| **Burning/stinging** | - |
| **Corneal toxicity** | With prolonged use; reversible |

#### Ganciclovir (Zirgan®) 0.15% Gel

**Overview:**
- Acyclic nucleoside analog
- Available as ophthalmic gel

**Mechanism:**

**Indications:**
- **Herpes simplex keratitis (epithelial)**

**Dosing:** 5 times daily until healing, then tid x 7 days

**Advantages:**
- **Less toxic to cornea** than trifluridine
- **Gel formulation** - longer contact time
- Better tolerated (less burning)

**Adverse Effects:**
- Blurred vision (gel)
- Punctate keratitis
- Conjunctival hyperemia

**Clinical Pearl:** Increasingly used as first-line in many countries due to better tolerability

#### Acyclovir (Zovirax®)

**Forms:**
- **Oral:** 200 mg, 400 mg, 800 mg tablets; suspension
- **Topical:** 3% ophthalmic ointment (not available in US)
- **IV:** For severe disease

**Mechanism:**
- Phosphorylated by viral thymidine kinase to acyclovir monophosphate
- Cellular kinases → acyclovir triphosphate
- Inhibits viral DNA polymerase; chain termination

**Indications:**

| Indication | Regimen | Duration |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **HSV epithelial keratitis** | 400 mg PO 5x/day OR topical 3% ointment 5x/day | 7-14 days |
| **HSV stromal keratitis** | 400 mg PO 5x/day + topical steroids | 4-6 weeks taper |
| **HSV iridocyclitis** | 400 mg PO 5x/day + topical steroids/cycloplegics | 4-6 weeks |
| **Herpes zoster ophthalmicus** | 800 mg PO 5x/day (within 72h of rash) | 7-10 days |
| **Prophylaxis (recurrent HSV)** | 400 mg PO bid | Long-term |

**Pharmacokinetics:**
| Parameter | Oral | Topical | IV |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Bioavailability** | 15-30% (variable) | Minimal systemic | 100% |
| **Half-life** | 2.5-3 hours | - | 2.5-3 hours |
| **CSF penetration** | 50% of plasma | - | - |
| **Ocular penetration** | Good to anterior segment; poor to vitreous | Corneal only | Good to all tissues |

**Adverse Effects:**
| Route | Adverse Effects |
|:---|:---|
| **Oral** | Nausea, diarrhea, headache, rash; well-tolerated |
| **IV** | Renal impairment (crystalluria), CNS effects (confusion, hallucinations), phlebitis |
| **Topical** | Stinging, punctate keratitis |

**Contraindications:**
- Hypersensitivity
- Renal impairment (dose adjust for IV/oral)
- Dehydration (IV)

**Advantages:**
- Less frequent dosing than acyclovir

**Dosing:**

### Antiviral Therapy by Condition

#### Herpes Simplex Keratitis

| Type | Treatment | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Epithelial keratitis (dendritic ulcer)** | **Topical trifluridine OR ganciclovir OR oral acyclovir** + debridement | Antivirals essential; debridement optional |

- **Antivirals alone ineffective** (poor stromal penetration)
- **Steroids alone risk reactivation**

#### Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

**Definition:** Varicella zoster virus reactivation in ophthalmic division of trigeminal nerve

**Clinical Features:**
- Vesicular rash in dermatome (forehead, nose tip - Hutchinson's sign)
- Keratitis, uveitis, glaucoma, optic neuritis, post-herpetic neuralgia

**Treatment:**
| Treatment | Regimen | Rationale |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Oral antiviral** | Valacyclovir 1000 mg tid x 7-10 days OR Acyclovir 800 mg 5x/day x 7-10 days | Reduce acute pain, ocular complications, post-herpetic neuralgia |
| **Start within 72h** | Of rash onset | Maximum benefit |
| **Topical antibiotics** | For secondary bacterial infection of rash | - |
| **Topical antivirals** | Not routinely needed (systemic sufficient) | - |
| **Topical steroids** | For keratitis, uveitis | Treat inflammation |
| **Cycloplegics** | For uveitis | Comfort, prevent synechiae |
| **Analgesics** | For pain | - |
| **Vaccination** | Shingrix® (recombinant) | Prevention in elderly |

#### CMV Retinitis

**Overview:** Opportunistic infection in HIV/AIDS (CD4 <50), transplant recipients

**Treatment:**

| Drug | Route | Induction | Maintenance | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Ganciclovir** | IV | 5 mg/kg q12h x 14-21d | 5 mg/kg/day | First-line; myelosuppression |
| **Valganciclovir** | Oral | 900 mg bid x 21d | 900 mg/day | Prodrug; good oral option |
| **Foscarnet** | IV | 90 mg/kg q12h x 14-21d | 90-120 mg/kg/day | Nephrotoxic; electrolyte disturbances; for resistant cases |
| **Cidofovir** | IV | 5 mg/kg weekly x 2 | 5 mg/kg q2wk | Nephrotoxic; prehydrate with probenecid |
| **Fomivirsen** | Intravitreal | 330 mcg q2wk x 2 | 330 mcg q4wk | For resistant CMV; local only |
| **Ganciclovir implant** | Intraocular | Surgical implant q6-8mo | - | Local only; not used now |
| **HAART** | - | - | - | Restore immunity; most important |

#### Adenoviral Conjunctivitis

**Overview:** Most common viral conjunctivitis; highly contagious

**Treatment:**
- **No specific antiviral available**
- **Supportive care:** Artificial tears, cool compresses
- **Topical antibiotics:** For secondary bacterial infection (not routine)
- **Corticosteroids:** Controversial; may prolong viral shedding; use only for severe membranous conjunctivitis, subepithelial infiltrates

| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Formulation** | 5% suspension |

**Overview:**
- Second-generation triazole

**Mechanism:** Inhibits fungal cytochrome P450 (14α-demethylase) → ergosterol depletion

**Spectrum:**

| Step | Treatment |
|:---|:---|

## 1. CORTICOSTEROIDS

### Overview
Corticosteroids are the most commonly used immunomodulators in ophthalmology for their potent anti-inflammatory effects.

### Ophthalmic Corticosteroid Preparations

|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Prednisolone acetate** | 0.12%, 0.125%, 1% | Suspension | **High** | Uveitis, post-op inflammation, keratitis |
| **Prednisolone phosphate** | 0.125%, 1% | Solution | Moderate | Same; less penetration |
| **Dexamethasone** | 0.1% | Solution, suspension, ointment | **Very High** | Severe inflammation, post-op |
| **Fluorometholone** | 0.1%, 0.25% | Suspension | Low-moderate | Mild inflammation, long-term use (lower IOP risk) |
| **Loteprednol etabonate** | 0.2%, 0.5% | Suspension, gel | Moderate (site-activated) | Allergic conjunctivitis, post-op (lower IOP risk) |
| **Rimexolone** | 1% | Suspension | Moderate | Uveitis, post-op |

| **Fluocinolone implant** | 0.19 mg, 0.59 mg | Intravitreal implant (Iluvien®, Retisert®) | - | Chronic uveitis, DME |

---

### Mechanism of Action

**Genomic Effects (hours-days):**
1. Bind cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptors
2. Receptor complex translocates to nucleus
3. Bind glucocorticoid response elements (GRE) on DNA
4. Regulate gene transcription:

| Effect | Mediators |
|:---|:---|
| **↓ Pro-inflammatory cytokines** | IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ |
| **↑ Anti-inflammatory proteins** | Lipocortin (inhibits phospholipase A2 → ↓ prostaglandins, leukotrienes) |
| **↓ Inflammatory enzymes** | COX-2, iNOS |
| **↓ Adhesion molecules** | ICAM-1, E-selectin |

**Non-genomic Effects (minutes):**
- Rapid vasoconstriction
- Mast cell stabilization
- Decreased vascular permeability

### Adverse Effects of Ophthalmic Corticosteroids

| Effect | Details | Management |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Increased IOP (steroid responder)** | Occurs in 30-40% of population; dose and duration dependent; mechanism: ↑ outflow resistance in trabecular meshwork | Monitor IOP frequently; use lowest effective dose; switch to lower-risk steroid (fluorometholone, loteprednol); add glaucoma meds; stop if possible |
| **Cataract (posterior subcapsular)** | Dose and duration dependent; mechanism: steroid-induced lens opacification | Monitor; cataract surgery when visually significant |
| **Delayed wound healing** | Inhibits fibroblast proliferation, collagen synthesis | Avoid after corneal abrasion, surgery (relative) |
| **Increased infection risk** | Suppresses immune response; can reactivate latent infections (HSV, fungal) | Avoid in active epithelial HSV; use with antiviral in stromal HSV; monitor for superinfection |
| **Corneal thinning/perforation** | Inhibits collagen synthesis; increases collagenase activity | Avoid in corneal melting disorders; use cautiously in severe keratitis |
| **Systemic effects** | With prolonged high-dose topical, periocular, or intravitreal use | Rare; growth suppression in children, adrenal suppression |

---

### Steroid-Induced IOP Elevation

**Risk Factors:**
| Factor | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Genetics** | Family history of glaucoma, steroid response |
| **Age** | Children more sensitive; elderly also at risk |
| **Type of steroid** | Potency: Dexamethasone > prednisolone > loteprednol > fluorometholone |
| **Duration** | Longer use → higher risk |
| **Pre-existing glaucoma** | Higher risk |
| **Connective tissue disease** | Rheumatoid arthritis |

**Time Course:**
- Usually occurs within 2-6 weeks of therapy
- Can occur as early as 1 week or as late as months
- Resolution usually within 2-4 weeks of stopping

**Management:**
1. **Monitor IOP** before starting, 2-4 weeks after, then periodically
2. **Use lowest effective potency and duration**
3. **Consider switching** to loteprednol or fluorometholone
4. **Add IOP-lowering agents** if IOP elevated
5. **Stop steroid** if possible and IOP not controlled

---

### Contraindications to Ophthalmic Corticosteroids

| Contraindication | Reason |
|:---|:---|
| **Active epithelial HSV keratitis** | May worsen/reactivate (except when used with antiviral in stromal disease) |
| **Fungal keratitis** | May worsen |
| **Viral keratitis (active)** | May worsen |
| **Corneal thinning/melting** | May worsen/perforate |
| **Known steroid responder with uncontrolled IOP** | Relative; use with extreme caution and monitoring |

### Clinical Pearls for Ophthalmic Steroid Use

| Pearl | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **"Shake well" for suspensions** | Prednisolone acetate, fluorometholone are suspensions - must shake vigorously |
| **Monitor IOP** | Always check baseline IOP and follow up at 2-4 weeks |
| **Taper slowly** | Do not stop abruptly in chronic inflammation (rebound) |
| **Antiviral coverage** | In HSV stromal disease, always use oral antiviral with steroids |
| **Avoid in epithelial HSV** | Can cause worse infection |
| **Loteprednol for long-term** | Lower IOP risk for allergic conjunctivitis, post-op |
| **Intravitreal steroids** | Almost always cause cataract; high rate of IOP elevation |

## 2. CALCINEURIN INHIBITORS

### Cyclosporine A (Restasis®, Cequa®)

**Overview:**
- Calcineurin inhibitor
- First immunomodulator approved for dry eye disease

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Binds cyclophilin → inhibits calcineurin
- Prevents dephosphorylation of NF-AT → ↓ T-cell activation
- ↓ Inflammatory cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ) in conjunctiva and lacrimal gland
- Inhibits T-cell mediated inflammation in dry eye

**Ophthalmic Formulations:**

| Formulation | Concentration | Vehicle | Dosing | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Restasis®** | 0.05% | Emulsion | bid | Original formulation |
| **Cequa®** | 0.09% | Nanomicellar solution | bid | Higher concentration; better penetration? |
| **Generic** | 0.05% | Emulsion | bid | Available |

**Indications:**

| Indication | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Dry eye disease (moderate-severe)** | Increase tear production in patients with ocular inflammation |
| **Keratoconjunctivitis sicca** | Associated with Sjögren's syndrome |
| **Graft-versus-host disease** | Ocular manifestations |
| **Uveitis** | Off-label; steroid-sparing |
| **Allergic eye disease** | Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (off-label) |

**Pharmacokinetics:**
| Parameter | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Onset** | 4-12 weeks for effect |
| **Absorption** | Minimal systemic absorption |
| **Metabolism** | Hepatic (if absorbed) |

**Efficacy:**
- Increases tear production (Schirmer test)
- Decreases corneal fluorescein staining
- Improves symptoms (dryness, grittiness, burning)
- **Not immediate** - takes weeks-months

**Adverse Effects:**

| Effect | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Burning/stinging** | Most common (15-20%); usually transient |
| **Conjunctival hyperemia** | - |
| **Blurred vision** | Transient (emulsion) |
| **Eye pain** | - |
| **Foreign body sensation** | - |

**Contraindications:**
- Active ocular infection
- Hypersensitivity

**Clinical Pearls:**
| Pearl | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Onset delayed** | Inform patients it takes 1-3 months to work |
| **Burning common** | Often improves with continued use |
| **Remove contact lenses** | May absorb cyclosporine |
| **Can combine** | With artificial tears (separate by 15 min) |
| **Cequa®** | Higher concentration; possibly better efficacy; less burning? |
| **Steroid-sparing** | In uveitis, often used with steroid to reduce steroid burden |

### Tacrolimus

**Overview:**
- Macrolide immunomodulator
- More potent than cyclosporine

**Formulations:**
- **Topical:** 0.03% ointment (Protopic®) - used off-label ophthalmically
- **Systemic:** Oral, IV for severe uveitis

**Mechanism:** Binds FKBP-12 → inhibits calcineurin → ↓ T-cell activation

**Ophthalmic Indications (off-label):**
- **Vernal keratoconjunctivitis** (severe allergic eye disease)
- **Atopic keratoconjunctivitis**
- **Graft-versus-host disease**
- **Uveitis** (steroid-sparing)
- **Corneal graft rejection prevention**

**Adverse Effects (topical ocular):**
- Burning, stinging
- Conjunctival hyperemia
- Perioral dermatitis (with spillover)

**Advantages over Cyclosporine:**
- More potent
- Better penetration
- May work faster

## 4. ANTI-VEGF AGENTS

### Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF) Inhibitors

**Overview:**
- VEGF is key mediator of angiogenesis and vascular permeability
- Anti-VEGF therapy revolutionized treatment of retinal diseases

**Drugs:**

| Drug | Type | Structure | Indications | Dosing | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Ranibizumab (Lucentis®)** | Monoclonal antibody fragment | Anti-VEGF-A Fab | nAMD, DME, RVO, myopic CNV | Monthly - PRN | High affinity; designed for ocular use; expensive |
| **Bevacizumab (Avastin®)** | Full monoclonal antibody | Anti-VEGF-A full IgG | Same (off-label) | Monthly - PRN | **Much cheaper**; compounded by pharmacy; widely used |
| **Aflibercept (Eylea®)** | VEGF Trap | Fusion protein (VEGFR1+VEGFR2 domains + Fc) | Same | q2 months after loading | Longer duration; binds VEGF-A,B, PlGF |
| **Brolucizumab (Beovu®)** | Single-chain antibody fragment | 26 kDa fragment | nAMD | q3 months after loading | Longer duration; risk of retinal vasculitis |
| **Pegaptanib (Macugen®)** | Aptamer | Anti-VEGF-165 | nAMD (historical) | q6 weeks | Rarely used now |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Bind and neutralize VEGF
- ↓ Vascular permeability
- ↓ Angiogenesis (new blood vessel growth)
- Induce regression of abnormal vessels

**Indications:**
| Condition | VEGF Role | Evidence |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Neovascular (wet) AMD** | CNV leakage and growth | First-line; improves vision in 30-40% |
| **Diabetic macular edema (DME)** | Vascular permeability | First-line; superior to laser |
| **Retinal vein occlusion (RVO)** | Macular edema | First-line |
| **Myopic choroidal neovascularization** | CNV | First-line |
| **Proliferative diabetic retinopathy** | Neovascularization | Alternative to panretinal photocoagulation |
| **Neovascular glaucoma** | Iris neovascularization | Intravitreal + PRP |

**Administration:**
- **Intravitreal injection** (into vitreous cavity)
- Technique: Topical anesthetic, povidone-iodine prep, lid speculum, injection 3.5-4 mm from limbus
- Frequency: Monthly to q3-4 months depending on drug and regimen

**Regimens:**
| Regimen | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Monthly** | Fixed monthly injections (ranibizumab, bevacizumab) |
| **Treat-and-extend** | Inject, then extend interval if stable |
| **PRN (as needed)** | Monthly monitoring, inject if recurrence |
| **Fixed interval** | q2 months (aflibercept after loading) |

**Efficacy Outcomes:**
- Vision gain: Average 8-15 letters (ETDRS chart)
- Anatomic improvement: Reduced retinal thickness on OCT
- Better outcomes with earlier treatment

**Adverse Effects:**

| Injection-related | Ocular | Systemic |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Endophthalmitis (0.02-0.05%) | Intraocular inflammation | **Systemic VEGF inhibition (theoretical)** |
| Retinal detachment | Increased IOP (transient) | Stroke, MI, hypertension - **controversial** |
| Vitreous hemorrhage | Retinal pigment epithelial tear | Bleeding (epistaxis, GI) |
| Cataract (rare) | Subconjunctival hemorrhage | Proteinuria |
| - | Uveitis (brolucizumab) | - |
| - | Sterile inflammation | - |

**Contraindications:**
- Active ocular infection
- Recent stroke or MI (relative)
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Pregnancy (category C)

**Clinical Pearls:**

| Pearl | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Bevacizumab vs Ranibizumab** | Similar efficacy; bevacizumab much cheaper; major cost savings |
| **Aflibercept** | Longer duration; fewer injections; good for non-compliant patients |
| **Brolucizumab** | Longer duration; but risk of retinal vasculitis (1-3%) |
| **Endophthalmitis** | Sterile technique critical; risk per injection |
| **Anti-platelet therapy** | Can continue (risk of hemorrhage low vs. thrombosis risk off meds) |
| **Monitoring** | OCT essential for follow-up |
| **Loading dose** | 3 monthly injections initially |
| **Systemic safety** | Reassure patients: risk very low with ocular dosing |

## SECTION E: ANESTHETICS IN OPHTHALMIC PROCEDURES

### Topical Anesthetics

**Overview:**
- Used for diagnostic and minor therapeutic procedures
- **NEVER prescribed for home use** (corneal toxicity)

---

### Comparison of Topical Ophthalmic Anesthetics

| Drug | Concentration | Onset | Duration | Features |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Proparacaine (Alcaine®, Ophthetic®)** | 0.5% | 15-30 sec | 10-20 min | **Least irritating**; preferred for tonometry, contact lens fitting |
| **Tetracaine (Pontocaine®)** | 0.5% | 30-60 sec | 15-30 min | More potent; more stinging; longer duration |
| **Lidocaine** | 4% | 1-2 min | 15-30 min | For procedures; also gel 2% |
| **Benoxinate (Oxybuprocaine)** | 0.4% | 30 sec | 15 min | Often combined with fluorescein (Fluress®) |
| **Cocaine** | 1-4% | - | - | Historical; rarely used now; corneal toxicity |

---

### Proparacaine 0.5%

**Overview:**
- Ester local anesthetic
- Most commonly used for diagnostic procedures

**Advantages:**
- **Least irritating** - minimal stinging
- Rapid onset
- Good corneal anesthesia
- Does not affect pupil size

**Indications:**
| Procedure | Use |
|:---|:---|
| **Tonometry** | IOP measurement |
| **Gonioscopy** | Angle examination |
| **Contact lens fitting** | - |
| **Removal of corneal foreign body** | Minor procedure |
| **Lacrimal irrigation** | - |
| **Preoperative for minor procedures** | - |

**Adverse Effects:**
- Transient stinging (minimal)
- Conjunctival hyperemia
- Rare allergic reactions

---

### Tetracaine 0.5%

**Overview:**
- Ester local anesthetic
- More potent, longer duration

**Advantages:**
- Longer duration for procedures
- More profound anesthesia

**Disadvantages:**
- **More stinging** than proparacaine
- Greater corneal toxicity with repeated use

**Indications:**
- Removal of corneal foreign body
- Short procedures
- When longer anesthesia needed

---

### Lidocaine

**Forms:**
- **Topical:** 4% solution, 2% gel
- **Injectable:** 1-2% for blocks

**Uses:**
- **Topical:** For procedures, cataract surgery (intracameral preservative-free)
- **Gel:** For corneal anesthesia; longer contact time
- **Intracameral:** Preservative-free lidocaine 1% for cataract surgery (supplements topical anesthesia)

---

### Combination Anesthetics with Fluorescein

| Product | Components | Use |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Fluress®** | Benoxinate 0.4% + Fluorescein 0.25% | Tonometry, contact lens fitting |
| **Fluoracaine®** | Proparacaine 0.5% + Fluorescein 0.25% | Same |

**Advantages:** Single drop provides anesthesia + fluorescein staining for tonometry, foreign body detection

---

### Toxicity of Topical Anesthetics

**Mechanism of Toxicity:**
- Inhibit corneal epithelial cell migration
- Disrupt epithelial microfilaments
- Decrease corneal metabolism
- Suppress blink reflex

**Effects of Repeated/Prolonged Use:**
| Effect | Details |
|:---|:---|
| **Punctate keratitis** | Early sign |
| **Persistent epithelial defects** | - |
| **Corneal ulceration** | May become infected |
| **Corneal melting/perforation** | Severe, vision-threatening |
| **Delayed healing** | - |

**Clinical Pearl:**
**NEVER prescribe or give topical anesthetics for home use.** Patients with pain should be evaluated for underlying cause, not given anesthetics to use at home.

---

### Critical "NEVER" Rules

1. **NEVER** prescribe topical anesthetics for home use
2. **NEVER** use echothiophate in patients who may receive succinylcholine (prolonged apnea)
3. **NEVER** use steroids in active epithelial HSV keratitis (without antiviral)
4. **NEVER** use 10% phenylephrine in infants, elderly, or cardiovascular disease
5. **NEVER** give systemic CAIs to sulfa-allergic patients (anaphylaxis history)

| Drug Class | Monitor |
|:---|:---|
| **β-blockers** | Heart rate, blood pressure, asthma symptoms |
| **Prostaglandins** | Iris color change, eyelash growth, CME risk |
| **Systemic CAIs** | Electrolytes, CBC, renal function, metabolic acidosis |
| **Corticosteroids** | IOP at baseline and follow-up |
| **Brimonidine** | Dry mouth, fatigue; avoid in children <2 |
| **Phenylephrine** | Blood pressure in at-risk patients |
