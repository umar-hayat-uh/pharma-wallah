### **1. Introduction and Physiological Basis**

**Definition and Core Function**
Oxytocic drugs, also known as uterotonics, are medications that stimulate uterine contractions. Their primary clinical use is in obstetrics to induce or augment labor, control postpartum hemorrhage, and facilitate uterine involution after delivery.

**Physiological Basis: The Role of Oxytocin**
The prototype and physiological regulator is the neurohypophyseal hormone **oxytocin**. Its secretion from the posterior pituitary increases during labor in a positive feedback loop (Ferguson reflex) and during breastfeeding.
*   **Receptor Mechanism**: Oxytocin acts by binding to specific **G protein-coupled oxytocin receptors (OTR)** on uterine myometrial cells.
*   **Cellular Action**: Receptor activation triggers the **phosphoinositide pathway**, increasing intracellular calcium (Ca²⁺) concentrations. This calcium binds to calmodulin, activating myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK), which leads to myosin phosphorylation and ultimately, smooth muscle contraction.
*   **Receptor Sensitivity**: The number and sensitivity of OTRs in the uterus increase dramatically during pregnancy, especially near term, making the uterus exquisitely responsive.

### **2. Major Classes of Oxytocic Drugs**

Oxytocic agents are classified based on their chemical structure and primary receptor target.

#### **A. Posterior Pituitary Hormone: Oxytocin**
*   **Source**: Synthetic duplicate of the endogenous hormone.
*   **Primary Action**: Potent, selective stimulant of uterine smooth muscle contraction.
*   **Key Pharmacokinetic Feature**: Very short plasma half-life (**3-5 minutes**), necessitating continuous intravenous infusion for precise control.
*   **Clinical Uses**:
    1.  **Labor Induction/Augmentation**: For medical indications when continuation of pregnancy poses greater risk.
    2.  **Control of Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)**: First-line agent to promote uterine tone after delivery of the placenta.
    3.  **Postpartum Uterine Atony**: Management of inadequate uterine contraction after delivery.

#### **B. Ergot Alkaloids (e.g., Methylergonovine)**
*   **Source**: Derivatives of a fungus (*Claviceps purpurea*).
*   **Primary Action**: Potent, prolonged constrictor of vascular and uterine smooth muscle.
*   **Mechanism**: Acts as a partial agonist/antagonist at **alpha-adrenergic, serotonin (5-HT₂)**, and **dopamine receptors**. Its uterotonic effect is less specific than oxytocin's.
*   **Key Pharmacokinetic Feature**: Effective orally and intramuscularly, with a duration of action of **3-6 hours**.
*   **Clinical Use**: Primarily for **prevention and treatment of postpartum hemorrhage and uterine atony** after placental delivery. **Not used for labor induction** due to potential for sustained, tetanic contractions.

#### **C. Prostaglandins (PGF₂α and PGE analogs)**
*   **Source**: Synthetic analogs of endogenous eicosanoids.
*   **Primary Action**: Potent stimulants of uterine contraction at any gestational age; also promote cervical ripening (softening, dilation, effacement).
*   **Mechanism**: Bind to **prostaglandin receptors (FP, EP)**. They increase intracellular calcium and also lower the threshold for myometrial cell excitability.
*   **Key Feature**: Effective in early and late pregnancy, unlike oxytocin.
*   **Clinical Uses and Common Agents**:
    1.  **Carboprost Tromethamine (15-methyl PGF₂α)**: Used for **refractory postpartum hemorrhage** unresponsive to oxytocin and ergot alkaloids. Administered intramuscularly.
    2.  **Dinoprostone (PGE₂)**: Available as a vaginal insert, gel, or suppository. Used primarily for **cervical ripening and labor induction**.
    3.  **Misoprostol (PGE₁ analog)**: Used off-label for cervical ripening, labor induction, and management of postpartum hemorrhage (oral, sublingual, or rectal administration). It is inexpensive and stable at room temperature.

### **3. Pharmacology in Detail**

**Administration and Dosing**
| Drug | Common Routes | Typical Dosage/Regimen | Key Consideration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxytocin** | IV infusion, IM | **Induction**: Start 0.5-2 mU/min, double q30-60min. **PPH**: 10-40 units in 1L IV or 10 units IM. | Must use controlled IV infusion pump. Low dose, gradual titration. |
| **Methylergonovine** | IM, Oral | **PPH**: 0.2 mg IM after placental delivery, may repeat q2-4h. **Oral**: 0.2 mg tid/qid for 2-7 days postpartum. | Contraindicated in hypertension. Avoid IV (can cause severe hypertension). |
| **Carboprost** | IM | **Refractory PPH**: 0.25 mg IM, may repeat q15-90min (max 2 mg). | Contraindicated in asthma. Causes intense GI side effects. |
| **Misoprostol** | Oral, SL, PR, PV | **PPH**: 600-1000 mcg PR. **Induction/Cervical Ripening**: 25-50 mcg PV q4-6h. | Dosing varies significantly by indication and route. |

**Adverse Effects and Contraindications**
*   **Oxytocin**: **Water intoxication and hyponatremia** (due to antidiuretic hormone-like effect at high doses), uterine hyperstimulation, hypotension (transient with rapid IV bolus).
*   **Ergot Alkaloids**: **Severe hypertension** (especially in patients with preeclampsia), nausea, vomiting, coronary vasospasm. Contraindicated in hypertension, preeclampsia, and peripheral vascular disease.
*   **Prostaglandins**:
    *   **Carboprost**: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bronchospasm (contraindicated in asthma), hypertension.
    *   **Dinoprostone/Misoprostol**: Uterine hyperstimulation, fever, chills, diarrhea.

### **4. Clinical Comparison and Use**

The choice of oxytocic depends on the clinical scenario, timing in labor, and patient-specific factors.
| Clinical Scenario | First-Line Agent | Alternative/Second-Line Agents | Key Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Labor Induction/Augmentation** | **Oxytocin** (IV infusion) | Dinoprostone, Misoprostol (for cervical ripening first) | Used when cervix is favorable. Requires continuous fetal monitoring. |
| **Active Management of 3rd Stage (PPH prevention)** | **Oxytocin** (10 units IM/IV after delivery of baby) | — | Standard of care to reduce PPH risk. |
| **Treatment of Postpartum Hemorrhage** | **Oxytocin** (high-dose IV infusion) | **Methylergonovine** (IM), **Carboprost** (IM), **Misoprostol** (PR) | Use in stepwise protocol if bleeding continues. |
| **Postpartum Uterine Atony** | **Oxytocin** | Methylergonovine, Carboprost | Bimanual compression is a critical simultaneous intervention. |
| **Cervical Ripening (prior to induction)** | **Dinoprostone** (vaginal insert) | **Misoprostol** (vaginal) | Used when cervix is unfavorable (Bishop score <6). |

**Important Considerations**
*   **Uterine Hyperstimulation**: A risk with all agents, characterized by prolonged (>90 sec) or excessive (>5 in 10 min) contractions, often with fetal heart rate changes. Treatment includes stopping the infusion, lateral positioning, oxygen, and tocolytics (e.g., terbutaline).
*   **Tachysystole**: More than 5 contractions in 10 minutes without fetal heart rate changes.
*   **Stepwise Protocol for PPH**: Modern management follows a structured sequence: 1) Oxytocin, 2) Ergometrine/Carboprost, 3) Uterine tamponade, 4) Surgical intervention.
