# Reproductive Drugs

## **1. Contraceptives**

Contraceptives work by interfering with the normal hormonal cycle, ovulation, fertilization, or implantation. They are broadly classified as hormonal and non-hormonal methods.

**A. Hormonal Contraceptives**
These primarily prevent ovulation by suppressing the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, leading to inhibited follicular development and an absence of the LH surge. They also thicken cervical mucus and alter the endometrium.

| Class & Formulation | Primary Hormonal Components | Mechanism & Key Features | Common Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs)** | **Estrogen** (ethinyl estradiol) + **Progestin** (various) | 1. Suppress FSH/LH to inhibit ovulation. 2. Thicken cervical mucus. 3. Thin endometrium. Monophasic, biphasic, or triphasic dosing. | Levonorgestrel/EE, Norethindrone/EE, Drospirenone/EE |
| **Progestin-Only Pills (POPs / "Mini-pills")** | Progestin only (e.g., norethindrone, drospirenone) | Primarily thicken cervical mucus; may suppress ovulation in some cycles. Must be taken at the same time daily. | Norethindrone 0.35 mg, Drospirenone 4 mg |
| **Long-Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARCs)** | Progestin only or progestin + estrogen | Sustained release for long-term, user-independent efficacy. | **Implants** (etonogestrel rod), **IUDs** (levonorgestrel IUDs), **Injections** (DMPA) |
| **Non-Oral Routes** | Estrogen + Progestin or Progestin only | Transdermal, vaginal, or injectable delivery; same core mechanisms. | **Patch** (norelgestromin/EE), **Vaginal Ring** (etonogestrel/EE) |

**B. Non-Hormonal Contraceptives**
*   **Copper Intrauterine Device (IUD)**: Creates a local inflammatory reaction in the uterus that is toxic to sperm and ova, preventing fertilization. Also impairs implantation. Can provide emergency contraception if inserted within 5 days of unprotected intercourse.
*   **Barrier Methods**: Physically prevent sperm from reaching the egg (condoms, diaphragms, cervical caps). Also reduce risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
*   **Emergency Contraceptive Pills (ECPs)**:
    *   **Levonorgestrel (Plan B)**: A progestin-only high dose that inhibits or delays ovulation.
    *   **Ulipristal Acetate (Ella)**: A selective progesterone receptor modulator (SPRM) that blocks progesterone receptors, preventing ovulation even after the LH surge has begun (effective up to 5 days).

### **2. Fertility Drugs (Agents for Ovulation Induction)**

These drugs are used to treat female infertility, primarily by stimulating the development and release of mature oocytes.

**Pharmacological Classes and Agents**

| Drug Class | Mechanism of Action | Primary Use & Key Drugs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Clomiphene Citrate** | Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulator (SERM). Blocks estrogen feedback in the hypothalamus, increasing GnRH → FSH/LH → follicle growth. | **First-line** for anovulatory disorders (e.g., PCOS). Oral administration. |
| **Aromatase Inhibitors (e.g., Letrozole)** | Inhibit conversion of androgens to estrogens. Low estrogen increases FSH secretion. | **First-line for PCOS** (often preferred over clomiphene). Also used in breast cancer patients. |
| **Gonadotropins** | Directly stimulate ovaries. **FSH** (recruits follicles). **LH/hCG** (triggers final oocyte maturation & ovulation). | **Second-line/ART**. Used for controlled ovarian hyperstimulation in IVF/IUI. **Menotropins** (hMG: FSH+LH), **Follitropins** (recombinant FSH), **Chorionic Gonadotropin** (hCG, recombinant LH). |
| **Dopamine Agonists (e.g., Bromocriptine, Cabergoline)** | Suppress pathological prolactin secretion from pituitary tumors, restoring normal GnRH pulsatility. | **Hyperprolactinemia** causing anovulation. |

**Key Considerations in Fertility Therapy**
*   **Monitoring**: Essential via transvaginal ultrasound and serum estradiol to track follicular growth and prevent complications.
*   **Major Risk**: **Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS)**, a potentially life-threatening condition of ovarian enlargement and fluid shift into the abdomen/chest, most commonly triggered by hCG.
*   **Adjuvant Therapy**: **GnRH agonists/antagonists** are used in IVF cycles to prevent premature ovulation.

### **3. Testosterone and Contraception in Males**

**A. Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT)**
*   **Indications**: Treatment of **hypogonadism** (low testosterone with symptoms), not for physiological decline with aging alone.
*   **Mechanism**: Exogenous testosterone replacement to restore physiological levels.
*   **Preparations**:
    *   **Intramuscular Injections**: Testosterone cypionate/enanthate.
    *   **Transdermal**: Gels, patches, solutions.
    *   **Subcutaneous Pellets**: Long-acting (3-6 months).
    *   **Buccal/Intranasal**: Less common.
*   **Adverse Effects**: Acne, erythrocytosis (elevated hematocrit), gynecomastia, sleep apnea exacerbation, potential suppression of spermatogenesis, and cardiovascular risk concerns (debated).

**B. Male Hormonal Contraception**
*   **Principle**: Suppress spermatogenesis by turning off the HPG axis. Exogenous **testosterone** (or a potent androgen like **7α-methyl-19-nortestosterone / MENT**) suppresses pituitary LH and FSH secretion. The testosterone provides androgen replacement, while the lack of FSH halts sperm production.
*   **Current Status**: **Experimental**. While effective (>95% suppression of spermatogenesis), real-world development has faced hurdles related to perfect efficacy, reversibility, long-term safety, and market viability. No product is currently FDA-approved. Non-hormonal methods (e.g., targeting retinoic acid receptors) are also under investigation.

### **4. Pharmacotherapy for Erectile Dysfunction (ED)**

ED drugs primarily target the hemodynamics of penile erection, which depends on nitric oxide (NO)-mediated relaxation of cavernosal smooth muscle.

**Mechanism of Action (PDE5 Inhibitors)**
1.  Sexual stimulation releases NO in the corpus cavernosum.
2.  NO increases cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP).
3.  cGMP causes smooth muscle relaxation, allowing blood inflow.
4.  **Phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE5)** normally breaks down cGMP.
5.  **PDE5 inhibitors** block this enzyme, potentiating the NO/cGMP signal and sustaining erection.

**Common PDE5 Inhibitors**

| Drug | Onset | Duration | Key Characteristics & Dosing |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sildenafil (Viagra)** | 30-60 min | 4-6 hours | First approved PDE5i. **Take on empty stomach** (high-fat meal delays absorption). 25, 50, 100 mg doses. |
| **Tadalafil (Cialis)** | 30-120 min | Up to 36 hours | Longer duration allows for **daily low-dose (2.5, 5 mg)** or "on-demand" (10, 20 mg). Less affected by food. |
| **Vardenafil (Levitra)** | 30-60 min | 4-6 hours | Similar to sildenafil. Also available as an orally disintegrating tablet. |
| **Avanafil (Stendra)** | 15-30 min | ~6 hours | Faster onset, more selective for PDE5, potentially fewer side effects. |

**Important Contraindications & Adverse Effects**
*   **Absolute Contraindication**: Concurrent use of **nitrate drugs** (e.g., nitroglycerin, isosorbide) for angina. Combined use can cause severe, life-threatening hypotension.
*   **Relative Contraindications**: Unstable cardiovascular disease, hypotension, recent stroke/MI, severe hepatic/renal impairment, use with alpha-blockers (can cause hypotension).
*   **Common Side Effects**: Headache, flushing, dyspepsia, nasal congestion, dizziness, visual disturbances (mild, blue-tinged vision more common with sildenafil).
*   **Priapism**: A rare but serious medical emergency (erection >4 hours) requiring immediate treatment.

**Alternative Therapies**
*   **Alprostadil**: Prostaglandin E1 analog. Can be administered via **intracavernosal injection** (self-injection into penis) or as a **urethral suppository** (MUSE). Directly relaxes cavernosal smooth muscle.
*   **Testosterone Therapy**: Only effective for ED if the patient has documented hypogonadism.
