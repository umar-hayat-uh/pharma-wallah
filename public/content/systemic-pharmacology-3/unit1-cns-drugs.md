# **Comprehensive Guide to Drugs Acting on the Central Nervous System**

## **1. Hypnotics and Sedatives**

### **Introduction**
Hypnotics and sedatives are CNS depressants used to induce sleep (hypnotics) or reduce anxiety and promote calmness (sedatives). They primarily work by enhancing the inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid).

### **Mechanism of Action**
Most hypnotics and sedatives potentiate GABAergic inhibition by binding to specific sites on GABA-A receptors, increasing chloride ion influx into neurons, leading to hyperpolarization and reduced neuronal excitability.

### **Major Classes**

#### **A. Benzodiazepines**
**Examples:** Diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam, temazepam  
**Mechanism:** Bind to specific site on GABA-A receptor complex  
**Clinical Uses:** 
- Insomnia (short-term treatment)
- Anxiety disorders
- Preoperative sedation
- Alcohol withdrawal
- Muscle relaxation

**Pharmacokinetics:**
| Drug | Onset | Half-life (hours) | Active Metabolites |
|------|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Diazepam | Fast | 20-80 | Yes |
| Lorazepam | Intermediate | 10-20 | No |
| Temazepam | Slow | 8-20 | No |

**Adverse Effects:**
- Daytime sedation
- Cognitive impairment
- Ataxia
- Tolerance and dependence
- Rebound insomnia
- Respiratory depression (especially with alcohol)

#### **B. Non-Benzodiazepine "Z-Drugs"**
**Examples:** Zolpidem, zaleplon, eszopiclone  
**Mechanism:** Selective binding to α₁-subunit of GABA-A receptor  
**Advantages over benzodiazepines:**
- Less daytime sedation
- Lower risk of dependence
- Less disruption of sleep architecture

**Clinical Uses:** Primary insomnia

#### **C. Barbiturates**
**Examples:** Phenobarbital, secobarbital, pentobarbital  
**Mechanism:** Prolong GABA receptor channel opening  
**Current Use:** Largely replaced by safer alternatives; still used in:
- Refractory epilepsy (phenobarbital)
- Anesthesia induction
- Assisted suicide/euthanasia (where legal)

**Significant Risks:**
- Narrow therapeutic index
- Severe respiratory depression
- High abuse potential
- Dangerous drug interactions (CYP450 induction)

#### **D. Miscellaneous Agents**
**Melatonin Receptor Agonists:** Ramelteon, tasimelteon  
**Orexin Receptor Antagonists:** Suvorexant, lemborexant  
**Antihistamines:** Diphenhydramine, doxylamine (OTC sleep aids)

### **Special Considerations**
- **Tolerance:** Develops within days to weeks
- **Dependence:** Psychological and physical dependence can occur
- **Withdrawal:** Can cause rebound insomnia, anxiety, seizures
- **Elderly:** Increased sensitivity; higher risk of falls and cognitive impairment

## **2. Analgesics: Narcotic Analgesics and Opioid Antagonists**

### **Introduction**
Opioids are the most potent analgesics available, used for moderate to severe pain. They act primarily on opioid receptors in the CNS and peripheral nervous system.

### **Opioid Receptor Types**
| Receptor | Location | Effects |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **μ (mu)** | Brain, spinal cord | Analgesia, respiratory depression, euphoria, dependence |
| **κ (kappa)** | Brain, spinal cord | Analgesia, sedation, dysphoria |
| **δ (delta)** | Brain, peripheral | Analgesia, mood regulation |

### **Classification of Opioids**

#### **A. Natural Opioids**
**Examples:** Morphine, codeine  
**Source:** Opium poppy (Papaver somniferum)  
**Characteristics:**
- Morphine: Gold standard for severe pain
- Codeine: Weak opioid; prodrug converted to morphine by CYP2D6

#### **B. Semisynthetic Opioids**
**Examples:** Oxycodone, hydrocodone, hydromorphone, oxymorphone  
**Derived from:** Natural opioids with chemical modifications  
**Clinical Uses:**
- Moderate to severe pain
- Often combined with non-opioids (acetaminophen, NSAIDs)

#### **C. Synthetic Opioids**
**Examples:** 
- **Phenylpiperidines:** Fentanyl, sufentanil, remifentanil
- **Diphenylheptanes:** Methadone, propoxyphene
- **Benzomorphans:** Pentazocine
- **Miscellaneous:** Tramadol, tapentadol

**Fentanyl Characteristics:**
- 50-100 times more potent than morphine
- Transdermal patch for chronic pain
- High abuse potential

**Methadone Characteristics:**
- Long half-life (15-60 hours)
- Used in opioid addiction treatment
- NMDA receptor antagonism may help with neuropathic pain

### **Pharmacokinetics of Selected Opioids**
| Drug | Route | Onset | Duration | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|----------|-------|
| Morphine | IV | 5-10 min | 3-4 hr | Gold standard |
| Fentanyl | IV | 1-2 min | 30-60 min | Very potent |
| Methadone | PO | 30-60 min | 4-8 hr | Long half-life |
| Oxycodone | PO | 10-30 min | 3-6 hr | Often combined |

### **Adverse Effects**
1. **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, constipation, sedation
2. **Serious:** Respiratory depression, hypotension
3. **Chronic:** Tolerance, dependence, addiction
4. **Endocrine:** Hypogonadism, adrenal insufficiency

### **Opioid Antagonists**

#### **Naloxone**
- **Mechanism:** Competitive μ-receptor antagonist
- **Uses:** Opioid overdose reversal
- **Administration:** IV, IM, intranasal
- **Duration:** 30-90 minutes (shorter than most opioids)
- **Caution:** Can precipitate acute withdrawal

#### **Naltrexone**
- **Mechanism:** Long-acting opioid receptor antagonist
- **Uses:** Alcohol and opioid dependence
- **Administration:** Oral or monthly injection
- **Duration:** 24-72 hours

#### **Nalmefene**
- Longer duration than naloxone
- Used in alcohol dependence

### **Opioid Use Disorder Treatment**

#### **Maintenance Therapy**
1. **Methadone:** Daily supervised administration
2. **Buprenorphine:** Partial agonist; ceiling effect reduces abuse potential
3. **Buprenorphine/Naloxone:** Combination to prevent injection abuse

#### **Harm Reduction**
- Naloxone distribution programs
- Safe injection sites
- Needle exchange programs

## **3. Psychiatric Medications**

### **Anxiolytics**

#### **Benzodiazepines** (as previously discussed)
**First-line for:** Acute anxiety, panic attacks

#### **Non-Benzodiazepine Anxiolytics**
1. **Buspirone**
   - 5-HT₁A partial agonist
   - No sedation, dependence, or abuse potential
   - Delayed onset (2-4 weeks)
   - Uses: Generalized anxiety disorder

2. **Hydroxyzine**
   - Antihistamine with anxiolytic properties
   - Rapid onset
   - Uses: Preoperative anxiety, pruritus

3. **Beta-blockers** (Propranolol)
   - Control physical symptoms of anxiety
   - Performance anxiety, social phobia

### **Antipsychotics**

#### **Classification**
**First-generation (Typical) Antipsychotics**
- **Examples:** Haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazine
- **Mechanism:** Primarily D₂ dopamine receptor antagonists
- **Uses:** Positive symptoms of schizophrenia (hallucinations, delusions)
- **Side Effects:** Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS), tardive dyskinesia

**Second-generation (Atypical) Antipsychotics**
- **Examples:** Clozapine, risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine
- **Mechanism:** D₂ + 5-HT₂A antagonism
- **Advantages:** Reduced EPS, efficacy for negative symptoms
- **Side Effects:** Metabolic syndrome, weight gain, sedation

#### **Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS) Pyramid**
```
                    Tardive Dyskinesia
                 (months to years of treatment)
                           |
                 Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome
                   (rare, potentially fatal)
                           |
                  Dystonic Reactions
               (hours to days, painful spasms)
                           |
                     Akathisia
            (days to weeks, motor restlessness)
                           |
                  Parkinsonism
         (weeks, tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia)
```

#### **Clozapine Special Considerations**
- Most effective antipsychotic
- Requires weekly blood monitoring (agranulocytosis risk)
- Used in treatment-resistant schizophrenia
- Lower risk of EPS, higher risk of seizures, myocarditis

### **Antidepressants**

#### **Classes and Mechanisms**
| Class | Mechanism | Examples | Key Features |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------------|
| **SSRIs** | Selective serotonin reuptake inhibition | Fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram | First-line, well-tolerated |
| **SNRIs** | Serotonin & norepinephrine reuptake inhibition | Venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine | Also used for neuropathic pain |
| **TCAs** | Broad monoamine reuptake inhibition | Amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine | Anticholinergic side effects |
| **MAOIs** | Monoamine oxidase inhibition | Phenelzine, tranylcypromine | Dietary restrictions (tyramine) |
| **Atypicals** | Various mechanisms | Bupropion, mirtazapine, trazodone | Unique side effect profiles |

#### **Clinical Considerations**
1. **Therapeutic Lag:** 2-4 weeks for full effect
2. **Discontinuation Syndrome:** Especially with short half-life agents
3. **Suicide Risk:** May increase in adolescents initially
4. **Switching Strategies:** Required when changing between classes

### **Mood Stabilizers (Antimanic Agents)**

#### **Lithium**
- **Mechanism:** Multiple; affects inositol metabolism, GSK-3 inhibition
- **Uses:** Bipolar disorder (acute mania and maintenance)
- **Therapeutic Range:** 0.6-1.2 mEq/L
- **Monitoring:** Serum levels, thyroid, renal function
- **Side Effects:** Polyuria, polydipsia, tremor, weight gain
- **Toxicity:** >1.5 mEq/L (GI symptoms, tremor, confusion, seizures)

#### **Anticonvulsants as Mood Stabilizers**
1. **Valproate**
   - First-line for acute mania
   - Monitoring: LFTs, CBC, serum levels
   - Teratogenic (neural tube defects)

2. **Carbamazepine**
   - Effective for acute mania
   - Autoinduction of metabolism
   - Monitoring: CBC (aplastic anemia risk)

3. **Lamotrigine**
   - Maintenance in bipolar depression
   - Slow titration (Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk)

#### **Atypical Antipsychotics in Bipolar Disorder**
- Olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone, aripiprazole
- Used for acute mania and maintenance
- Often combined with traditional mood stabilizers

## **4. Cerebral Stimulants and Spinal Cord Stimulants**

### **Cerebral Stimulants**

#### **A. Psychostimulants**
**Mechanism:** Increase dopamine and norepinephrine signaling

| Drug | Mechanism | Uses | Key Points |
|------|-----------|------|------------|
| **Methylphenidate** | DA/NE reuptake inhibitor | ADHD, narcolepsy | Multiple formulations (IR, SR, ER) |
| **Amphetamines** | DA/NE release + reuptake inhibition | ADHD, narcolepsy, obesity | Higher abuse potential |
| **Modafinil/Armodafinil** | Unknown; orexin activation | Narcolepsy, shift work disorder | Lower abuse potential |
| **Caffeine** | Adenosine antagonism | Fatigue, headache adjunct | Most widely used stimulant |

#### **B. ADHD Treatment Strategy**
```
First-line: Methylphenidate or Amphetamine formulations
            ↓
Alternatives: Atomoxetine (non-stimulant)
            ↓
Adjunctive: Alpha-2 agonists (guanfacine, clonidine)
```

#### **C. Side Effects of Stimulants**
- Insomnia
- Anorexia, weight loss
- Increased heart rate and BP
- Growth suppression in children
- Psychosis (at high doses)
- Dependence and abuse

### **Spinal Cord Stimulants (Muscle Relaxants)**

#### **A. Centrally Acting Muscle Relaxants**
1. **Baclofen**
   - GABA-B agonist
   - Uses: Spasticity (MS, spinal cord injury)
   - Administration: Oral or intrathecal pump
   - Withdrawal: Can be life-threatening

2. **Tizanidine**
   - α₂-adrenergic agonist
   - Uses: Spasticity
   - Side Effects: Sedation, hypotension, hepatotoxicity

3. **Benzodiazepines** (Diazepam)
   - GABA-A potentiation
   - Uses: Spasticity, muscle spasms

#### **B. Direct-Acting Muscle Relaxants**
1. **Dantrolene**
   - Inhibits Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum
   - Uses: Malignant hyperthermia, spasticity
   - Side Effects: Hepatotoxicity, muscle weakness

2. **Botulinum Toxin**
   - Prevents ACh release at neuromuscular junction
   - Uses: Focal dystonias, spasticity, cosmetics
   - Duration: 3-6 months

## **5. Drug Treatment of Epilepsy**

### **Classification of Seizures**
```
Epileptic Seizures
├── Focal (Partial)
│   ├── Without impaired consciousness
│   └── With impaired consciousness
└── Generalized
    ├── Absence
    ├── Tonic-clonic
    ├── Myoclonic
    └── Atonic
```

### **Antiepileptic Drugs by Mechanism**

#### **A. Sodium Channel Blockers**
| Drug | Spectrum | Key Characteristics |
|------|----------|---------------------|
| **Phenytoin** | Focal, tonic-clonic | Zero-order kinetics, gingival hyperplasia |
| **Carbamazepine** | Focal, tonic-clonic | Autoinduction, hyponatremia |
| **Lamotrigine** | Broad spectrum | Slow titration, SJS risk |
| **Oxcarbazepine** | Focal, tonic-clonic | Fewer drug interactions than carbamazepine |

#### **B. GABA Enhancers**
| Drug | Mechanism | Spectrum |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Valproate** | Multiple mechanisms | Broad spectrum |
| **Benzodiazepines** | GABA-A potentiation | Acute seizures, status epilepticus |
| **Barbiturates** | Prolong GABA channel opening | Refractory epilepsy |
| **Vigabatrin** | GABA transaminase inhibitor | Infantile spasms |

#### **C. Calcium Channel Blockers**
- **Ethosuximide:** T-type calcium channel blocker; first-line for absence seizures

#### **D. Synaptic Vesicle Protein Binders**
- **Levetiracetam:** Binds SV2A protein; broad spectrum, well-tolerated

#### **E. Glutamate Antagonists**
- **Topiramate:** Multiple mechanisms; also used for migraine prophylaxis

### **Treatment Guidelines by Seizure Type**

| Seizure Type | First-line | Alternatives |
|--------------|------------|--------------|
| **Focal** | Lamotrigine, levetiracetam | Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine |
| **Generalized Tonic-Clonic** | Valproate, lamotrigine | Levetiracetam, topiramate |
| **Absence** | Ethosuximide, valproate | Lamotrigine |
| **Myoclonic** | Valproate, levetiracetam | Topiramate, zonisamide |

### **Special Considerations**
1. **Women of Childbearing Age:**
   - Avoid valproate (teratogenic)
   - Folic acid supplementation
   - Consider lamotrigine, levetiracetam

2. **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:**
   - Essential for phenytoin (nonlinear kinetics)
   - Helpful for carbamazepine, valproate

3. **Withdrawal:**
   - Gradual taper (weeks to months)
   - Higher relapse risk if rapid discontinuation

4. **Status Epilepticus Treatment:**
   - **Step 1:** Benzodiazepines (lorazepam IV)
   - **Step 2:** Fosphenytoin/phenytoin, valproate, levetiracetam
   - **Step 3:** Anesthetics (propofol, midazolam)

## **6. Drug Treatment of Parkinsonism and Other Movement Disorders**

### **Parkinson's Disease Pathophysiology**
**Dopamine Deficiency** in substantia nigra → basal ganglia dysfunction
**ACh Relative Excess** → motor symptoms

### **Pharmacotherapy Strategies**

#### **A. Dopamine Replacement**
1. **Levodopa/Carbidopa**
   - Gold standard for symptomatic treatment
   - Carbidopa inhibits peripheral decarboxylation
   - **Motor Complications:**
     - Wearing-off phenomenon
     - On-off fluctuations
     - Dyskinesias

2. **Controlled-Release Formulations**
   - Provide more stable dopamine levels
   - May reduce motor fluctuations

#### **B. Dopamine Agonists**
| Drug | Receptor Specificity | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------|---------------------|------------|---------------|
| **Pramipexole** | D₂, D₃ | Neuroprotective? | Impulse control disorders |
| **Ropinirole** | D₂, D₃ | Once-daily formulation | Similar to pramipexole |
| **Rotigotine** | Broad spectrum | Transdermal patch | Skin reactions |

#### **C. MAO-B Inhibitors**
- **Selegiline, Rasagiline**
- Neuroprotective potential
- Mild symptomatic benefit
- Can be used with levodopa

#### **D. COMT Inhibitors**
- **Entacapone, Tolcapone**
- Prolong levodopa effect
- Tolcapone requires LFT monitoring

#### **E. Anticholinergics**
- **Trihexyphenidyl, Benztropine**
- Help tremor and rigidity
- Side effects: Cognitive impairment, dry mouth, urinary retention

### **Treatment Progression**
```
Early Disease → MAO-B inhibitor or Dopamine agonist
                    ↓
Moderate Disease → Add levodopa
                    ↓
Advanced Disease → Add COMT inhibitor, consider infusion therapies
                    ↓
Motor Complications → Adjust timing, consider DBS, continuous infusion
```

### **Other Movement Disorders**

#### **A. Essential Tremor**
- **First-line:** Propranolol, primidone
- **Alternatives:** Topiramate, gabapentin
- **Refractory:** Botulinum toxin, DBS

#### **B. Dystonias**
- **Focal:** Botulinum toxin
- **Generalized:** Anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, baclofen

#### **C. Huntington's Disease**
- **Chorea:** Tetrabenazine, deutetrabenazine
- **Psychiatric symptoms:** Atypical antipsychotics, antidepressants

#### **D. Restless Legs Syndrome**
- **First-line:** Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole)
- **Alternatives:** Gabapentin enacarbil, opioids

## **7. General and Local Anesthetics**

### **General Anesthetics**

#### **A. Stages of Anesthesia**
1. **Stage I:** Analgesia (conscious)
2. **Stage II:** Excitement (delirium, irregular breathing)
3. **Stage III:** Surgical anesthesia
4. **Stage IV:** Medullary depression (overdose)

#### **B. Inhalation Anesthetics**
**Mechanism:** Enhance GABA, inhibit glutamate receptors

| Agent | MAC (%) | Blood:Gas | Key Properties |
|-------|---------|-----------|----------------|
| **Sevoflurane** | 2.0 | 0.65 | Rapid onset/offset, airway friendly |
| **Desflurane** | 6.0 | 0.42 | Very rapid offset, airway irritant |
| **Isoflurane** | 1.2 | 1.4 | Coronary steal phenomenon |
| **Nitrous Oxide** | 105 | 0.47 | Weak anesthetic, good analgesic |

**MAC (Minimum Alveolar Concentration):** Concentration producing immobility in 50% of patients

#### **C. Intravenous Anesthetics**
1. **Propofol**
   - GABA-A potentiation
   - Rapid onset/offset
   - Antiemetic properties
   - **Propofol Infusion Syndrome:** Rare but fatal (metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis)

2. **Barbiturates** (Thiopental, Methohexital)
   - GABA-A potentiation
   - Rapid induction
   - Cardiovascular depression

3. **Benzodiazepines** (Midazolam)
   - Anxiolysis, amnesia
   - Reversible with flumazenil

4. **Ketamine**
   - NMDA receptor antagonist
   - Produces dissociative anesthesia
   - Bronchodilation, analgesia
   - Emergence reactions

5. **Etomidate**
   - Minimal cardiovascular effects
   - Adrenal suppression

6. **Dexmedetomidine**
   - α₂-adrenergic agonist
   - Sedation with minimal respiratory depression
   - Used in ICU sedation

### **Local Anesthetics**

#### **A. Mechanism of Action**
Block voltage-gated sodium channels → prevent nerve conduction

#### **B. Classification**
**Esters:** Procaine, chloroprocaine, tetracaine, cocaine
**Amides:** Lidocaine, bupivacaine, ropivacaine, mepivacaine

#### **C. Pharmacokinetic Properties**
| Drug | Onset | Duration | Max Dose (mg) | Special Characteristics |
|------|-------|----------|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Lidocaine** | Fast | Medium | 300-500 | Versatile, multiple formulations |
| **Bupivacaine** | Slow | Long | 150-200 | Cardiotoxic if intravascular |
| **Ropivacaine** | Medium | Long | 200-250 | Less cardiotoxic than bupivacaine |
| **Mepivacaine** | Fast | Medium | 300-400 | Less vasodilation |

#### **D. Clinical Uses by Duration**
- **Short:** Procaine, chloroprocaine (30-60 minutes)
- **Intermediate:** Lidocaine, mepivacaine (60-180 minutes)
- **Long:** Bupivacaine, ropivacaine, tetracaine (4-12 hours)

#### **E. Techniques of Administration**
1. **Topical:** Creams, gels, patches
2. **Infiltration:** Direct tissue injection
3. **Nerve Blocks:** Peripheral or plexus blocks
4. **Spinal:** Subarachnoid injection
5. **Epidural:** Epidural space injection
6. **Intravenous Regional (Bier Block):** Limb anesthesia

#### **F. Systemic Toxicity**
**CNS Symptoms:**
1. Perioral numbness, tinnitus
2. Restlessness, tremor
3. Seizures
4. Coma, respiratory arrest

**Cardiovascular Symptoms:**
1. Myocardial depression
2. Bradycardia
3. Ventricular arrhythmias (bupivacaine)

**Treatment of Toxicity:**
1. Stop injection
2. Airway management, oxygen
3. Seizure control (benzodiazepines)
4. Lipid emulsion therapy (Intralipid 20%)
5. ACLS protocol (avoid vasopressin, calcium channel blockers)

#### **G. Adjuvants to Local Anesthetics**
1. **Epinephrine:** Vasoconstriction → prolonged duration, reduced systemic absorption
2. **Clonidine:** Prolongs block, analgesic properties
3. **Bicarbonate:** Increases pH → faster onset
4. **Dexamethasone:** Prolongs block duration
